1. Introduction: Your Lungs' Home Base

Imagine your chest as a cozy, closed room. Inside this room are your lungs!

  • The chest cavityThe space inside your ribs, diaphragm, and backbones. It's like a protective box for your lungs and heart. is like a protective box.
  • Your lungs are wrapped in thin membranes called pleuraeShiny, slippery membranes that surround your lungs, helping them move smoothly when you breathe..
  • Fun Fact: Your right lung has 3 parts (lobes), but your left lung only has 2 (to make space for your heart!).
  • Your heart hangs out on the mid-left side, not attached to the lungs. It just chills in the same room!

Focus Prompt!

Understanding this basic setup helps you see how breathing works and what happens when things go wrong!

Recap: Intro Key Points

  • Chest cavity = lung's "room."
  • Pleurae = lung wrappers.
  • Right lung = 3 lobes; Left lung = 2 lobes.

2. Lung Compliance: How Stretchy Are Your Lungs?

Think of Lung complianceHow easily your lungs can stretch and expand when you breathe in. Think of a balloon: easy to inflate = high compliance. Stiff balloon = low compliance. as how "stretchy" your lungs are. It's about how much air they can hold based on the pressure in the tiny air sacs (alveoli).

Two Main Factors:

  1. Connective Tissue: Things like collagen and elastin (they're like tiny rubber bands in your lungs!).
  2. Surface Tension in Alveoli: This is controlled by surfactantA special soap-like substance in your lungs that reduces surface tension, making it easier for the tiny air sacs (alveoli) to stay open..
    • Surfactant is like a non-stick coating for your alveoli. It lowers surface tension and stops fluid from getting in.

Compliance in Diseases:

  • Increased Lung Compliance: Happens with COPD (think of an old, over-stretched rubber band).
  • Decreased Lung Compliance: Occurs with Restrictive Pulmonary Disease (lungs become "stiff" – like trying to inflate a very stiff new balloon).
    • This means less lung volume and it takes a *lot* more effort to breathe.

Recap: Lung Compliance

  • Compliance = lung stretchiness.
  • Affected by connective tissue & surfactant.
  • COPD = floppy lungs (high compliance).
  • Restrictive disease = stiff lungs (low compliance).

3. Control of Respiration: Who's the Boss of Your Breathing?

Your body constantly checks how much oxygen (O2), carbon dioxide (CO2), and hydrogen (H+) are in your blood. Special sensors called chemoreceptorsSpecial sensors in your body that detect changes in blood gases (oxygen, carbon dioxide) and pH, sending signals to your brain to adjust your breathing. tell your brain when to adjust your breathing.

Central Chemoreceptors (Brain Buddies):

  • Where: Deep in your brain (medulla) and spinal fluid.
  • What they react to: TOO MUCH CO2 and LOW pH (blood becoming acidic).
  • Their job: They tell you to breathe more to get rid of CO2!
  • ⚠️ Important ADHD Warning! If CO2 stays high for a long time (like in severe COPD), these sensors get "tired" and stop responding. This means the main trigger to breathe shifts...

Peripheral Chemoreceptors (Bodyguards):

  • Where: In your carotid arteries (neck) and aorta (big artery from heart).
  • What they react to: LOW OXYGEN (PO2 < 60 mm Hg).
  • Their job: If central chemoreceptors are "tired" (from chronic high CO2), these guys take over and become the main signal for breathing.
  • 🚨 NCLEX Alert! If you give too much oxygen to a COPD patient who relies on this "hypoxic drive," you might turn off their breathing signal! Keep oxygen low and steady.

Recap: Breathing Control

  • Breathing is controlled by O2, CO2, H+ levels.
  • Central chemoreceptors (brain) react to high CO2/low pH.
  • Peripheral chemoreceptors (body) react to low O2.
  • Crucial: Chronic high CO2 can shift COPD patients' breathing drive to low O2!

4. Bronchial Smooth Muscle: Airway Squeeze or Relax?

Your airways (trachea and bronchi) are wrapped in smooth muscleMuscles that control the size of your airways, blood vessels, and other internal organs. They're not controlled voluntarily like your arm muscles.. When these muscles tighten, they squeeze your airways, making it harder to breathe. When they relax, your airways open up!

Visualize It!

FIG. 39.1: Bronchial smooth muscle fibers become more closely spaced as they near the alveoli. (Imagine a diagram here!)

Who Controls the Squeeze?

  • Parasympathetic Nervous System (Relax/Rest & Digest):
    • Releases acetylcholine.
    • Causes BRONCHOCONSTRICTION (airways squeeze!).
  • Sympathetic Nervous System (Fight or Flight!):
    • Releases epinephrine.
    • Stimulates beta2 receptors in airway muscles.
    • Causes BRONCHODILATION (airways open wide!).

These two systems are like a tug-of-war, trying to keep your airways just right (homeostasis).

Meet cAMP: The Airway Opener!

  • Cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP)A crucial messenger molecule inside your cells that helps relax bronchial smooth muscles, leading to bronchodilation (opening of airways). (cAMP) is a special molecule inside your bronchial cells.
  • More cAMP = More Bronchodilation! (Airways open!)
  • There's a sneaky enzyme called phosphodiesteraseAn enzyme in your lungs that breaks down cAMP (the molecule that helps open airways). Drugs like theophylline stop phosphodiesterase, letting cAMP do its job. that can *inactivate* cAMP. Not good for open airways!
  • Drug Superpowers: Drugs like theophylline (a methylxanthine) block phosphodiesterase, so cAMP can do its job and keep those airways open!

Recap: Airway Control

  • Tight muscles = constricted airways.
  • Parasympathetic (acetylcholine) = constrict.
  • Sympathetic (epinephrine, beta2) = dilate.
  • cAMP = opens airways.
  • Phosphodiesterase = tries to close airways (theophylline fights it!).

5. Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD): The Big Picture

COPD is a big umbrella term for lung diseases that block airflow and make breathing hard. Think of it as a constant fight for air.

COPD (Airflow Obstruction):

  • Problem: Air gets trapped or has trouble flowing OUT of the lungs.
  • Caused by:
    1. Chronic Bronchitis
    2. Bronchiectasis
    3. Emphysema
    4. Asthma
  • Key Feature: Chronic bronchitis, bronchiectasis, and emphysema often cause PERMANENT lung damage. Asthma can be reversible, but long-term asthma can also cause irreversible changes.
  • Measurement: Doctors often see a reduced FEV1Forced Expiratory Volume in 1 second: How much air you can blow out in one second. It's a key measurement for lung function. (Forced Expiratory Volume in 1 second) in lung tests.

Restrictive Lung Disease (Lung Stiffness/Fluid):

  • Problem: Lungs can't expand fully; total lung capacity is decreased.
  • Caused by: Fluid build-up (pulmonary edema), stiff scarring (pulmonary fibrosis), lung tumors, or chest wall problems (like severe scoliosis).

Digging Deeper: The COPD Crew

Overlapping Symptoms!

FIG. 39.2: Overlapping signs and symptoms of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) conditions. (Imagine a Venn diagram here!)

  • Chronic Bronchitis:
    • Cause: Often smoking or long-term lung infections.
    • What happens: Airways get inflamed, lots of mucus is produced.
    • Sounds like: Productive cough, rattling sounds (rhonchi) when breathing.
    • Danger: Can lead to too much CO2 (hypercapnia) and not enough O2 (hypoxemia), causing respiratory acidosis.
  • Bronchiectasis:
    • What happens: Airways become abnormally dilated (stretched out) due to repeated infections/inflammation. Leads to damage and scarring.
  • Emphysema:
    • Cause: Mostly smoking, pollution, or a lack of a protective protein (alpha1-antitrypsin).
    • What happens: Tiny air sacs (alveoli) get destroyed and merge into bigger, less efficient sacs. Air gets trapped inside these over-expanded sacs, making gas exchange (O2 in, CO2 out) very poor.

Quick Fact!

Cigarette smoking is the #1 risk factor for COPD. Quitting smoking is the best way to prevent and slow down this disease!

Medications for COPD: Your Arsenal!

We use different types of drugs to help manage COPD symptoms:

  • Bronchodilators: Your "airway openers"!
    • Sympathomimetics (adrenergics)
    • Anticholinergic drugs (like ipratropium bromide)
    • Methylxanthines (like theophylline)
  • Glucocorticoids (Steroids): Your "inflammation fighters"! They calm down irritation in the lungs.
  • Leukotriene Modifiers & Cromolyn: More inflammation fighters! They prevent swelling and mucus.
  • Expectorants: Help loosen up that sticky mucus so you can cough it out.
  • Antibiotics: Only if a bacterial infection pops up (like with excess mucus).

Recap: COPD Essentials

  • COPD = blocked airflow. Restrictive = stiff lungs.
  • Types of COPD: Chronic Bronchitis, Bronchiectasis, Emphysema, Asthma.
  • Smoking is a major cause.
  • Drugs: Bronchodilators (open), Steroids (reduce inflammation), others.

6. Bronchial Asthma: The Inflammatory Attack!

Asthma is a type of COPD where your airways get inflamed and tighten up (bronchospasm) in response to triggers. This leads to wheezing, coughing, and feeling breathless.

Common Triggers (Watch out for these!):

  • Stress, emotions
  • Allergens (pet dander, dust mites, food)
  • Pollutants (smoke, fumes, perfume)
  • Changes in humidity, air pressure, temperature
  • Exercise
  • Certain drugs (e.g., aspirin, ibuprofen, beta-blockers)

Sometimes, asthma is called Reactive Airway Disease (RAD) when it's super sensitive to things like allergens and temperature changes.

Pathophysiology: The Mast Cell Mayhem!

Imagine tiny "alarm cells" in your body called mast cellsImmune cells found in connective tissues that play a key role in allergic reactions by releasing chemicals like histamine.. When an allergen (like pollen) shows up, it attaches to these mast cells in your lungs. This sets off a chain reaction!

Mast cells release powerful chemical "messengers" called mediators:

  • Histamines: Strong airway constrictors!
  • Cytokines, Serotonin, ECF-A: More constrictors!
  • Leukotrienes: Cause inflammation, mucus, and constriction. (More on these drugs later!)

Did you know?

If you have high eosinophil counts in your blood, it often means an allergic reaction is happening!

These mediators make your airways:

  • Constrict! (Bronchoconstriction)
  • Produce lots of mucus!
  • Get inflamed and swollen!

Pathophysiology Flowchart!

FIG. 39.3: Factors that contribute to bronchoconstriction. (Imagine a flowchart showing how triggers lead to mast cell release, then mediators, then constriction!)

Remember cAMP? (Cyclic AMP, the airway opener). Histamine and leukotrienes try to *inhibit* cAMP, which leads to bronchoconstriction. Good news: Bronchodilators and methylxanthines work by increasing cAMP to keep airways open!

Focus Prompt!

For an acute asthma attack, the **first line of defense are short-acting sympathomimetics** (beta2-adrenergic agonists). They quickly boost cAMP and open airways. Long-acting ones are for daily maintenance.

Recap: Asthma

  • Asthma = inflammation + bronchospasm from triggers.
  • Mast cells release histamine, leukotrienes, etc.
  • These chemicals cause constriction, mucus, swelling.
  • Short-acting beta2 agonists are key for acute attacks.

7. Sympathomimetics: Your "Fight-or-Flight" Airway Openers!

These drugs mimic your body's "fight-or-flight" system. They increase cAMP, which makes your airways relax and dilate (open up!).

Epinephrine (The Emergency Big Gun!):

  • What it is: A nonselective sympathomimetic (hits alpha1, beta1, and beta2 receptors).
  • When used: Emergency situations! Like severe bronchospasm from anaphylaxis (a serious allergic reaction).
  • How it helps: Quickly opens airways (bronchodilation) and boosts blood pressure.
  • Route: Given subcutaneously (under the skin).

Selective Beta2-Adrenergic Agonists (Your Asthma Go-To!):

  • What they are: Drugs that primarily target the beta2 receptors in your lungs.
  • When used: For chronic asthma or COPD bronchospasm.
  • How they help: Cause bronchodilation.
  • Why they're better than Epinephrine for daily use: Fewer side effects because they are more specific to the lungs (don't hit beta1 receptors as much, which affect the heart).
  • Route: Often given by aerosol inhaler or tablet.

Recap: Sympathomimetics

  • Increase cAMP, causing bronchodilation.
  • Epinephrine: Emergency, non-selective.
  • Beta2-agonists: Daily use, lung-specific (fewer heart side effects).

8. Albuterol: The Quick Rescue

AlbuterolA commonly used, selective beta2-adrenergic agonist that quickly relaxes bronchial smooth muscle, causing bronchodilation. It's often called a "rescue inhaler." is a super effective, selective beta2 drug. It's often called your "rescue inhaler" because it works fast to open airways for asthma and bronchospasm.

Focus Prompt!

Newer beta-adrenergic drugs are more selective for beta2 receptors, meaning fewer heart-related side effects. But high doses can still cause some beta1 effects (like shaky hands or fast pulse!).

  • Fast-acting rescue for asthma/COPD.
  • Side effects: tremor, fast heart rate (especially high doses).
  • Watch blood glucose and potassium!

9. Metaproterenol: Another Beta-Agonist

MetaproterenolA beta-adrenergic agent used for asthma and COPD, with primary beta2 effects (bronchodilation) but also some beta1 (cardiac) effects. Can be taken orally or by inhalation. is another beta-adrenergic agent. It mostly works on beta2 receptors, but can have some beta1 effects (so be a bit more cautious with heart patients than with pure beta2 drugs).

  • Routes: Oral, MDI (inhaler), or nebulizer.
  • Why Inhalation is Great for Asthma: It delivers the drug right to your lungs, meaning you need a smaller dose, and you get fewer side effects compared to taking it by mouth.
  • Speed:
    • Oral inhalation: Onset in 1 minute (super fast!).
    • Nebulization: Onset in 5-30 minutes.
    • Oral: Onset in 15-30 minutes.
  • Peak Effect: About 1 hour for all routes.
  • Duration: About 4 hours.

Recap: Metaproterenol

  • Beta2 agonist, some beta1 effect.
  • Inhalation is preferred route (faster, fewer side effects).
  • Fast onset for inhalation.

10. Using Your Inhaler: The Right Way to Breathe Easy!

Getting your medication *into* your lungs is key! Proper inhaler technique makes a HUGE difference. If your inhaler isn't working, it's either your technique or it's empty!

The Step-by-Step for Beta2 Agonist MDI:

  1. Insert: Put the medication canister into the plastic mouthpiece.
  2. Shake it! Shake the inhaler well before *every* use.
  3. Cap Off: Remove the cap from the mouthpiece.
  4. Position: Hold the mouthpiece 1 to 2 inches from your mouth, or place it *in* your mouth. Ask your healthcare provider about using a spacer deviceA holding chamber attached to an inhaler that helps deliver more medication to the lungs and reduces deposition in the mouth and throat. Great for those with coordination challenges! – it can really help!
  5. Exhale & Inhale: Breathe out fully through your mouth. Then, take a slow, deep breath in through your mouth while simultaneously pressing the top of the canister once.
  6. Hold it! Hold your breath for a few seconds. Then, exhale slowly through pursed lips.
  7. Repeat (if needed): Wait 2 minutes if a second dose is required. Shake the inhaler again (with cap in place!) before the next puff.
  8. Test Spray: If it's a new inhaler or hasn't been used in a while, do a test spray into the air first.

Why Technique Matters:

  • Poor technique = medication trapped in your mouth/throat, not reaching your lungs.
  • Can cause dry mouth and throat irritation.

Don't Overdo It!

  • Using your aerosol drug too much can lead to tolerance (it stops working as well) and loss of effectiveness.
  • Sometimes, excessive use can even cause paradoxical bronchoconstriction (your airways actually constrict MORE!).
  • Too frequent dosing can also cause side effects like tremors, nervousness, and a fast heart rate.
  • If your usual dose isn't working, it means your asthma might be getting worse, and you need to see your healthcare provider! Don't just increase the dose yourself.

Common Side Effects (Reminders!):

  • Epinephrine: Tremors, dizziness, high BP, fast heart rate, palpitations, irregular heartbeats, chest pain. Monitor closely!
  • Beta2-adrenergic drugs (like Albuterol): Tremors, headaches, restlessness, increased pulse, palpitations (with high doses).
  • Diabetes Alert! Beta2 agonists can increase blood glucose. Patients with diabetes need to check their blood sugar more often.
  • Good news: Many side effects lessen after a week or two.

Recap: Inhaler Use

  • Shake, breathe out, inhale slowly while pressing, hold breath, wait 2 min for next puff.
  • Spacers help!
  • Overuse = tolerance, paradoxical constriction.
  • Side effects: tremors, fast heart rate, increased blood sugar.

11. Anticholinergics: Blocking the Squeeze!

These drugs block the action of acetylcholine, which usually causes airways to constrict. By blocking it, they help relax and dilate the bronchi!

Tiotropium (Spiriva®):

  • Main Use: Maintenance treatment for COPD bronchospasms linked to COPD. It's a daily medication, NOT for acute attacks.
  • How it works: Blocks muscarinic cholinergic receptors (specifically M3 receptors), stopping acetylcholine from causing bronchoconstriction.
  • Route: Inhalation only, using a special device called a HandiHalerA dry-powder inhaler (DPI) device used to administer certain medications like tiotropium by inhalation. (DPI).
  • Important!: Once you open a capsule, use it immediately! Don't save opened capsules. Wash the HandiHaler with warm water and dry it.
  • Common Side Effects: Dry mouth (very common!), constipation, insomnia, dizziness, headache, sinusitis, blurred vision, urinary retention, candidiasis.
  • Serious Reactions: Tachycardia, ileus (bowel paralysis), dehydration, hyperglycemia, dysrhythmias, anaphylaxis.
Drug Class Dosage
Anticholinergic COPD: A: Oral inhalation: 2 inhalations/day (2.5 mcg/actuation) at the same time daily; max: 2 inhalations/day.
Watch Out For! (Contraindications & Interactions)
Do NOT use if: Hypersensitivity to lactose, narrow-angle glaucoma, bladder obstruction, kidney problems, heart dysrhythmias, breastfeeding, older adults.
Drug: Increased anticholinergic effects if taken with phenothiazines. Decreased action of "prokinetics" (like cisapride, metoclopramide) which help move food through the gut.
How it Moves (Pharmacokinetics) How it Works (Pharmacodynamics)

Distribution: 72% sticks to proteins in blood.
Metabolism: Half-life (t½): 5–6 days.
Excretion: Excreted in urine as metabolites. 		Inhalation: Onset: 30 min, Peak: 1–4 hours, Duration: Unknown.
Therapeutic Use: Maintenance treatment of asthma and COPD.
Mechanism: Blocks muscarinic cholinergic receptors and stops acetylcholine, relaxing bronchial smooth muscle and dilating bronchi.
Side Effects (Common Annoyances) Adverse Reactions (More Serious Stuff!)
Insomnia, dizziness, depression, headache, sinusitis, nasopharyngitis, cough, dry mouth, vomiting, abdominal pain, constipation, urinary retention, joint pain, muscle pain, peripheral swelling, blurred vision, oral ulcers, candidiasis, infection. Tachycardia, ileus, dehydration, hyperglycemia, chest pain, GI obstruction, cataracts.

Ipratropium Bromide: Another Anticholinergic

  • Used to treat asthma by dilating bronchioles.
  • Good news: Few systemic side effects compared to other anticholinergics.
  • Route: MDI (inhaler).

The Dynamic Duo: Ipratropium & Albuterol!

  • This combination is super effective for COPD.
  • They work better together than alone (longer duration, more effective).
  • This combo significantly increases FEV1 (remember that from COPD?).

12. Methylxanthine (Xanthine) Derivatives: Theophyllines & More!

This group of bronchodilators includes aminophylline, theophylline, and caffeine! Yes, caffeine is a xanthine!

Besides opening airways, xanthines also:

  • Stimulate your Central Nervous System (CNS) and respiration.
  • Dilate your heart's blood vessels (coronary) and lung blood vessels (pulmonary).
  • Act as diuretics (make you pee more!).

Fun Fact!

Because they affect respiration and lung blood vessels, xanthines are used in asthma treatment.

Recap: Methylxanthines

  • Bronchodilators.
  • Includes aminophylline, theophylline, caffeine.
  • Also stimulate CNS, dilate vessels, cause diuresis.

13. Aminophylline-Theophylline: A Tricky Drug!

Aminophylline-theophyllineA methylxanthine bronchodilator that relaxes bronchial smooth muscles by inhibiting phosphodiesterase, leading to increased cAMP and bronchodilation. Known for its narrow therapeutic range and potential for toxicity. helps relax the smooth muscles of your bronchi, bronchioles, and lung blood vessels. How? By blocking that sneaky enzyme, phosphodiesterase, which boosts cAMP and leads to bronchodilation!

Focus Prompt!

Theophylline has a VERY LOW therapeutic index and a NARROW desired therapeutic range (5 to 15 mcg/mL). This means it's easy to give too much or too little.

Toxicity is HIGHLY likely when serum levels are greater than 20 mcg/mL. You MUST monitor these levels frequently!

  • Once a first-line drug for asthma, its use has dropped because of serious risks: dysrhythmias, seizures, cardiac arrest. Plus, it's not much better than beta agonists or glucocorticoids.
  • Now, it's mainly for maintenance therapy in chronic stable asthma/COPD when other drugs aren't enough.
  • Avoid in: Seizure disorders, heart, kidney, or liver disease.
Drug Route and Dosage
Aminophylline-theophylline A: 18–59 y: IV: 0.4 mg/kg/h infusion; max: 900 mg/day.
Older adults >60 y: IV: 0.2–0.3 mg/kg/h infusion.
Important: Individual dose adjusted based on actual serum theophylline levels.
Therapeutic range: 5–15 mcg/L (or mL).
Uses and Considerations
For bronchospasm due to asthma or COPD. Can cause restlessness, headache, insomnia, GERD, dizziness, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, low potassium, high calcium, palpitations, and fast heart rate. PB: 40%; t½: 6.5–10.5 hours.

Pharmacokinetics: How Your Body Handles It

  • Absorption: Good orally (especially liquids/uncoated tablets). Sustained-release forms absorb slower. Food/antacids can slow absorption but don't stop it. High fluid/protein meals can speed it up. Also given IV.
  • Metabolism: Processed by your liver enzymes. 90% excreted by kidneys.
  • 🚬 Smoking Alert! Tobacco smoking speeds up theophylline metabolism, meaning it leaves your body faster (shorter half-life). Smokers might need higher doses!
  • Kids: Shorter half-life, may need higher doses.
  • Nonsmokers/Older Adults: Longer half-life (6.5-10.5 hours), may need *lower* doses.
  • Certain Conditions: Heart failure, cor pulmonale, COPD, liver disease = very long half-life (24 hours!). Be super careful with dosing to avoid toxicity.

Pharmacodynamics: How it Acts

  • Increases cAMP, leading to bronchodilation.
  • Onset: 30 mins (oral), 1-2 hours (sustained-release).
  • Peak: 30 mins (IV), 1-2 hours (oral).

Side Effects & Adverse Reactions: Be Vigilant!

  • Common: Anorexia, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, gastric pain (more acid!), vomiting blood.
  • Cardiac: Dysrhythmias (irregular heartbeats), tachycardia (fast heart rate), palpitations, marked low blood pressure.
  • CNS (Brain): Headaches, irritability, restlessness, insomnia, dizziness, seizures (often worse in children!).
  • Avoid: Taking other xanthines (like caffeine) with theophylline – it adds to the side effects!
  • Toxicity: Remember, >20 mcg/mL is toxic!
  • Can cause high blood sugar, decreased clotting time, and rarely, high white blood cell count.
  • Diuretic Effect: Because it makes you pee, avoid too much caffeine (coffee, tea, cola, chocolate) and drink plenty of fluids!
  • IV Admin: Giving IV aminophylline too fast can cause severe dizziness, flushing, low BP, very slow heart rate, palpitations. ALWAYS administer IV slowly with an infusion pump!

Drug Interactions: The Combo Chaos!

  • Increase Theophylline Effect: Beta blockers, cimetidine, propranolol, erythromycin (they slow down its breakdown). Dose adjustment needed!
  • Decrease Theophylline Effect: Barbiturates, carbamazepine. Dose adjustment needed!
  • Digitalis: Theophylline increases risk of digitalis toxicity.
  • Lithium: Theophylline decreases lithium effects.
  • Phenytoin: Phenytoin decreases theophylline levels.
  • Beta-adrenergic agonists: If given together, can cause a synergistic effect, leading to cardiac dysrhythmias!

Recap: Theophylline (High-Alert Drug!)

  • Opens airways by increasing cAMP.
  • Narrow therapeutic range: 5-15 mcg/mL. Toxic >20!
  • Monitor levels closely.
  • Many side effects: GI, cardiac (dysrhythmias, tachycardia), CNS (seizures).
  • Affected by smoking, age, liver/heart disease.
  • Many drug interactions.

14. Leukotriene Receptor Antagonists: Inflammatory Blockers!

Leukotrienes (LTs)Powerful chemical mediators released in allergic reactions that cause inflammation, mucus production, and bronchoconstriction in the lungs. are chemical "bad guys" that cause inflammation in your lungs: more eosinophils, more mucus, swollen airways, and bronchoconstriction!

Leukotriene modifiers (LT receptor antagonists and LT synthesis inhibitors) are like "anti-inflammation superheroes" for asthma!

Focus Prompt!

Leukotriene modifiers are NOT for acute asthma attacks! They are for prevention (prophylaxis) and maintenance therapy of chronic asthma, especially for exercise-induced asthma.

The Three Amigos:

In the US, we have zafirlukast, zileuton, and montelukast.

  • Zafirlukast:
    • The first in its class! It blocks leukotriene receptors.
    • Reduces inflammation and constriction.
    • Oral, absorbed fast, taken twice a day.
  • Zileuton:
    • Blocks leukotriene *synthesis* (stops them from being made).
    • Also reduces inflammation and constriction.
    • Short half-life.
  • Montelukast (Singulair®):
    • Blocks leukotriene receptors.
    • Short half-life (2.7-5.5 hours).
    • Considered safe for children age 2 and up!
Drug Class Dosage
Bronchodilator: Leukotriene receptor antagonist A: PO: 10 mg/day in the evening or at least 2 hours before exercise; max: 10 mg/day.

15. Glucocorticoids (Steroids): The Mighty Anti-Inflammatories!

These are powerful anti-inflammatory drugs, super helpful for asthma, especially if it's not responding to bronchodilators.

Synergy!

Glucocorticoids work even better (synergistically) when given with a beta2 agonist!

Ways to Take Steroids:

  • MDI Inhaler: Like Beclomethasone (breathed in).
  • Tablet: Like Dexamethasone, Prednisone (swallowed).
  • Intravenous (IV): Like Dexamethasone (given in a vein).

Inhaled Steroids vs. Acute Attacks:

  • 🚫 NOT for severe acute asthma attacks! They take 1 to 4 weeks to fully kick in.
  • Best for: Long-term control and prevention of attacks. Patients on inhaled steroids usually have fewer symptoms and attacks.
  • Benefits: More effective than beta2 agonists for controlling asthma symptoms, especially by reducing how "twitchy" your airways are.
  • Safer: Inhaled steroids are preferred over oral steroids because they cause less adrenal suppression (a serious side effect of long-term oral steroids). Use oral steroids only if inhaled ones aren't enough.

Systemic Steroids (Oral/IV) for Severe Asthma:

  • For moderate to severe asthma flare-ups (exacerbations).
  • Examples: Prednisone, prednisolone, dexamethasone, methylprednisolone.
  • Typical dose: Methylprednisolone 40-80 mg/day for 3-10 days.
  • Short-term use: Can often be stopped suddenly once symptoms are controlled (adrenal suppression usually doesn't occur within 1-2 weeks).
  • Long-term use: Must be slowly weaned or tapered off to prevent asthma flare-ups and adrenal problems. Inhaled steroids are generally better for long-term use than alternate-day oral therapy.

Stomach Alert!

Steroids can irritate your stomach. ALWAYS take them with food to prevent ulcers!

Combination Drugs: Two-in-One Power!

  • A combo of fluticasone propionate (steroid) and salmeterol (beta2 agonist) is great for controlling asthma symptoms daily.
  • It helps both with constriction and inflammation.
  • Taken once in the morning and once at night.
  • Reminder: This combo drug does NOT replace your fast-acting "rescue" inhaler!

Side Effects & Adverse Reactions: What to Watch For!

Inhaled Glucocorticoids (Usually Local):

  • Throat irritation, hoarseness, dry mouth, coughing.
  • Fungal infections in mouth/throat (e.g., Candida albicans - "thrush") can occur.
    • Prevention! Use a spacer, rinse your mouth and throat with water after each dose, and wash your inhaler parts daily.

Oral & IV Glucocorticoids (Many More Systemic Side Effects with Long-Term Use!):

  • Short-term use (less than 2 weeks) usually has few major side effects.
  • Most adverse reactions appear within 2 weeks and are reversible.
  • Common Side Effects: Headache, feeling good (euphoria), confusion, sweating, insomnia, nausea, vomiting, weakness, irregular periods.
  • Serious Adverse Effects: Depression, stomach ulcers, loss of bone density/osteoporosis, psychosis.
  • Prolonged Use Dangers:
    • Electrolyte imbalance
    • Fluid retention (puffy eyelids, swollen legs, "moon face," weight gain)
    • High blood pressure
    • Thinning skin, bruising (purpura)
    • Hyperglycemia (high blood sugar)
    • Impaired immune response (more prone to infections)

Recap: Glucocorticoids

  • Powerful anti-inflammatories for asthma.
  • Inhaled for long-term control (preferred), NOT for acute attacks.
  • Oral/IV for severe exacerbations (taper off!).
  • Side effects: Thrush (inhaled), bone loss, high blood sugar, fluid retention, immune suppression (systemic).
  • Take with food!

16. Nursing Process: Bronchodilators - Bringing It All Together!

Let's use our nursing judgment with bronchodilators! Remember our core concept: Oxygenation (getting enough oxygen into the body).

Recognize Cues [Assessment]: What do you notice?

  • History: Get a full medical and drug history. Spot potential drug interactions!
  • Vital Signs: Check baseline vital signs and pulse oximetry. These are your benchmarks!
  • Respiratory Assessment: Listen for wheezing, note decreased breath sounds, cough type, and sputum production.
  • Brain Check: Look for confusion or restlessness – these could mean low oxygen (hypoxia) or too much CO2 (hypercapnia).
  • Hydration: Assess fluid status. Bronchodilators can cause diuresis, leading to dehydration, especially in kids and older adults.
  • Theophylline Specific: ALWAYS check serum theophylline levels. Remember: Toxicity > 20 mcg/mL!

Analyze Cues & Prioritize Hypothesis [Patient Problems]: What's the biggest issue?

  • #1: Airway Obstruction! (The core problem).
  • Decreased gas exchange (less O2 in, less CO2 out).
  • Dyspnea (shortness of breath).
  • Fatigue (from working so hard to breathe).

Generate Solutions [Planning]: What's the goal?

  • Goal 1: Patient will breathe freely (no wheezing!) and have clear lung sounds within 2-5 days.
  • Goal 2: Patient will correctly use their oral meds and inhaler as prescribed.

Take Action [Nursing Interventions]: What do YOU do?

  • Monitor VS: Watch blood pressure and heart rate closely – they can really jump! Look for cardiac dysrhythmias.
  • Hydration: Encourage fluids! It helps loosen thick secretions.
  • Drug Monitoring: Watch how the medication is working.
  • Side Effects: Be alert for any side effects.
  • Timing Meds: Give oral meds *after meals* to reduce stomach upset.
  • Consistent Dosing: Administer at regular intervals around the clock to keep a steady therapeutic level.
  • NO CRUSHING! Never crush enteric-coated (EC) or sustained-release (SR) tablets/capsules.
  • Theophylline Specific: ALWAYS check serum theophylline levels (normal: 5-15 mcg/mL).

Patient Teaching: Empowering Your Patient!

General Advice:

  • Pulse Check: Teach patients to monitor their own pulse rate.
  • Inhaler Levels: Remind them to check how much medication is left in their inhaler canister.
  • OTC Meds: Warn about taking over-the-counter (OTC) meds without checking with their healthcare provider first. Some OTCs can have dangerous additive effects!
  • Pregnancy: If planning pregnancy, discuss meds with provider first.
  • 🚫 Smoking: Advise patients to stop smoking! Sudden changes in smoking habits can mess with theophylline levels. Smoking makes the drug leave the body faster, so doses might need to increase.
  • Anxiety Relief: Suggest relaxation techniques, music, etc.
  • ID Alert: Encourage wearing a MedicAlert tag/bracelet if they have frequent or severe asthma attacks.
  • Herbal Interactions: Inform about potential interactions with complementary/alternative therapies.
  • Mental Health: Advise to report aggressive behavior, altered mood, or suicidal thoughts to their provider.

Self-Administration (Inhaler Technique Reminder):

  • Re-teach and demonstrate correct inhaler/nebulizer use. Warn against overuse (tolerance!).
  • Teach to monitor pulse and report irregularities compared to baseline.

Diet:

  • High Protein, Low Carb: Increases theophylline elimination.
  • Low Protein, High Carb: Prolongs theophylline half-life. (Dosage adjustments might be needed for diet changes!)

Evaluate Outcomes [Evaluation]: Did it work?

  • Effectiveness: Is the bronchodilator working? Is the patient breathing without wheezing? Are they free from side effects?
  • Theophylline Check: Confirm serum theophylline levels are within the therapeutic range (5-15 mcg/mL).

Recap: Nursing Process for Bronchodilators

  • Assess: Vitals, lung sounds, mental status, hydration, theophylline levels (5-15 mcg/mL).
  • Problems: Airway obstruction, poor gas exchange.
  • Goals: Clear lungs, correct self-admin.
  • Intervene: Monitor VS, hydrate, consistent timing, no crushing, check levels.
  • Teach: Inhaler use, avoid OTC/smoking, hydration, diet.
  • Evaluate: Clear lungs, no side effects, safe levels.

17. Nursing Process: Leukotriene Receptor Antagonists - Keeping Airways Calm!

Let's apply our nursing judgment to leukotriene receptor antagonists. Again, our core concept is Oxygenation.

Recognize Cues [Assessment]: What do you notice?

  • History: Get a full medical, drug, and *herbal* history. Watch for interactions!
  • Vital Signs: Baseline VS for comparison.
  • Respiratory Assessment: Look for wheezing, decreased breath sounds, cough, sputum.
  • Brain Check: Assess for confusion/restlessness (signs of low O2/high CO2).
  • Montelukast Specific: If children are prescribed chewable montelukast, ask about a history of phenylketonuria (PKU) – these tablets contain phenylalanine.
  • Hydration: Check fluid status; diuresis can lead to dehydration.

Analyze Cues & Prioritize Hypothesis [Patient Problems]: What's the biggest issue?

  • Airway obstruction.
  • Decreased gas exchange.
  • Dyspnea.
  • Fatigue.

Generate Solutions [Planning]: What's the goal?

  • Goal 1: Patient will be free from wheezing, or it will be much better.
  • Goal 2: Patient's lung fields will be clear within 2-5 days.
  • Goal 3: Patient will take meds as prescribed.

Take Action [Nursing Interventions]: What do YOU do?

  • Monitor Respirations: Rate, depth, rhythm, type.
  • Monitor Lung Sounds: Listen for rhonchi, wheezing, or rales.
  • Cyanosis Check: Observe lips and fingernails for blueness (cyanosis).
  • Drug Effectiveness: Monitor if the drug is actually working.
  • Side Effects: Watch for any side effects.
  • Hydration: Ensure adequate fluids to help loosen secretions.
  • Liver Function: Monitor liver function tests (AST and ALT) periodically – they can be elevated with zafirlukast and montelukast!
  • Pulmonary Therapy: Provide chest clapping and postural drainage as appropriate.

Patient Teaching: Empowering Your Patient!

General Advice:

  • Allergic Reaction: If rash or hives occur, stop the drug and call the provider immediately!
  • Liver Monitoring: Remind them about periodic liver function tests.
  • Herbal Alert: DO NOT take St. John’s wort without checking with provider (it decreases montelukast).
  • Caffeine Alert: Black or green tea, and guarana, with montelukast/zafirlukast can cause increased stimulation!
  • 🚫 Smoking: Encourage quitting.
  • Anxiety Relief: Discuss relaxation techniques.
  • ID Alert: MedicAlert tag for frequent/severe asthma attacks.
  • Pregnancy: Discuss with provider if planning pregnancy while on montelukast.
  • Granule Packets (Montelukast):
    • Don't open until ready to use.
    • Administer within 15 minutes of opening.
    • If mixed with baby formula/approved food (applesauce, carrots, rice, ice cream), DO NOT store for later!
  • Aspirin/NSAID Sensitivity: If they have aspirin sensitivity, avoid aspirin and NSAIDs while on montelukast to prevent bronchoconstriction.

Self-Administration:

  • 🚫 NOT for Acute Attacks! Emphasize that montelukast is for *prevention* and *chronic treatment*, NOT for rescuing acute attacks.
  • Rescue Inhaler Still Needed: Advise them to continue their usual inhaled prevention and short-acting rescue medication for exercise-induced bronchospasm.
  • Report Increased Rescue Use: Tell them to call their provider if they need their short-acting bronchodilator more often with montelukast.
  • Stay Compliant: They must stick to the medication regimen even when feeling symptom-free!
  • Chewable Tablets: Children MUST chew chewable tablets thoroughly – swallowing whole can change absorption.

Diet:

  • Take leukotriene receptor antagonists in the evening for best effect.

Evaluate Outcomes [Evaluation]: Did it work?

  • Effectiveness: Is the patient breathing without wheezing and free from drug side effects?
  • Activity Tolerance: Has their ability to tolerate activity improved?

Recap: Nursing Process for Leukotrienes

  • Assess: History (herbal!), VS, lungs, mental status, PKU history (montelukast), hydration.
  • Problems: Airway obstruction, poor gas exchange.
  • Goals: Improved breathing, clear lungs, med compliance.
  • Intervene: Monitor respirations/lungs, hydration, liver tests.
  • Teach: Not for acute attacks, report allergic reactions, herbal/caffeine warnings, proper granule use, continue rescue meds, compliance.
  • Evaluate: Improved breathing, increased activity tolerance.

18. Cromolyn: The Mast Cell Stabilizer

Cromolyn sodiumA mast cell stabilizer used for prophylactic treatment of asthma. It prevents the release of inflammatory mediators like histamine, thus preventing asthma attacks. It is NOT a bronchodilator and NOT for acute attacks. is a drug used for prophylactic treatment of asthma (meaning it prevents attacks!). You need to take it daily.

Focus Prompt!

Cromolyn DOES NOT have bronchodilator properties! It also is NOT used for acute asthmatic attacks!

  • How it works: It's a mast cell stabilizer. It stops mast cells from releasing histamine and other inflammatory mediators that cause asthma attacks.
  • Common Side Effects: Postnasal drip, nose/throat irritation, cough.
  • Tip to Reduce Side Effects: Drink water before and after using the drug.
  • Routes: Oral inhalation (MDI or nebulizer) and nasal inhalation (metered spray).
  • Can be used alongside beta adrenergics and xanthine derivatives.

⚠️ Serious Side Effect Alert!

Rebound bronchospasm is a serious side effect! You should NEVER discontinue cromolyn abruptly because it can lead to a severe asthmatic attack!

While cromolyn has few side effects, it's only moderately effective, and many newer drugs have mostly replaced its use.

Recap: Cromolyn

  • Prophylactic asthma treatment (daily).
  • NOT a bronchodilator, NOT for acute attacks.
  • Stabilizes mast cells (prevents histamine release).
  • Side effects: throat irritation (drink water!).
  • DO NOT STOP ABRUPTLY (rebound bronchospasm).

19. Asthma Drug Therapy: Tailoring Treatment to Severity

Asthma treatment isn't one-size-fits-all. It's often broken into two parts: long-term daily control and quick relief for sudden symptoms.

  • The long-term program may vary according to the symptoms of the asthma and its severity.
  • The quick-relief therapy is the same no matter how severe your asthma is. It's for emergencies! (Think your rescue inhaler!)

Recap: Asthma Severity

  • Treatment varies by severity.
  • Two main types: Long-term control (daily) and Quick-relief (acute).

20. Asthma Drug Therapy: Age Matters!

How we treat asthma changes a bit depending on whether the patient is a child or an older adult.

Young Children:

  • Cromolyn: Often used for its anti-inflammatory effects in kids.
  • Oral Glucocorticoids: Might be prescribed for moderate to severe asthma.
  • Inhaled Glucocorticoids: Typical dose is 1-2 inhalations, 4 times a day.
  • Oral Beta2-adrenergic agonists: May be ordered for severe conditions.

Older Adults:

  • Caution with Beta2-Adrenergic Agonists & Methylxanthines (like Theophylline):
    • Can cause tachycardia (fast heart rate), nervousness, and tremors in older adults, especially those with heart conditions.
  • Frequent Glucocorticoid Use Risks:
    • Increased risk of developing cataracts.
    • Increased risk of osteoporosis (brittle bones).
    • Increased risk of diabetes mellitus.
  • If a theophylline drug is prescribed, glucocorticoid dosages are usually *decreased*.

Recap: Asthma & Age

  • Kids: Cromolyn, oral/inhaled steroids, sometimes oral beta2.
  • Older Adults: Watch for heart side effects with beta2/theophylline. Steroids increase risk of cataracts, osteoporosis, diabetes.

21. Antimicrobials: When Infections Join the Party

Antibiotics are only used if a bacterial infection pops up, usually because of mucus getting trapped in the airways.

Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole is a common and effective antibiotic for mild to moderate acute exacerbations of chronic bronchitis (AECBs) from infectious causes.

Recap: Antimicrobials

  • Antibiotics only for bacterial infections.
  • Example: Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole for bronchitis flare-ups.

22. NCLEX Next Gen! Critical Thinking Case Study

Cognitive Skill: Prioritize hypothesis.

After not receiving any relief the symptoms worsened, so she went to the emergency department. Her oxygen saturation was 91% on room air. Rationale: Why these answers are correct!
