1. Introduction to the Cardiovascular System

The cardiovascular system is your body's vital transportation network, consisting of the heart, blood vessels (arteries and veins), and blood. It ensures that every cell receives the oxygen, nutrients, and hormones it needs, while also clearing out waste products.

  • Arteries carry oxygen-rich blood, narrowing into arterioles and then capillaries.
  • Capillaries deliver nutrients to cells and pick up waste (CO2, urea, creatinine, ammonia).
  • Deoxygenated blood returns via venules and veins to the lungs and kidneys for elimination.

The heart's powerful pumping action is the energy source that keeps this entire system circulating. Blockages in these vessels can severely hinder blood flow and oxygen delivery.

Recap: CV System Basics

  • Heart, blood vessels, blood = CV system.
  • Arteries carry O2-rich blood to cells.
  • Capillaries exchange nutrients for waste.
  • Veins return deoxygenated blood.
  • Heart is the pump; blockages impede flow.

2. Heart Anatomy: The Four-Chambered Pump

Your heart has four chambers: the right atrium, left atrium, right ventricle, and left ventricle.

  • Right Atrium: Receives deoxygenated blood from the body.
  • Right Ventricle: Pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs via the pulmonary artery for gas exchange.
  • Left Atrium: Receives oxygenated blood from the lungs.
  • Left Ventricle: Pumps oxygenated blood into the aorta for systemic circulation (the rest of the body). This is the thickest, strongest chamber!

The heart muscle is called the myocardium. It's protected by a fibrous covering called the pericardium and lined internally by the endocardium.

Four valves ensure one-way blood flow:

  • Atrioventricular (AV) valves: Tricuspid (Right) and Mitral (Left)
  • Semilunar valves: Pulmonic (Right) and Aortic (Left)

The heart muscle itself needs blood! This is supplied by the coronary arteries:

  • Right Coronary Artery: Supplies the right atrium and both ventricles.
  • Left Coronary Artery: Divides into the left circumflex artery and the anterior descending artery, supplying the left atrium and both ventricles.

Why Blockage is Bad!

Blockage in any of these coronary arteries can lead to a myocardial infarction (MI), or heart attack, due to lack of oxygen to the heart muscle.

3. Heart Function: Electrical Impulses & Blood Flow Regulation

Conduction of Electrical Impulses: The Heart's Built-in Electrical System!

The myocardium is unique – it generates and conducts its own electrical impulses!

  • Sinoatrial (SA) node: "The Pacemaker!" Located in the right atrium, it initiates cardiac impulses at 60-80 beats/min.
  • Atrioventricular (AV) node: If the SA node fails, the AV node takes over at a slower rate (40-60 beats/min). It sends impulses to the ventricles.
  • Ventricles can contract independently at 30-40 times/min if both SA and AV nodes fail.

Drugs can influence these impulses:

  • Sympathetic Nervous System (and stimulating drugs): Increases heart rate.
  • Parasympathetic Nervous System (and stimulating drugs): Decreases heart rate.

Regulation of Heart Rate and Blood Flow

Your heart typically pumps 4-8 liters of blood per minute (Cardiac Output). This is determined by Heart Rate and Stroke Volume.

  • Stroke Volume: The amount of blood ejected from the left ventricle with each heartbeat (~70 mL/beat).
  • Stroke Volume is influenced by:
    • Preload: The blood flow force stretching the ventricle at the end of diastole (filling). More preload = more stroke volume.
    • Contractility: The force of ventricular contraction.
    • Afterload: The resistance the ventricle must overcome to eject blood into the aorta. More afterload = less stroke volume.

Pulmonary Circulation:

  • Deoxygenated blood from the right ventricle to the lungs (via pulmonary artery).
  • Oxygenated blood returns to the left atrium (via pulmonary vein).

Systemic (Peripheral) Circulation:

  • Oxygenated blood from the left ventricle to the aorta and throughout the body (via arteries and arterioles).
  • Nutrients exchanged at capillaries; waste collected.
  • Deoxygenated blood returns to the heart (via venules and veins).

What's in your blood?

Blood is composed of plasma (90% water, 10% solutes), red blood cells (RBCs/hemoglobin for O2), white blood cells (WBCs for defense), and platelets (for clotting).

RBCs live ~120 days, WBCs only 2-24 hours!

Recap: Heart Function

  • SA Node (pacemaker, 60-80 bpm) > AV Node (40-60 bpm).
  • Sympathetic nervous system increases HR, Parasympathetic decreases HR.
  • Cardiac Output = Heart Rate x Stroke Volume.
  • Stroke Volume influenced by Preload, Contractility, Afterload.
  • Two circulations: Pulmonary (heart-lungs-heart), Systemic (heart-body-heart).

4. Heart Failure (HF): When the Pump Weakens

Heart Failure (HF)Also known as pump failure or chronic heart failure. Occurs when the heart muscle weakens and enlarges, losing its ability to pump blood efficiently through the heart and into systemic circulation. occurs when the heart muscle weakens and enlarges, losing its ability to pump blood effectively. This leads to blood backing up, causing congestion in the lungs and peripheral tissues (acute heart failure).

Common Causes of HF:

  • Chronic hypertension
  • Myocardial Infarction (MI)
  • Coronary Artery Disease (CAD)
  • Valvular heart disease
  • Congenital heart disease
  • Arteriosclerosis

Left-Sided vs. Right-Sided HF:

  • Left-Sided HF: Left ventricle doesn't pump enough blood to the body, causing blood to back up into the **lungs**. Symptoms: Shortness of breath (SOB), dyspnea.
  • Right-Sided HF: Right ventricle doesn't pump enough blood to the lungs, causing blood to back up into **peripheral tissues**. Symptoms: Peripheral edema.

In HF, both preload (excess blood volume in ventricles) and afterload (excess resistance in aorta) increase, making the heart work harder.

Laboratory Tests to Diagnose Heart Failure:

  • Atrial Natriuretic Hormone (ANH) / Atrial Natriuretic Peptide (ANP):
    • Reference: 20-77 pg/mL.
    • Elevated levels may confirm HF.
    • Secreted from heart atria, acts as antagonist to renin and aldosterone, causing vasodilation and increased urine output to decrease blood volume and BP.
  • Brain Natriuretic Peptide (BNP):
    • Desired value: <100 pg/mL; positive: >100 pg/mL.
    • Primarily secreted from atrial cardiac cells.
    • Aids in HF diagnosis, especially differentiating dyspnea from HF vs. lung dysfunction.
    • More sensitive test than ANP for diagnosing HF.
    • Note: Often higher in older women (e.g., 160 pg/mL at 80 years old), but significantly higher in HF (>400 pg/mL).

Nonpharmacologic Measures to Treat Heart Failure:

These are crucial for managing HF:

  • Limit salt intake (2 g/day, ~1 tsp).
  • Decrease or avoid alcohol intake.
  • Fluid restriction (if advised by provider).
  • Avoid smoking (deprives heart of O2).
  • Modify unhealthy behaviors associated with obesity.
  • Decrease saturated fat intake.
  • Engage in mild exercise (walking, bicycling) after consulting with provider.

Recap: Heart Failure & Management

  • HF = weakened, enlarged heart, poor pumping.
  • Left HF -> lung congestion (SOB, dyspnea). Right HF -> peripheral edema.
  • Labs: Elevated ANH/ANP, and especially BNP (>100 pg/mL, >400 in significant HF).
  • Nonpharmacologic: Low salt, limit alcohol, no smoking, healthy weight, mild exercise.

5. Agents Used to Treat Heart Failure: Cardiac Glycosides (Digoxin)

Cardiac GlycosidesA group of drugs (like Digoxin) derived from the foxglove plant, used to treat heart failure and certain dysrhythmias. They increase the force of myocardial contraction. are one of the oldest heart medications, obtained from the poisonous foxglove plant. They are primarily used for Heart Failure (HF) and certain cardiac dysrhythmias (atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter).

How Cardiac Glycosides Work (Digoxin Effects on Heart Muscle):

They inhibit the sodium-potassium pump, increasing intracellular calcium, which makes cardiac muscle contract more efficiently.

  • Positive Inotropic Action: Increases myocardial contraction (strengthens pump).
  • Negative Chronotropic Action: Decreases heart rate.
  • Negative Dromotropic Action: Decreases conduction of heart cells (especially through the AV node).

This leads to increased cardiac output, decreased preload, improved blood flow, and reduced edema.

Digoxin: Secondary HF Drug!

Digoxin does NOT prolong life in HF. It improves symptoms. First-line drugs for acute HF often include IV inotropic agents (dopamine, dobutamine) and phosphodiesterase (PDE) inhibitors (milrinone). Oral diuretics, beta blockers, ACE inhibitors, ARBs, and CCBs are also commonly used.

Digitalis (Digoxin) Toxicity: The Danger Zone!

Overdose or accumulation of digoxin can lead to digitalis toxicity, which is serious and potentially life-threatening. Older adults are particularly prone.

  • GI Symptoms: Anorexia, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain.
  • Cardiac Symptoms: Bradycardia (pulse < 60 bpm), premature ventricular contractions (PVCs), other cardiac dysrhythmias (ventricular dysrhythmias are especially dangerous).
  • Neurologic/Visual Symptoms: Headaches, malaise, blurred vision, visual illusions (white, green, or yellow halos around objects), confusion, delirium, hallucinations.

Remember: Hypokalemia (low serum potassium) significantly increases the risk of digitalis toxicity because digoxin binds more easily to its site of action when potassium levels are low.

Antidote for Digoxin Toxicity:

For severe digitalis toxicity, Digoxin-immune Fab can be given IV. It binds with digoxin, preventing it from acting and allowing it to be excreted.

Other Agents Used to Treat Heart Failure:

  • Phosphodiesterase (PDE) Inhibitors (e.g., Milrinone): Positive inotropic effects and vasodilation. Used IV for acute HF, monitor for cardiac dysrhythmias. (High-alert medication!)
  • Vasodilators: Decrease preload and afterload, reducing heart workload.
  • ACE Inhibitors & ARBs: Dilate vessels, improve renal blood flow, decrease fluid retention. (Monitor potassium with ACEIs!)
  • Diuretics: First-line for reducing fluid volume (e.g., Furosemide).
  • Spironolactone (Potassium-Sparing Diuretic): Blocks aldosterone, useful for moderate to severe HF. Monitor potassium.
  • Beta Blockers (e.g., Carvedilol, Metoprolol, Bisoprolol): Previously contraindicated, but now used cautiously at low, gradually increased doses to improve cardiac performance in chronic HF.
  • Nesiritide: Atrial natriuretic peptide hormone. Promotes vasodilation, natriuresis, and diuresis for acute decompensated HF.
  • Vericiguat: Guanylate cyclase stimulant. Newest drug for HF with reduced ejection fraction. Regulates vascular tone, cardiac contractility, cardiac remodeling. (Contraindicated in pregnancy!)

Herbal Alert for Cardiac Meds!

  • Ginseng, Licorice, Aloe, Ma-huang (Ephedra) may increase digoxin levels/toxicity.
  • St. John's wort, Psyllium may decrease digoxin absorption.
  • Hawthorn may increase digoxin effect and hypotension.
  • Goldenseal may decrease cardiac glycoside effects.

Recap: Cardiac Glycosides & HF Agents

  • Cardiac Glycosides (Digoxin): Positive Inotrope (+contraction), Negative Chronotrope (-HR), Negative Dromotrope (-conduction). Used for HF, AFib.
  • Digoxin Toxicity: Anorexia, N/V/D, bradycardia (<60), dysrhythmias, yellow/green halos, confusion. Risk increased by hypokalemia.
  • Antidote: Digoxin-immune Fab.
  • Other HF drugs: PDE inhibitors (Milrinone), Vasodilators, ACEIs/ARBs, Diuretics, Spironolactone, Beta Blockers (some), Nesiritide, Vericiguat.
  • Many herbal interactions!

6. Clinical Judgment: Nursing Process - Cardiac Glycosides (Digoxin)

Our core concept here is: Perfusion (the passage of blood flow through the arteries and capillaries that deliver oxygen and nutrients to body cells).

Recognize Cues [Assessment]: What do you notice?

  • Drug and Herbal History: Identify all medications and supplements. Pay attention to potassium-wasting diuretics or cortisone drugs (increase hypokalemia risk, which increases digoxin toxicity).
  • Baseline Pulse Rate: Obtain apical pulse for a full minute (should be >60 bpm before administering digoxin).
  • Signs of Digitalis Toxicity: Actively assess for anorexia, nausea, vomiting, bradycardia, cardiac dysrhythmias, and visual disturbances (e.g., yellow halos). Report immediately.

Analyze Cues & Prioritize Hypothesis [Patient Problems]: What's the biggest issue?

  • Decreased gas exchange
  • Decreased tissue perfusion
  • Hypoxemia
  • Ischemia
  • Digitalis toxicity (potential or actual)
  • Hypokalemia

Generate Solutions [Planning]: What's the goal?

  • The patient will demonstrate checking their pulse rate correctly before taking digoxin.
  • The patient will state the importance of reporting pulse rate <60 bpm or a marked decline.
  • The patient will list six foods high in potassium to maintain a desired serum potassium level.

Take Action [Nursing Interventions]: What do YOU do?

  • Apical Pulse: Always check apical pulse rate for a full minute before administering digoxin. Do NOT administer if pulse rate is below 60 beats/min.
  • Edema: Determine signs of peripheral and pulmonary edema (indicative of HF).
  • Serum Digoxin Level: Monitor serum digoxin level (normal therapeutic range: 0.8 to 2 ng/mL). Report levels >2 ng/mL (indicates digitalis toxicity).
  • Serum Potassium Level: Monitor serum potassium level (normal range: 3.5 to 5.0 mEq/L). Report if hypokalemia (<3.5 mEq/L) is present.

Patient Teaching: Empowering Your Patient!

General Advice:

  • Adherence: Emphasize the importance of adherence to drug therapy.
  • OTC Drugs: Advise patients to avoid OTC drugs without consulting a healthcare provider first.
  • Safety: Keep drugs out of reach of children and use childproof bottles.
  • Pulse Check: Teach patients and caregivers how to check pulse rate before administering.
  • Herb-Drug Interactions: Inform about potential interactions (e.g., ginseng, St. John's Wort).

Self-Administration:

  • Teach how to check pulse and notify provider if irregular or <60 bpm.

Side Effects:

  • Instruct patients to report side effects: pulse rate <60 bpm, nausea, vomiting, headache, diarrhea, and visual disturbances (like double vision or halos).

Diet:

  • Advise patients to eat foods high in potassium (fresh/dried fruits, fruit juices, vegetables including potatoes) to counteract potential hypokalemia.

Evaluate Outcomes [Evaluation]: Did it work?

  • Evaluate effectiveness by noting patient response (decreased heart rate, decreased rales) and absence of side effects.
  • Continue monitoring pulse rate.

Recap: NP - Cardiac Glycosides

  • Assess: Drug/herb history (K-wasting meds), apical pulse (>60 bpm), signs of digoxin toxicity (GI, cardiac, visual).
  • Problems: Decreased perfusion, digitalis toxicity, hypokalemia.
  • Goals: Proper pulse check, reporting abnormal pulse, K+-rich diet.
  • Intervene: Hold digoxin if apical pulse <60 bpm. Monitor edema, digoxin levels (0.8-2.0 ng/mL), potassium levels (3.5-5.0 mEq/L).
  • Teach: Adherence, no OTCs without consult, pulse check, report side effects (GI, bradycardia, visual), K+-rich foods.
  • Evaluate: Improved symptoms, stable pulse, absence of side effects.

7. Antianginal Drugs: Relieving Chest Pain

Angina PectorisAcute cardiac pain caused by inadequate blood flow (ischemia) to the myocardium, often due to plaque occlusions or spasms in the coronary arteries. is acute cardiac pain caused by insufficient blood flow (ischemia) to the myocardium. It's often described as tightness or pressure in the chest, sometimes radiating down the left arm or to the neck.

Types of Angina Pectoris:

  • Classic (Stable) Angina: Occurs predictably with stress or exertion.
  • Unstable (Preinfarction) Angina: Occurs frequently, is progressive, unpredictable, and unrelated to activity. Often indicates an impending MI, requiring immediate medical intervention.
  • Variant (Prinzmetal, Vasospastic) Angina: Occurs during rest, caused by coronary artery spasm.

Nonpharmacologic Measures to Control Angina:

  • Avoid heavy meals, smoking, extreme weather changes.
  • Avoid strenuous exercise and emotional upset.
  • Practice proper nutrition, moderate exercise (with provider approval), adequate rest, and relaxation techniques.

Types of Antianginal Drugs:

These drugs increase oxygen supply or decrease oxygen demand by the myocardium.

  1. Nitrates
  2. Beta Blockers
  3. Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs)

Nitrates and CCBs are effective for variant (vasospastic) angina. Beta blockers are NOT. For classic (stable) angina, beta blockers are effective. Unstable angina requires immediate medical care, typically with sublingual/IV nitrates, followed by IV beta blockers or CCBs.

Nitrates: The First Line of Defense!

Nitrates (like Nitroglycerin) cause generalized vascular and coronary vasodilation, increasing blood flow to myocardial cells and reducing myocardial ischemia. They also decrease cardiac preload and afterload, which reduces the heart's workload.

Patient Safety: Don't Confuse!

Do NOT confuse Nitrostat (nitroglycerin), a coronary vasodilator, with Nystatin, an antifungal antibiotic!

Sublingual (SL) nitroglycerin: Most common. Place under tongue, do not swallow (first-pass metabolism). If pain not subsided in 5 minutes, call 911. Store in original airtight, amber glass containers (heat/light sensitive). Common side effects: dizziness, faintness, headache (may decrease with continued use, use acetaminophen).

Topical (ointment, transdermal patch): Slower absorption. Patch needs an 8-12 hour nitrate-free interval daily to prevent tolerance.

Rebound Effect Alert!

Do NOT abruptly discontinue nitroglycerin ointment or patches; taper doses over several weeks to prevent severe rebound pain from myocardial ischemia and reflex tachycardia.

Beta Blockers: Slowing the Heart's Pace

Beta-adrenergic blockers decrease the heart rate and blood pressure by blocking sympathetic nervous system effects (epinephrine, norepinephrine). They are used as antianginal, antidysrhythmic, and antihypertensive drugs.

  • Effective for classic (stable) angina by reducing heart rate and contractility, thus decreasing oxygen demand.
  • Nonselective Beta Blockers (e.g., Propranolol, Nadolol): Block beta1 and beta2 receptors. Decrease HR, but can cause bronchoconstriction.
  • Selective (Cardioselective) Beta Blockers (e.g., Atenolol, Metoprolol): Primarily block beta1 receptors, decreasing HR without causing bronchoconstriction. Preferred for angina.

Critical Warning!

Do NOT abruptly discontinue beta blockers! Taper the dose over several days/weeks to avoid reflex tachycardia and recurrence of anginal pain, or even life-threatening cardiac dysrhythmias.

Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs): Relaxing Vessels

CCBs (e.g., Verapamil, Nifedipine, Diltiazem) treat stable and variant angina, dysrhythmias, and hypertension. They relax coronary artery spasms (variant angina) and peripheral arterioles (stable angina), decreasing cardiac oxygen demand.

  • Decrease cardiac contractility, afterload, and peripheral resistance.
  • Reduce workload of the heart, decreasing oxygen need.
  • Nifedipine (Immediate-release capsules): Associated with increased risk of sudden cardiac death at high doses; typically used only for acute BP increases in hospital. Sustained-release forms are safer.

Side effects of CCBs include headache, hypotension, dizziness, flushing, and peripheral edema. Monitor liver enzymes periodically.

Recap: Antianginal Drugs

  • Angina: Chest pain from myocardial ischemia. Types: Classic (exertion), Unstable (unpredictable, emergency!), Variant (rest/spasm).
  • Nitrates (Nitroglycerin): Vasodilate, decrease preload/afterload, increase O2 supply. SL (rapid onset), topical (need nitrate-free interval). Headache common. Don't stop abruptly!
  • Beta Blockers (Atenolol, Metoprolol): Decrease HR & contractility, reduce O2 demand. Best for classic angina. DON'T STOP ABRUPTLY.
  • CCBs (Verapamil, Nifedipine, Diltiazem): Relax coronary arteries & peripheral arterioles. Reduce O2 demand. Effective for both types. Headache, hypotension, edema possible.

8. Clinical Judgment: Nursing Process - Antianginals

Our core concept here is: Perfusion.

Recognize Cues [Assessment]: What do you notice?

  • Baseline Vital Signs: Obtain for future comparisons.
  • Health and Drug Histories: Note contraindications (e.g., marked hypotension or AMI for nitroglycerin).

Analyze Cues & Prioritize Hypothesis [Patient Problems]: What's the biggest issue?

  • Dyspnea
  • Myocardial tissue injury
  • Pain
  • Decreased tissue perfusion
  • Hypoxemia
  • Anxiety
  • Reduced functional ability
  • Hypotension

Generate Solutions [Planning]: What's the goal?

  • The patient will report that angina pain is controlled by nitroglycerin or other antianginals.

Take Action [Nursing Interventions]: What do YOU do?

  • Monitor Vital Signs: Hypotension is common with most antianginal drugs.
  • Patient Positioning (Nitrates): Position the patient sitting or lying down when administering a nitrate for the first time. Check vital signs lying down, then sitting up, and have the patient rise slowly.
  • SL Nitrates: Offer sips of water before giving SL nitrates (dryness inhibits absorption).
  • IV Nitroglycerin: Monitor effects; report persistent angina.
  • Nitroglycerin Ointment/Patch: Apply ointment with tongue blade/gloves to designated paper mark; do not touch medication. Rotate skin sites for patches. Avoid hairy areas.
  • Defibrillator Paddles: Do NOT apply nitroglycerin ointment or patch near defibrillator-cardioverter paddle placement (risk of explosion/burns).

Patient Teaching: Empowering Your Patient!

General Advice:

  • SL Nitroglycerin: Administer if chest pain occurs. If pain not subsided or worsened in 5 minutes, call 911 immediately.
  • Alcohol: Advise against alcohol with nitroglycerin (can cause hypotension, weakness, faintness).
  • Tolerance: Advise to notify provider if chest pain not completely alleviated (tolerance can occur).
  • Discontinuation: Inform patients NOT to discontinue beta blockers and calcium blockers without provider approval (severe withdrawal symptoms: reflex tachycardia, pain).

Self-Administration:

  • SL Nitroglycerin: Demonstrate proper sublingual administration (under tongue, quick absorption). Note: Stinging/biting sensation indicated freshness with older tablets; may not be present with newer ones.
  • Storage: Store nitrates away from light in a dry place, in original screw-cap, amber glass bottle (protects potency).
  • Nitroglycerin Patches: Apply once daily, usually morning. Rotate sites (chest, thighs, arms); avoid hairy areas.
  • Persistent Pain: Advise seeking medical attention if nitroglycerin doesn't relieve pain.

Side Effects:

  • Headache: Suggest acetaminophen for headache (common with initial nitroglycerin use, lasts ~30 min).
  • Hypotension: If hypotension occurs from SL nitroglycerin, place patient in supine position with legs elevated.
  • Pulse Rate: Instruct how to check pulse rate.
  • Dizziness/Faintness: Advise patients on beta blockers/CCBs to notify provider if dizziness/faintness occurs (may indicate hypotension).

Evaluate Outcomes [Evaluation]: Did it work?

  • Evaluate patient's response to nitrates for relieving anginal pain.
  • Note occurrence of headache, dizziness, or faintness.

Recap: NP - Antianginals

  • Assess: Baseline VS, health/drug history (contraindications for nitrates).
  • Problems: Pain, decreased perfusion, hypoxemia.
  • Goals: Angina pain control.
  • Intervene: Monitor VS (hypotension!), position with nitrates, offer water for SL, monitor IV, proper ointment/patch application (NO DEFIB PADDLE AREAS!).
  • Teach: SL nitroglycerin use (call 911 if no relief in 5 min), avoid alcohol, report tolerance, NO ABRUPT STOP (beta blockers/CCBs). Proper storage, patch use. Treat headache (acetaminophen), hypotension (supine/legs up).
  • Evaluate: Pain relief, side effects management.

9. Antidysrhythmic Drugs: Restoring Heart Rhythm

Cardiac Dysrhythmia (Arrhythmia)Any deviation from the normal rate or pattern of the heartbeat, including too slow (bradycardia), too fast (tachycardia), or irregular. is any deviation from the normal rate or pattern of the heartbeat.

  • ECG waves: P wave (atrial), QRS (ventricular depolarization), T wave (ventricular repolarization).
  • Atrial dysrhythmias: Decrease cardiac output by 33%.
  • Ventricular dysrhythmias: Life-threatening (ineffective pumping, decreased/absent cardiac output, risk of ventricular fibrillation and death). CPR is often needed.

Causes of Dysrhythmias:

  • Myocardial Infarction (MI)
  • Hypoxia, hypercapnia
  • Thyroid disease, CAD
  • Cardiac surgery, excess catecholamines
  • Electrolyte imbalance

Cardiac Action Potentials: The Electrical Dance of the Heart

This is how cardiac cells generate electrical impulses for contraction.

  • Sodium and Calcium influx leads to depolarization (contraction).
  • Potassium efflux leads to repolarization (relaxation).
  • Myocardial ischemia can cause irregular contractions.
  • Phase 0: Rapid depolarization (Na+ influx).
  • Phase 1: Initial repolarization (Na+ influx termination).
  • Phase 2: Plateau (Ca2+ influx, prolonged action potential, contraction).
  • Phase 3: Rapid repolarization (K+ efflux).
  • Phase 4: Resting membrane potential.

Types of Antidysrhythmic Drugs: Restoring Harmony

The goal is to restore normal cardiac rhythm. These are HIGH-ALERT drugs.

Mechanisms of Action of Antidysrhythmics:

  • Block adrenergic stimulation.
  • Depress myocardial excitability/contractility.
  • Decrease conduction velocity.
  • Increase recovery time (repolarization).
  • Suppress automaticity.

Antidysrhythmics are grouped into four classes:

  1. Class I: Sodium (Fast) Channel Blockers: Decrease sodium influx.
    • IA (Quinidine, Procainamide): Slow conduction, prolong repolarization.
    • IB (Lidocaine, Mexiletine): Slow conduction, shorten repolarization. Used for acute ventricular dysrhythmias.
    • IC (Flecainide): Prolong conduction, little effect on repolarization.
  2. Class II: Beta Blockers: Reduce calcium entry, decrease conduction velocity, automaticity, and refractory time. (e.g., Propranolol, Acebutolol, Esmolol, Sotalol).
  3. Class III: Drugs that Prolong Repolarization: Prolong action potential duration and refractory period (e.g., Amiodarone). Used in emergencies when other drugs are ineffective.
  4. Class IV: Calcium (Slow) Channel Blockers: Block calcium influx, slow conduction velocity, decrease myocardial contractility, increase refraction in AV node (e.g., Verapamil, Diltiazem).

All Antidysrhythmics are Potentially Prodysrhythmic!

They can sometimes *cause* new or worsen existing dysrhythmias due to drug activity on a diseased heart. Therapy is often initiated with continuous cardiac monitoring in a hospital setting.

Recap: Antidysrhythmics

  • Dysrhythmia: Abnormal heart rate/rhythm. Ventricular types are life-threatening.
  • Caused by MI, hypoxia, electrolytes, etc.
  • Action Potentials: Na+/Ca2+ influx (depolarization/contraction), K+ efflux (repolarization/relaxation).
  • Antidysrhythmic Classes:
    • Class I (Sodium Channel Blockers): IA (prolong repol.), IB (shorten repol., e.g., Lidocaine), IC (little repol. effect).
    • Class II (Beta Blockers): Decrease conduction, automaticity.
    • Class III (Prolong Repolarization): Increase refractory period (e.g., Amiodarone).
    • Class IV (CCBs): Block Ca2+ influx, decrease contractility (e.g., Verapamil, Diltiazem).
  • WARNING: All are potentially prodysrhythmic.

10. Clinical Judgment: Nursing Process - Antidysrhythmics

Our core concept here is: Perfusion.

Recognize Cues [Assessment]: What do you notice?

  • Health and Drug Histories: Document history of SOB, palpitations, coughing, chest pain (type, duration, severity), previous angina/dysrhythmias, and current medications.
  • Baseline Vital Signs & ECG: Obtain for future comparisons.
  • Cardiac Enzyme Results: Monitor early AST, LDH, CPK, and cardiac-specific troponins.

Analyze Cues & Prioritize Hypothesis [Patient Problems]: What's the biggest issue?

  • Decreased gas exchange
  • Dysrhythmia
  • Decreased tissue perfusion
  • Hypoxemia
  • Anxiety
  • Reduced cardiac output

Generate Solutions [Planning]: What's the goal?

  • The patient will be in normal sinus rhythm.
  • The patient will comply with the antidysrhythmic drug regimen.

Take Action [Nursing Interventions]: What do YOU do?

  • Monitor Vital Signs: Watch for hypotension.
  • Administration: Administer IV push or bolus over 2-3 minutes or as prescribed.
  • ECG Monitoring: Monitor for abnormal patterns (PVCs, increased PR/QT intervals, widening QRS complex). Report findings (increased QT interval risk for torsades de pointes).

Patient Teaching: Empowering Your Patient!

General Advice:

  • Compliance: Teach patients to take prescribed drugs as ordered (drug compliance is essential).
  • Specific Instructions: Provide specific instructions for each drug (e.g., photosensitivity for amiodarone).

Side Effects:

  • Tell patients to report side effects and adverse reactions (dizziness, faintness, nausea, vomiting) to a healthcare provider.
  • Avoidances: Advise patients to avoid alcohol (intensifies hypotensive reaction), caffeine (increases catecholamine levels), and tobacco (promotes vasoconstriction).

Evaluate Outcomes [Evaluation]: Did it work?

  • Evaluate effectiveness by comparing heart rates with baseline and assessing patient response.
  • Report side effects and adverse reactions (drug regimens may need adjustment, or drug discontinuation may be necessary if prodysrhythmic effects occur).

Recap: NP - Antidysrhythmics

  • Assess: Health/drug history (symptoms, prior issues), baseline VS/ECG, cardiac enzymes.
  • Problems: Dysrhythmia, decreased perfusion.
  • Goals: Normal sinus rhythm, drug compliance.
  • Intervene: Monitor VS (hypotension), proper IV administration, ECG monitoring (PVCs, PR/QT changes, QRS widening).
  • Teach: Compliance, drug-specific info (amiodarone photosensitivity), report side effects. Avoid alcohol, caffeine, tobacco.
  • Evaluate: Rhythm control, patient response, side effects.

Question 1: Immediate Assessment Data

Question 2: Priority Lab Interventions

  • Potassium (K) 3.0 mEq/L: Priority. Normal range is 3.5–5.0 mEq/L. Low potassium significantly increases the risk and severity of digoxin toxicity.
  • Chloride (Cl) 90 mEq/L: Priority. Normal range is 96–106 mEq/L. A low chloride level can contribute to vomiting and electrolyte imbalance.
