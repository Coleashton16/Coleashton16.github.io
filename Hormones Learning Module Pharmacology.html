1. Introduction: Your Body's Master Control System!

Welcome to the fascinating world of endocrine disorders! This module will cover drugs that affect the pituitary, thyroid, parathyroid, and adrenal glands.

Understanding these glands, their hormones, and how they function is key to grasping how drugs either replace missing hormones or inhibit overactive ones. It's all about maintaining homeostasis!

Recap: Intro Key Points

  • Focus: Drugs for pituitary, thyroid, parathyroid, and adrenal glands.
  • Goal: Understand hormone replacement/inhibition.
  • Importance: Maintaining body balance (homeostasis).

2. Pituitary Gland: The Master Gland!

The pituitary gland (hypophysis)A small gland at the base of the brain, often called the "master gland," that secretes hormones regulating growth, metabolism, reproduction, and other vital functions. It has anterior and posterior lobes. is tiny but mighty, located at the base of your brain. It has two main parts, each with its own set of important hormones:

  • Anterior Lobe (Adenohypophysis): The "front" part, which secretes hormones that control other glands.
  • Posterior Lobe (Neurohypophysis): The "back" part, which stores and releases hormones made by the hypothalamus.

Visualize It!

FIG. 49.1: The anterior and posterior pituitary glands. (Imagine a diagram showing the two lobes and their connections!)

Most endocrine systems work on a negative feedback loop. Think of it like a thermostat:

  • If hormone levels are too low, the gland is stimulated to produce more.
  • If hormone levels are too high, the gland is inhibited to produce less.

This keeps things balanced!

Quick Fact: The hypothalamus directly controls the pituitary gland by producing releasing and inhibiting hormones!

Recap: Pituitary Gland Key Points

  • Pituitary: "Master gland" at brain base.
  • Two Lobes: Anterior (controls other glands), Posterior (stores hypothalamus hormones).
  • Regulation: Negative feedback loop for hormone balance.

2.1. Anterior Lobe Hormones: The Stimulators!

The anterior pituitary produces many hormones. We'll focus on a few key ones and their related drugs:

Growth Hormone (GH) and Growth Hormone-Releasing Hormone (GHRH) / Growth Hormone-Inhibiting Hormone (GHIH)

GH is crucial for growth in children and maintaining tissue in adults.

Disorders:

  • Growth Hormone Deficiency (Dwarfism): Insufficient GH, leading to short stature.
  • Growth Hormone Excess (Gigantism/Acromegaly): Too much GH. Gigantism in children (before epiphyseal plates close), acromegaly in adults (after plates close, leading to enlarged hands, feet, face).
Drug Class / Prototype Mechanism of Action Indications Adverse Effects / Nursing Considerations
Somatropin (recombinant GH) Replaces endogenous GH. Stimulates skeletal growth, internal organ, and protein synthesis. GH deficiency (pediatric growth failure), Turner's syndrome, chronic renal failure, Prader-Willi syndrome.
  • Hyperglycemia
  • Hypothyroidism
  • Pancreatitis (rare)
  • Injection site reactions
  • Monitor growth (height/weight), glucose, thyroid function.
  • Administer IM or SubQ, usually daily at bedtime.
  • Discontinue when epiphyses close.
Octreotide (Sandostatin) (GH receptor antagonist / Somatostatin analog) Mimics somatostatin; inhibits GH release, as well as TSH, insulin, glucagon, and gastrin. Acromegaly, severe diarrhea/flushing associated with carcinoid tumors, VIPomas.
  • GI disturbances (nausea, diarrhea, abdominal pain) - very common!
  • Gallstones (long-term use)
  • Hyper/hypoglycemia
  • Cardiac arrhythmias (bradycardia)
  • Monitor glucose, ECG, gallbladder function (ultrasound).
  • Administer SubQ, IM, or IV.

Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone (TSH / Thyrotropin)

TSH stimulates the thyroid gland to produce thyroid hormones (T3 and T4).

Prototype:

Drug Class / Prototype Mechanism of Action Indications Adverse Effects / Nursing Considerations
Thyrotropin Alfa (Thyrogen) (recombinant TSH) Stimulates thyroid tissue to absorb iodine and produce thyroglobulin. Adjunct for diagnostic testing (thyroglobulin measurements) and radioactive iodine ablation in clients with differentiated thyroid cancer after thyroidectomy.
  • Nausea, headache, fatigue
  • May cause transient increase in thyroid tumor size/bleeding.
  • Administered IM.
  • Monitor for signs of tumor growth or bleeding in cancer patients.

Focus Box: Somatropin and Octreotide!

Remember that somatropin replaces growth hormone, and its main use is for growth deficiency in children. Octreotide (think "octo-stop") stops the release of GH and other hormones, making it useful for conditions like acromegaly.

Recap: Anterior Lobe Key Points

  • Anterior Pituitary: Releases hormones controlling other glands.
  • Somatropin: Treats GH deficiency (dwarfism); monitor growth, glucose.
  • Octreotide: Treats GH excess (acromegaly); inhibits GH; monitor GI, glucose, cardiac.
  • Thyrotropin Alfa: Diagnostic tool for thyroid cancer after surgery.

2.2. Posterior Lobe Hormones: The Water & Contraction Duo!

The posterior pituitary doesn't make its own hormones; it stores and releases two key hormones produced by the hypothalamus:

  • Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH) / Vasopressin: Controls water reabsorption in the kidneys.
  • Oxytocin: Stimulates uterine contractions and milk ejection.

Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH) / Vasopressin

ADH helps the body retain water, preventing dehydration.

Disorders:

  • Diabetes Insipidus (DI): Deficiency of ADH, leading to excessive urination and thirst.
  • Syndrome of Inappropriate Antidiuretic Hormone (SIADH): Excess ADH, leading to water retention and hyponatremia.
Drug Class / Prototype Mechanism of Action Indications Adverse Effects / Nursing Considerations
Desmopressin (DDAVP) (synthetic ADH analog) Increases water reabsorption in renal tubules, leading to decreased urine output and increased urine osmolality. Also increases factor VIII and von Willebrand factor. Diabetes Insipidus, nocturnal enuresis (bedwetting), hemophilia A, von Willebrand's disease.
  • Hyponatremia (due to water intoxication from excessive fluid intake) - BIG RISK!
  • Headache, drowsiness, GI upset.
  • Hypertension (vasoconstrictive effect).
  • Monitor fluid intake and output (I&O), serum sodium (Na+), blood pressure (BP), urine specific gravity.
  • Instruct client to restrict fluid intake, especially when used for nocturnal enuresis.
  • Available PO, intranasal, SubQ, IV.

Oxytocin (Pitocin)

Oxytocin is a hormone that stimulates uterine contractions.

Drug Class / Prototype Mechanism of Action Indications Adverse Effects / Nursing Considerations
Oxytocin (Pitocin) (Uterine stimulant) Stimulates uterine smooth muscle contraction. Induction or augmentation of labor, postpartum hemorrhage (to promote uterine contraction and prevent excessive bleeding).
  • Uterine hyperstimulation/tachysystole (contractions too strong or too frequent).
  • Fetal distress (due to reduced oxygenation from hyperstimulation).
  • Water intoxication/hyponatremia (less common with therapeutic doses).
  • Maternal hypertension.
  • Administered IV (always via infusion pump).
  • HIGH ALERT MEDICATION! Monitor fetal heart rate (FHR), uterine contraction pattern, maternal BP/HR, I&O closely.
  • Have tocolytics (e.g., terbutaline) readily available to relax uterus if hyperstimulation occurs.

Focus Box: Desmopressin!

Think "D for Desmopressin, D for DI, D for Decreased Urine Output!" But remember the biggest risk: hyponatremia due to fluid overload if patients drink too much.

Recap: Posterior Lobe Key Points

  • Posterior Pituitary: Stores ADH & Oxytocin (made by hypothalamus).
  • Desmopressin: Synthetic ADH; treats DI & bedwetting; major risk: hyponatremia from fluid overload.
  • Oxytocin: Induces labor, prevents postpartum hemorrhage; HIGH ALERT! Monitor FHR, contractions.

3. Nursing Process: Pituitary Hormones

Applying the nursing process ensures safe and effective care when administering pituitary-related drugs.

Assessment:

  • Baseline VS & Weight: Crucial for monitoring fluid balance and therapeutic effects.
  • Fluid & Electrolytes: Especially sodium for desmopressin, glucose for somatropin/octreotide.
  • Growth & Development: For pediatric clients on somatropin.
  • Neurological Status: Changes can indicate fluid/electrolyte imbalances (e.g., hyponatremia with desmopressin, improved mental status with Octreotide in certain conditions).

Nursing Diagnoses:

  • Deficient Fluid Volume (for DI, before desmopressin)
  • Excess Fluid Volume (risk with desmopressin)
  • Risk for Injury (due to altered growth, adverse effects)
  • Deficient Knowledge (regarding medication administration and side effects)

Planning:

  • Client will achieve optimal hormone balance.
  • Client will demonstrate understanding of medication regimen and adverse effects.

Interventions:

  • Administer medications per order: Pay close attention to route (e.g., SubQ for somatropin, IV infusion for oxytocin).
  • Monitor for therapeutic effects: Increased growth, decreased urine output, improved uterine tone.
  • Monitor for adverse effects: Hyperglycemia (somatropin/octreotide), hyponatremia (desmopressin), hyperstimulation (oxytocin).
  • Fluid Restriction: Educate clients on desmopressin to restrict fluids to prevent water intoxication.

Client Teaching:

  • Purpose of medication, dose, route, frequency.
  • Importance of consistent administration (e.g., somatropin at bedtime).
  • Signs and symptoms of adverse effects to report (e.g., headache/drowsiness with desmopressin, severe GI upset with octreotide).
  • Importance of regular follow-up for lab tests (e.g., electrolytes, glucose, thyroid function).

Evaluation:

  • Are desired outcomes achieved? (e.g., adequate growth rate, normal I&O, stable sodium levels).
  • Is the client adhering to the regimen and managing side effects effectively?

NCLEX Next Gen Question!

A 6-year-old client with growth hormone deficiency is prescribed daily subcutaneous somatropin. The nurse is reinforcing teaching with the parents.

The nurse instructs the parents to rotate injection sites daily. What is another crucial instruction the nurse should provide regarding somatropin administration?

Recap: Nursing Process Pituitary Key Points

  • Assessment: VS, I&O, weight, electrolytes, growth, neuro status.
  • Interventions: Correct administration, monitor for therapeutic/adverse effects.
  • Teaching: Fluid restriction (desmopressin), report adverse effects, follow-up labs.
  • Evaluation: Are outcomes met?

4. Thyroid Gland: The Metabolism Maestro!

The thyroid gland, located in your neck, produces hormones that regulate metabolism, growth, and development. Its main hormones are:

  • Thyroxine (T4) and Triiodothyronine (T3): These increase metabolic rate. T3 is more potent than T4.
  • Calcitonin: Helps regulate calcium levels (lowers them).

The thyroid gland needs iodine to produce T3 and T4. Without enough iodine, the gland can enlarge (goiter).

Anatomy Snapshot

FIG. 50.1: The thyroid and parathyroid glands. (Imagine a diagram of the neck showing these glands!)

Recap: Thyroid Gland Key Points

  • Thyroid: Metabolism, growth, development.
  • Hormones: T3 (potent), T4.
  • Iodine: Essential for T3/T4 synthesis.

4.1. Hypothyroidism: Low and Slow!

Hypothyroidism occurs when the thyroid gland doesn't produce enough thyroid hormones. This leads to a slowed metabolic rate.

Signs & Symptoms (think "slow"):

  • Fatigue, lethargy
  • Weight gain
  • Cold intolerance
  • Bradycardia
  • Constipation
  • Dry skin, brittle hair
  • Depression

The most common cause is Hashimoto's disease (autoimmune destruction of the thyroid).

Drug Class / Prototype Mechanism of Action Indications Adverse Effects / Nursing Considerations
Levothyroxine (Synthroid, Levoxyl) (synthetic T4) Replaces endogenous T4; converted to T3 in the body. Increases metabolic rate. Hypothyroidism (all forms), thyroid suppression therapy.
  • Adverse effects typically occur with **overdosage** (signs of hyperthyroidism):
    • Tachycardia, palpitations, angina
    • Tremors, nervousness, insomnia
    • Weight loss
    • Heat intolerance
    • Diarrhea
  • Lifelong therapy.
  • Take on an empty stomach in the morning, at least 30-60 minutes before food/other meds (especially calcium, iron, antacids, sucralfate).
  • Monitor TSH levels (primary monitor), T3, T4.
  • Full therapeutic effect may take several weeks.
  • Safe in pregnancy (dose often needs to increase).
  • Do NOT switch brands without HCP approval.
Liothyronine (Cytomel) (synthetic T3) Synthetic T3; faster onset and shorter half-life than levothyroxine. Severe hypothyroidism (myxedema coma), short-term thyroid replacement.
  • Similar to levothyroxine, but adverse effects may be more rapid/intense due to faster onset.
  • Not for long-term routine replacement due to shorter half-life requiring multiple daily doses and higher risk of fluctuations.
  • Interacts with many drugs like levothyroxine.

Focus Box: Levothyroxine Admin!

Remember: EMPTY STOMACH, MORNING, AWAY FROM OTHER MEDS! This is a classic NCLEX point.

Recap: Hypothyroidism Key Points

  • Hypothyroidism: Low thyroid hormones, slowed metabolism.
  • S/S: Fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, bradycardia.
  • Levothyroxine: Lifelong T4 replacement; take on empty stomach, morning; monitor TSH.
  • Liothyronine: Synthetic T3; faster, for severe cases.

4.2. Hyperthyroidism: High and Fast!

Hyperthyroidism occurs when the thyroid gland produces too much thyroid hormone, leading to a sped-up metabolic rate.

Signs & Symptoms (think "fast"):

  • Nervousness, irritability, anxiety
  • Weight loss
  • Heat intolerance, sweating
  • Tachycardia, palpitations
  • Diarrhea
  • Exophthalmos (bulging eyes - specific to Grave's Disease)

The most common cause is Grave's disease (autoimmune stimulation of the thyroid).

Drug Class / Prototype Mechanism of Action Indications Adverse Effects / Nursing Considerations
Propylthiouracil (PTU) (Thioamide / Antithyroid) Inhibits thyroid hormone synthesis; also inhibits conversion of T4 to T3 (unique to PTU). Hyperthyroidism (Grave's disease), thyroid storm/crisis (due to rapid action). Preferred in first trimester of pregnancy if needed.
  • Agranulocytosis (severe decrease in WBCs, increasing infection risk) - Monitor CBC, report fever/sore throat immediately!
  • Liver damage (hepatotoxicity) - Monitor LFTs.
  • Hypothyroidism (from over-treatment)
  • Rash, nausea, arthralgia.
  • Take with food to reduce GI upset.
  • Multiple daily doses.
Methimazole (Tapazole) (Thioamide / Antithyroid) Inhibits thyroid hormone synthesis. Does NOT inhibit T4 to T3 conversion. Hyperthyroidism. Preferred over PTU usually due to once-daily dosing and less severe hepatotoxicity risk (except in first trimester of pregnancy, where PTU is preferred).
  • Agranulocytosis (same as PTU, but slightly lower incidence) - Monitor CBC, report fever/sore throat immediately!
  • Liver damage (hepatotoxicity) - Less common than PTU.
  • Hypothyroidism (from over-treatment)
  • Rash, nausea.
  • Teratogenic (contraindicated in pregnancy, especially first trimester).
  • Once daily dosing.
Radioactive Iodine (I-131) (Radioactive isotope) Absorbed by thyroid, emits radiation to destroy thyroid cells. Hyperthyroidism (definitive treatment), thyroid cancer.
  • Hypothyroidism (common long-term side effect, often requiring lifelong thyroid hormone replacement).
  • Neck tenderness, swelling, nausea.
  • Contraindicated in pregnancy and lactation.
  • Radiation precautions: Limit contact with others (especially children/pregnant women), flush toilet twice, don't share utensils, proper waste disposal.
Propranolol (Inderal) (Beta Blocker) Blocks beta-adrenergic receptors, reducing sympathetic symptoms. Also inhibits peripheral conversion of T4 to T3. Symptomatic relief of hyperthyroidism (tachycardia, tremors, nervousness), thyroid storm. NOT a primary antithyroid drug.
  • Bradycardia, hypotension
  • Bronchospasm (contraindicated in asthma/COPD)
  • Fatigue, depression
  • Masks signs of hypoglycemia in diabetics.
  • Monitor HR, BP, respiratory status.
  • Do NOT abruptly discontinue.

Focus Box: Antithyroid Meds - Safety First!

For PTU and Methimazole: Always remember the huge risk of agranulocytosis! Teach patients to report sore throat or fever immediately. For Radioactive Iodine: Pregnancy is a no-go, and radiation precautions are essential!

Recap: Hyperthyroidism Key Points

  • Hyperthyroidism: High thyroid hormones, sped-up metabolism.
  • S/S: Nervousness, weight loss, heat intolerance, tachycardia.
  • PTU & Methimazole: Inhibit hormone synthesis; risk of agranulocytosis. PTU for pregnancy 1st trimester.
  • Radioactive Iodine: Destroys thyroid; causes hypothyroidism; strict radiation precautions.
  • Propranolol: Symptomatic relief, especially cardiac symptoms.

5. Nursing Process: Thyroid Hormones

Nursing care for clients with thyroid disorders involves careful assessment, intervention, and teaching.

Assessment:

  • Baseline VS & Weight: Monitor for changes indicative of hyper/hypothyroidism.
  • Energy Level & Activity Tolerance: Changes indicate effectiveness of therapy.
  • Skin, Hair, GI, Neuro Status: All affected by thyroid function.
  • Thyroid Function Tests (TSH, T3, T4): Key lab values.
  • CBC: For agranulocytosis with antithyroid drugs.
  • Liver Function Tests (LFTs): For hepatotoxicity with antithyroid drugs.

Nursing Diagnoses:

  • Activity Intolerance (hypothyroidism)
  • Decreased Cardiac Output (hypothyroidism) / Risk for Decreased Cardiac Output (hyperthyroidism)
  • Disturbed Thought Processes (hyperthyroidism)
  • Imbalanced Nutrition: Less Than Body Requirements (hyperthyroidism) / More Than Body Requirements (hypothyroidism)
  • Risk for Infection (agranulocytosis)

Planning:

  • Client will achieve euthyroid state (normal thyroid function).
  • Client will demonstrate safe medication administration and adherence.
  • Client will report signs of adverse effects promptly.

Interventions:

  • Levothyroxine: Administer on empty stomach in the morning, consistently.
  • Antithyroid Drugs (PTU/Methimazole): Monitor for agranulocytosis (fever, sore throat), jaundice.
  • Radioactive Iodine: Implement strict radiation precautions (client, visitors, waste).
  • Symptom Management: Address fatigue, constipation, heat intolerance, etc.

Client Teaching:

  • Levothyroxine: Lifelong therapy, consistent timing, avoid interactions with food/other meds. Report chest pain, palpitations.
  • Antithyroid Drugs: Report fever, sore throat immediately (agranulocytosis). Monitor for signs of hypothyroidism.
  • Radioactive Iodine: Detailed radiation safety instructions, signs of hypothyroidism.
  • Importance of regular follow-up and lab work.

Evaluation:

  • Are TSH, T3, T4 levels within normal range?
  • Has the client's metabolism stabilized (weight, energy, VS)?
  • Is the client free from severe adverse effects?

NCLEX Next Gen Question!

A client with Grave's disease is prescribed methimazole. The nurse is reinforcing teaching about potential adverse effects.

The nurse instructs the client to report which symptom immediately due to the risk of a severe adverse effect?

Recap: Nursing Process Thyroid Key Points

  • Assessment: VS, weight, energy, labs (TSH, CBC, LFTs).
  • Interventions: Correct admin, symptom management.
  • Teaching: Levothyroxine timing, agranulocytosis S/S (fever/sore throat for antithyroids), radiation safety.
  • Evaluation: Normal labs, stable metabolism, no severe adverse effects.

6. Parathyroid Glands: The Calcium Controllers!

The parathyroid glands are four tiny glands located on the posterior surface of the thyroid gland. They produce Parathyroid Hormone (PTH), which is the primary regulator of calcium and phosphate in the blood.

PTH increases blood calcium by:

  • Releasing calcium from bone.
  • Increasing calcium reabsorption in the kidneys.
  • Activating Vitamin D, which increases calcium absorption in the GI tract.

Calcitonin (from the thyroid) has the opposite effect: it lowers blood calcium.

Recap: Parathyroid Glands Key Points

  • Parathyroid Glands: Produce PTH.
  • PTH: Increases blood calcium.
  • Calcitonin: Lowers blood calcium.

6.1. Hypoparathyroidism: Low Calcium!

Hypoparathyroidism results from insufficient PTH, leading to hypocalcemia (low blood calcium) and increased phosphate levels.

Signs & Symptoms (think "neuromuscular excitability"):

  • Tetany (muscle spasms, tingling, numbness)
  • Chvostek's sign (facial twitching when facial nerve is tapped)
  • Trousseau's sign (carpal spasm when BP cuff inflated)
  • Seizures
  • Laryngospasm, bronchospasm
  • Prolonged QT interval on ECG

Often caused by accidental removal during thyroid surgery.

Drug Class / Prototype Mechanism of Action Indications Adverse Effects / Nursing Considerations
Calcium Supplements (e.g., Calcium Carbonate, Calcium Gluconate) Replaces deficient calcium. Hypocalcemia (acute and chronic hypoparathyroidism), osteoporosis.
  • Hypercalcemia (constipation, nausea, vomiting, polyuria, polydipsia, muscle weakness, confusion, cardiac arrhythmias).
  • IV calcium gluconate for acute tetany (administer slowly, monitor ECG).
  • Oral calcium should be taken with meals for better absorption.
  • Do NOT administer IV calcium with bicarbonate; it will precipitate.
Calcitriol (Rocaltrol) (active form of Vitamin D) Promotes calcium absorption from the GI tract and reabsorption from renal tubules. Hypoparathyroidism, chronic kidney disease (to manage hypocalcemia).
  • Hypercalcemia (same symptoms as above).
  • Monitor serum calcium and phosphate levels closely.
  • Teach signs of hypercalcemia.

Focus Box: Hypocalcemia S/S!

Chvostek's and Trousseau's signs are classic indicators of hypocalcemia. Remember "CATS" for convulsion, arrhythmias, tetany, spasms/stridor!

Recap: Hypoparathyroidism Key Points

  • Hypoparathyroidism: Insufficient PTH, leading to hypocalcemia.
  • S/S: Tetany, Chvostek's, Trousseau's, seizures.
  • Treatment: Calcium supplements (IV for acute), Calcitriol (active Vit D).
  • Monitor for hypercalcemia (over-treatment).

6.2. Hyperparathyroidism: High Calcium!

Hyperparathyroidism results from excessive PTH, leading to hypercalcemia (high blood calcium) and low phosphate levels.

Signs & Symptoms (think "bones, stones, abdominal groans, and psychic moans"):

  • Bone pain, fractures (calcium taken from bones)
  • Kidney stones (calcium deposits)
  • Constipation, nausea, abdominal pain
  • Fatigue, weakness, depression, confusion
  • Polyuria, polydipsia (excessive urination and thirst)
  • Shortened QT interval on ECG

Often caused by a benign tumor (adenoma) on one of the parathyroid glands.

Drug Class / Prototype Mechanism of Action Indications Adverse Effects / Nursing Considerations
Calcitonin Salmon (Miacalcin) (synthetic calcitonin) Directly inhibits bone resorption (calcium release from bone) and increases renal excretion of calcium. Hypercalcemia, osteoporosis, Paget's disease.
  • Nausea, vomiting, flushing (especially with IV administration).
  • Nasal irritation (with nasal spray form).
  • Administer IM, SubQ, or intranasal.
  • Test dose recommended for allergy if given for first time (salmon allergy).
Cinacalcet (Sensipar) (Calcimimetic) Increases the sensitivity of calcium-sensing receptors on the parathyroid gland, leading to decreased PTH secretion. Secondary hyperparathyroidism in chronic kidney disease, parathyroid carcinoma.
  • Hypocalcemia (most common and serious adverse effect).
  • Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea.
  • Monitor serum calcium closely, especially after initiation or dose adjustment.
  • Administer with food or shortly after a meal.
Alendronate (Fosamax) (Bisphosphonate) Inhibits osteoclast activity, reducing bone resorption and decreasing serum calcium (primarily for osteoporosis, but can help with hypercalcemia from bone breakdown). Osteoporosis, Paget's disease, hypercalcemia of malignancy.
  • Esophagitis, esophageal ulceration (major risk!).
  • Musculoskeletal pain, osteonecrosis of the jaw (rare).
  • Administer in the morning with a full glass of water, at least 30 minutes before food/other meds. Client must remain upright for at least 30 minutes after administration.
  • Monitor serum calcium.

Focus Box: Alendronate Admin!

Remember the strict administration rules for Alendronate: Morning, empty stomach, full glass of water, remain upright for 30-60 mins! This prevents serious esophageal issues.

Recap: Hyperparathyroidism Key Points

  • Hyperparathyroidism: Excess PTH, leading to hypercalcemia.
  • S/S: Bone pain, kidney stones, GI upset, fatigue.
  • Treatment: Calcitonin (lowers Ca), Cinacalcet (reduces PTH), Alendronate (reduces bone resorption).
  • Alendronate: Strict administration to prevent esophagitis.

7. Nursing Process: Parathyroid Hormones / Calcium Balance

Managing calcium and phosphate balance is key in parathyroid disorders.

Assessment:

  • Baseline VS: Especially HR for cardiac arrhythmias associated with electrolyte imbalances.
  • Fluid & Electrolytes: Serum calcium, phosphate, magnesium, albumin.
  • Neuromuscular Status: Chvostek's/Trousseau's (hypocalcemia), muscle weakness (hypercalcemia).
  • Renal Function: BUN, creatinine (calcium affects kidneys, and renal function affects calcium/phosphate).
  • Bone Pain/Fractures: Monitor for skeletal issues.

Nursing Diagnoses:

  • Risk for Injury (fractures, seizures)
  • Imbalanced Nutrition
  • Deficient Knowledge
  • Constipation (hypercalcemia) / Diarrhea (rarely, severe hypocalcemia treatments)

Planning:

  • Client will maintain serum calcium and phosphate levels within normal limits.
  • Client will be free from signs/symptoms of hypo/hypercalcemia.

Interventions:

  • Hypocalcemia: Administer calcium/Vitamin D as ordered. Monitor ECG during IV calcium. Institute seizure precautions.
  • Hypercalcemia: Promote hydration (IV fluids as ordered) to excrete calcium. Administer calcitonin, bisphosphonates as ordered.
  • Dietary Teaching: High calcium/Vit D for hypocalcemia; low calcium for hypercalcemia.
  • Safe Medication Administration: Especially for alendronate.

Client Teaching:

  • Dietary considerations for calcium/phosphorus.
  • Signs and symptoms of hypo/hypercalcemia to report.
  • Correct administration of medications (e.g., alendronate instructions).
  • Importance of regular lab monitoring.

Evaluation:

  • Are serum calcium and phosphate levels within normal range?
  • Are neuromuscular symptoms resolved or controlled?
  • Is the client adhering to dietary and medication regimens?

NCLEX Next Gen Question!

A client with hypercalcemia due to hyperparathyroidism is prescribed calcitonin salmon nasal spray. The nurse is teaching the client about administering this medication.

The nurse instructs the client to alternate nostrils daily to prevent which common local adverse effect?

Recap: Nursing Process Parathyroid Key Points

  • Assessment: VS, fluid/electrolytes (Ca, Phos), neuromuscular.
  • Interventions: Treat hypo/hypercalcemia, seizure precautions.
  • Teaching: Diet, S/S of imbalances, specific drug admin.
  • Evaluation: Normal labs, symptom resolution.

8. Adrenal Glands: The Stress Responders!

The adrenal glands sit on top of your kidneys and have two main parts:

  • Adrenal Cortex (Outer part): Produces steroid hormones:
    • Glucocorticoids: (e.g., cortisol) affect metabolism, reduce inflammation, regulate immune response.
    • Mineralocorticoids: (e.g., aldosterone) regulate fluid and electrolyte balance (sodium, potassium).
    • Androgens (sex hormones)
  • Adrenal Medulla (Inner part): Produces catecholamines (epinephrine, norepinephrine).

The adrenal cortex is regulated by ACTH from the anterior pituitary. This is another example of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis.

Anatomy Snapshot

FIG. 51.1: The adrenal glands and the adrenal cortex and medulla. (Imagine a diagram of the adrenal glands on the kidneys!)

Recap: Adrenal Glands Key Points

  • Adrenal Glands: On top of kidneys, two parts.
  • Cortex: Glucocorticoids (cortisol), Mineralocorticoids (aldosterone), Androgens.
  • Medulla: Epinephrine, Norepinephrine.
  • Regulated by HPA axis.

8.1. Glucocorticoids: The Stress & Anti-Inflammatory Hormones!

Glucocorticoids are widely used for their anti-inflammatory, immunosuppressive, and metabolic effects. They are essential for life.

Disorders related to endogenous glucocorticoids:

  • Addison's Disease (Adrenal Insufficiency): Insufficient production of adrenal cortical hormones (glucocorticoids and mineralocorticoids).
  • Cushing's Syndrome: Excessive production of adrenal cortical hormones.
Drug Class / Prototype Mechanism of Action Indications Adverse Effects / Nursing Considerations
Prednisone (Glucocorticoid) Mimics natural cortisol. Anti-inflammatory, immunosuppressive, metabolic effects. Inflammatory/autoimmune disorders (asthma, arthritis, lupus), adrenal insufficiency (replacement), organ transplant rejection prevention, cancer.
  • Short-term use: Increased appetite, insomnia, nervousness.
  • Long-term/high-dose use (Cushingoid effects):
    • Hyperglycemia (monitor blood glucose!)
    • Increased risk of infection (immunosuppression)
    • Osteoporosis/fractures
    • Fluid retention, hypertension
    • Mood changes (euphoria, depression)
    • Moon face, buffalo hump, truncal obesity
    • Muscle wasting, thinning skin, striae
    • Peptic ulcers
  • Adrenal crisis/insufficiency with abrupt withdrawal: MUST TAPER OFF!
  • Administer with food/milk to reduce GI upset.
  • Monitor blood glucose, BP, weight, electrolytes (especially K+).
  • Risk for cataracts and glaucoma.
Dexamethasone (Glucocorticoid) Potent synthetic glucocorticoid, high anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressive effects. Longer acting. Cerebral edema, severe inflammation, diagnostic testing for Cushing's, chemotherapy adjunct.
  • Similar to prednisone, but effects may be more pronounced due to potency.
  • Monitor for all signs of Cushingoid effects.
  • Taper carefully.

Focus Box: Glucocorticoids - Tapering is Key!

Never, ever abruptly stop systemic glucocorticoids! This can lead to a life-threatening adrenal crisis. Always taper them slowly under medical supervision.

Recap: Glucocorticoids Key Points

  • Glucocorticoids: Anti-inflammatory, immunosuppressive, metabolic.
  • Prednisone/Dexamethasone: Common prototypes.
  • Long-term AE: Cushingoid effects (hyperglycemia, infection, osteoporosis, fluid retention).
  • CRITICAL: ALWAYS TAPER OFF to prevent adrenal crisis.

8.2. Mineralocorticoids: The Fluid & Salt Balancers!

Mineralocorticoids primarily regulate sodium and potassium balance, and thus fluid balance. The main natural mineralocorticoid is aldosterone.

Drug Class / Prototype Mechanism of Action Indications Adverse Effects / Nursing Considerations
Fludrocortisone (Florinef) (Mineralocorticoid) Mimics aldosterone; enhances reabsorption of sodium and water in renal tubules, leading to potassium and hydrogen ion excretion. Addison's disease (adrenal insufficiency) as replacement therapy, often with a glucocorticoid.
  • Fluid retention, edema, hypertension (due to sodium/water retention).
  • Hypokalemia (due to potassium excretion).
  • Heart failure exacerbation.
  • Monitor BP, weight, I&O, serum electrolytes (Na+, K+).
  • Clients may need potassium supplements.
  • Teach signs of fluid overload and hypokalemia.

Focus Box: Fludrocortisone Effects!

Remember "Fluid-rocortisone" helps you remember it's about fluid balance. Too much fluid retention and dangerously low potassium are the main concerns!

Recap: Mineralocorticoids Key Points

  • Mineralocorticoids (Fludrocortisone): Regulate Na+/K+/fluid.
  • Used for Addison's disease.
  • AE: Fluid retention, HTN, hypokalemia. Monitor BP, weight, I&O, K+.

9. Nursing Process: Adrenal Hormones

Nursing care for clients on adrenal hormone therapy requires careful monitoring for a wide range of effects.

Assessment:

  • Baseline VS & Weight: Crucial for fluid status (fludrocortisone) and general well-being (glucocorticoids).
  • Fluid & Electrolytes: Na+, K+, glucose (all affected by both classes).
  • Signs of Infection: Glucocorticoids suppress immunity.
  • Skin Integrity: Thinning skin, delayed healing.
  • Mood/Mental Status: Mood swings, psychosis with glucocorticoids.
  • Adrenal Insufficiency S/S: Fatigue, hypotension, dehydration (if tapering too fast).
  • Cushing's Syndrome S/S: Moon face, buffalo hump, striae (if over-treated with glucocorticoids).

Nursing Diagnoses:

  • Risk for Infection
  • Fluid Volume Excess / Deficient
  • Disturbed Body Image
  • Imbalanced Nutrition
  • Risk for Injury (osteoporosis)
  • Deficient Knowledge

Planning:

  • Client will maintain optimal fluid and electrolyte balance.
  • Client will be free from infection.
  • Client will understand the importance of tapering glucocorticoids.

Interventions:

  • Administer exactly as ordered: Especially critical for tapering glucocorticoids.
  • With Food: Administer glucocorticoids with food/milk to reduce GI upset.
  • Monitor Blood Glucose: Regularly, as hyperglycemia is common.
  • Monitor for Fluid Retention: Daily weights, I&O, edema, BP.
  • Infection Control: Teach client signs of infection, avoid crowds.
  • Potassium Monitoring/Supplementation: For fludrocortisone.

Client Teaching:

  • Do NOT abruptly stop glucocorticoids! Explain tapering.
  • Signs and symptoms of infection to report.
  • Monitor blood glucose if diabetic.
  • Dietary considerations (e.g., low sodium/high potassium with fludrocortisone).
  • Wear a medical alert bracelet if on long-term therapy.
  • Report any unusual mood changes or severe GI upset.

Evaluation:

  • Are fluid/electrolyte balance and blood glucose within normal limits?
  • Is the client free from infection?
  • Is the client adhering to the tapering schedule and reporting any concerns?
