1. Introduction: Your Body's Master Control System!

Welcome to the fascinating world of endocrine disorders! This module will cover drugs that affect the pituitary, thyroid, parathyroid, and adrenal glands.

Understanding these glands, their hormones, and how they function is key to grasping how drugs either replace missing hormones or inhibit overactive ones. It's all about maintaining homeostasis!

Recap: Intro Key Points

  • Focus: Drugs for pituitary, thyroid, parathyroid, and adrenal glands.
  • Goal: Understand hormone replacement/inhibition.
  • Importance: Maintaining body balance (homeostasis).

2. Pituitary Gland: The Master Gland!

The pituitary gland (hypophysis)A small gland at the base of the brain, often called the "master gland," that secretes hormones regulating growth, metabolism, reproduction, and other vital functions. It has anterior and posterior lobes. is tiny but mighty, located at the base of your brain. It has two main parts, each with its own set of important hormones:

  • Anterior Lobe (Adenohypophysis): The "front" part, which secretes hormones that control other glands.
  • Posterior Lobe (Neurohypophysis): The "back" part, which stores and releases hormones made by the hypothalamus.

Visualize It!

FIG. 49.1: The anterior and posterior pituitary glands. (Imagine a diagram showing the two lobes and their connections!)

The amount of hormones secreted from the pituitary (especially the anterior lobe) is controlled by a negative feedback system. Think of it like a thermostat:

  • Too Much Hormone? If your target gland (like the thyroid) secretes too much hormone, the pituitary gland gets a signal to **reduce its own hormone release.**
  • Too Little Hormone? If there's a lack of hormone from the target gland, the pituitary gets a signal to **increase its hormone release.**

FIG. 49.2: Negative feedback loop of hypothalamus and pituitary. (Imagine a diagram showing hypothalamus -> pituitary -> target gland, with arrows indicating inhibition and stimulation!)

This system keeps your body's hormone levels just right!

Recap: Pituitary Overview

  • Master gland with Anterior (adenohypophysis) and Posterior (neurohypophysis) lobes.
  • Regulates other glands.
  • Uses negative feedback to maintain hormone balance.

3. Anterior Lobe: The Hormone Commanders!

The anterior pituitary gland secretes several key hormones that act as "commanders," telling other glands and tissues what to do:

  • Growth Hormone (GH): Stimulates growth in tissue and bone.
  • Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone (TSH): Acts on the thyroid gland.
  • Adrenocorticotropic Hormone (ACTH): Stimulates the adrenal gland.
  • Gonadotropins (FSH & LH): Affect the ovaries and testes (reproduction).
  • Prolactin (PRL): Primarily affects breast tissues (milk production).

Growth Hormone (GH)

What GH Does:

  • GH does **not** have a specific target gland; it affects **all body tissues and bone**.
  • GH replacement stimulates linear growth (height) when there are GH deficiencies.
  • GH drugs **cannot be given orally** because they are destroyed by GI enzymes. They must be given subcutaneously (subcut) or intramuscularly (IM).

GH Deficiency: Dwarfism

  • If a child's height is well below average, GH deficiency might be the cause, leading to **dwarfism**.
  • Testing for GH deficiency (like GH stimulation test with insulin tolerance test, or Macimorelin) is done to ensure replacement therapy is truly needed, as it's costly.
  • GH must be given **before the epiphyses (growth plates in bones) are fused** in children. Prolonged therapy can significantly increase height.
  • **Caution!** Prolonged GH therapy can interfere with insulin secretion, potentially causing **diabetes mellitus**.
  • Athletes should **NOT** take GH for muscle building due to blood glucose effects and other serious side effects.

GH Excess: Gigantism & Acromegaly

  • **Gigantism:** Excessive growth during childhood.
  • **Acromegaly:** Excessive growth *after* puberty (e.g., enlarged hands, feet, facial features).
  • Both are often caused by a pituitary tumor.
  • **Treatment for GH Excess:**
    • **GH receptor antagonists** (e.g., pegvisomant): Block GH receptor sites.
    • **Somatostatin analogues** (e.g., lanreotide, octreotide): Inhibit GH secretion.
    • **Dopamine agonists** (e.g., bromocriptine): Inhibit GH secretion (especially from pituitary adenomas).

Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone (TSH)

  • The anterior pituitary secretes TSH in response to TRH (from the hypothalamus).
  • TSH tells the thyroid gland to release **thyroxine (T4)** and **triiodothyronine (T3)**.
  • Excess TSH can lead to hyperthyroidism; TSH deficit can lead to hypothyroidism.
  • **Thyrotropin** (a purified TSH extract) is used diagnostically for thyroid cancer and to differentiate between primary and secondary hypothyroidism. Side effects include hyperthyroidism symptoms, hives, rash, itching, and flushing.

Adrenocorticotropic Hormone (ACTH)

  • Hypothalamus releases CRF, stimulating pituitary to secrete ACTH.
  • ACTH stimulates the adrenal cortex to release glucocorticoids (cortisol), mineralocorticoids (aldosterone), and androgens. It also affects catecholamines (epinephrine, norepinephrine) from the adrenal medulla.
  • ACTH and cortisol follow a diurnal rhythm (higher in early morning, decreases throughout the day), but stress overrides this.
  • **Adrenal insufficiency (hypocortisolism):** Can be due to low ACTH or adrenal gland dysfunction.
  • **Cosyntropin (synthetic ACTH)** or **corticotropin (exogenous ACTH)** is used to diagnose adrenal disorders.
    • **Cosyntropin:** Diagnostic only. Less potent, less allergenic. Measures cortisol response. Can increase electrolyte loss with diuretics. Side effects: bradycardia, hypertension, sinus tachycardia, peripheral edema.
    • **Corticotropin:** Used to diagnose adrenal disorders, treat MS exacerbations, and infantile spasms. Controls ACTH synthesis from cholesterol. Should be **tapered over 2 weeks** to avoid adrenal insufficiency. Many drug interactions (e.g., diuretics/penicillins can cause hypokalemia; digitalis toxicity with hypokalemia; phenytoin/rifampin/barbiturates decrease effect; diabetics may need more insulin).

Prolactin (PRL)

  • Primary function: **Stimulation of breast tissue for milk production.**
  • **PRL deficiency (hypoprolactinemia):** No symptoms except lactation disruption during breastfeeding.
  • **Excess PRL (hyperprolactinemia):**
    • Males: Gynecomastia (excess breast tissue), galactorrhea (lactation), decreased sperm production.
    • Females: Non-pregnancy-related lactation, amenorrhea.
  • Treatment for excess PRL: **Dopamine agonists** (e.g., bromocriptine, cabergoline). Cabergoline is generally better tolerated.

Recap: Anterior Lobe Hormones

  • GH: Growth. Deficiency=dwarfism, Excess=gigantism/acromegaly. Administer subcut/IM. Monitor blood glucose.
  • TSH: Stimulates thyroid. Used diagnostically.
  • ACTH: Stimulates adrenal cortex (cortisol, aldosterone). Used diagnostically (cosyntropin) or for MS/spasms (corticotropin). Taper corticotropin.
  • PRL: Milk production. Excess treated with dopamine agonists.

4. Posterior Lobe: The Storage Unit!

The posterior pituitary gland (neurohypophysis) doesn't *make* hormones; it stores and releases hormones produced by the hypothalamus: **Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH)** and **Oxytocin** (Oxytocin is covered in Chapter 54).

Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH) / Vasopressin

What ADH Does:

  • Promotes **water reabsorption from the renal tubules** (kidneys) to maintain fluid balance.

ADH Deficiency: Diabetes Insipidus (DI)

  • When ADH is deficient, large amounts of water are excreted by the kidneys, leading to **Diabetes Insipidus (DI)**.
  • Symptoms: Severe fluid volume deficit and electrolyte imbalances.
  • Causes: Head injury, brain tumors (trauma to hypothalamus/pituitary).
  • Treatment: **ADH replacement** with preparations like **vasopressin** and **desmopressin acetate** (intranasal or injection). Desmopressin also used for bleeding disorders (hemophilia A, von Willebrand).
  • **Desmopressin vs. Vasopressin:** Desmopressin doesn't induce ACTH release or increase serum cortisol like vasopressin.
  • **Contraindications for ADH:** Moderate to severe renal disease, hyponatremia (low sodium), or history of hyponatremia.
  • **Side Effects:** Hyponatremia (can lead to seizures!), headache, dyspepsia, diarrhea, nausea, vomiting. Hypotension and tachycardia due to hypovolemia.

ADH Excess: Syndrome of Inappropriate Antidiuretic Hormone (SIADH)

  • When ADH is excessive, too much water is retained, expanding intracellular and intravascular volume.
  • Most common cause: **Small cell carcinoma of the lung.** Other causes: Medications, other malignancies, stress (pain, infection, anxiety, trauma).
  • This leads to **Syndrome of Inappropriate Antidiuretic Hormone (SIADH)**, causing hyponatremia (low sodium) due to dilution and increased urinary sodium excretion (natriuresis).
  • Treatment: Fluid restrictions, hypertonic saline, or drugs like **demeclocycline, conivaptan, and tolvaptan.**
  • **Demeclocycline:** A tetracycline antibiotic that can induce nephrogenic DI (reverses water retention). Common side effect: photosensitivity. Dental discoloration possible.
  • **Vaptans (conivaptan, tolvaptan):** Vasopressin receptor antagonists. Increase serum sodium and free water clearance.
    • **Conivaptan:** IV. Contraindicated with corn allergy. Common complications: injection site reactions (phlebitis, pain, edema, pruritus) – administer in large veins, rotate sites. Other side effects: orthostatic hypotension, syncope, hypertension, atrial fibrillation, electrolyte imbalances.
    • **Tolvaptan:** Oral. **Black-box warnings** for alcoholism, hepatic disease, malnutrition (avoid in these patients). Common side effects: thirst, dry mouth, constipation, hyperglycemia, dizziness, weakness. Monitor fluids and electrolytes.
  • **Vaptans Contraindications:** Hypovolemia. Avoid fluid restriction during initial therapy to prevent sudden serum sodium increases.

Recap: Posterior Lobe Hormones (ADH)

  • Posterior lobe stores ADH (made by hypothalamus).
  • ADH: Water reabsorption by kidneys.
  • Deficiency (DI): Large water excretion, dehydration. Treat with vasopressin, desmopressin.
  • Excess (SIADH): Water retention, hyponatremia. Treat with demeclocycline, vaptans.
  • Monitor electrolytes closely for both.

5. Clinical Judgment: Nursing Process - Pituitary Hormones

Let's apply our nursing judgment to pituitary hormones! Our core concept here is: Hormonal Regulation (maintaining homeostasis via endocrine system hormones).

Recognize Cues [Assessment]: What do you notice?

  • Baseline Vital Signs: Obtain for future comparison. Report any abnormalities.
  • Fluid, Hydration, Electrolytes: Assess status and report abnormal findings (e.g., hyponatremia with ADH excess, fluid volume deficit with DI).
  • Infectious Process: Assess for infection, as corticotropin can suppress signs/symptoms.
  • Physical Growth: Note the patient's physical growth; compare child's growth with standards. Report findings (especially for GH therapy).

Analyze Cues & Prioritize Hypothesis [Patient Problems]: What's the biggest issue?

  • Hormonal imbalance related to the body’s inability to regulate and secrete appropriate hormones.
  • Need for health teaching related to complex drug regimen.
  • Potential for infection (especially with ACTH-related drugs).

Generate Solutions [Planning]: What's the goal?

  • The patient will be free from any pituitary disorder with the appropriate drug regimen.
  • The patient will understand the drug regimen.
  • The patient will remain free of infection.
  • The patient will maintain fluid and electrolyte balance.

Take Action [Nursing Interventions]: What do YOU do?

Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH) Specifics:

  • Monitor Vital Signs: Increased heart rate and decreased systolic pressure can indicate fluid volume loss due to decreased ADH.
  • Record Urinary Output: Increased output signals fluid loss from low ADH.
  • Obtain Daily Weight: A liter of fluid is approx. 2.2 lbs; monitor for fluid shifts.

Growth Hormone (GH) Specifics:

  • Monitor Blood Glucose & Electrolytes: Hyperglycemia can occur with high GH doses.

Repository Corticotropin (ACTH) Specifics:

  • Monitor Growth/Development: For children receiving corticotropin.
  • Observe Weight & Edema: Corticotropin can cause sodium and water retention.
  • Watch for Adverse Effects on Discontinuation: Taper dose, do not stop abruptly, to avoid adrenal hypofunction.
  • Check Lab Findings: Especially electrolytes and glucose. Corticotropin can increase blood glucose; electrolyte replacement may be needed.
  • Observe for Infection: Corticotropin suppresses immune function, increasing infection risk.

Patient Teaching: Empowering Your Patient!

General Advice:

  • Growth Hormone: Suggest patient/family monitor growth rate. Emphasize that GH is not for muscle building in healthy adults due to serious side effects (e.g., diabetes mellitus from abuse).
  • Corticotropin: Advise strict adherence to drug regimen. Abrupt discontinuation can cause hypofunction of the gland. Teach to decrease salt intake to avoid edema; potassium supplementation may be needed.
  • **Patient Safety: DO NOT CONFUSE...**
    • Calcitonin with calcitriol or calcium
    • Levothyroxine with liothyronine
    • Somatropin with somatostatin or sandostatin

Side Effects:

  • Growth Hormone: Advise athletes not to take GH. Inform diabetics to monitor blood glucose closely (insulin adjustment may be needed). Instruct patients to monitor weight for fluid retention.
  • Corticotropin: Teach patients to report side effects like muscle weakness, edema, petechiae, ecchymosis, decreased growth/wound healing, and menstrual irregularities.

Evaluate Outcomes [Evaluation]: Did it work?

  • Evaluate effectiveness of drug therapy (e.g., normal growth for GH, fluid/electrolyte balance for ADH, cortisol levels for ACTH).
  • Continue monitoring for side effects and infection.
  • Evaluate the patient’s knowledge of the medication regimen and compliance.

Recap: NP - Pituitary Hormones

  • **Assess:** VS, fluids/electrolytes, infection signs, physical growth.
  • **Problems:** Hormonal imbalance, teaching needs, infection risk.
  • **Goals:** Hormone balance, patient understanding, no infection.
  • **Intervene:** Monitor VS, I&O, weight. GH: monitor glucose. ACTH: taper, monitor labs (electrolytes, glucose), infection.
  • **Teach:** GH is not for body building, ACTH must be tapered, salt intake, fluid retention, report side effects.
  • **Evaluate:** Drug effectiveness, side effect monitoring, patient compliance/knowledge.

6. Thyroid Gland: Your Metabolism Maestro!

The thyroid glandAn endocrine gland located in the neck that produces hormones (T3, T4, calcitonin) regulating metabolism, growth, and development. is a super important regulator of many bodily functions. It produces three key hormones:

  • Triiodothyronine (T3)
  • Thyroxine (T4): Primarily for metabolism.
  • Calcitonin: Regulates serum calcium (to a lesser extent).
  • Most thyroid hormone is produced as T4, which then converts to T3 to act on target cells. **T3 is more potent than T4!**
  • **Iodide** (an inorganic form of iodine) is essential for T3 and T4 synthesis.
  • T3 and T4 are carried in the blood by proteins (thyroxine-binding globulin [TBG], albumin) which protect them. Only **unbound (free) T3 and T4** have biological action.

Negative Feedback & the HPT Axis:

The hypothalamus-pituitary-thyroid (HPT) axis controls thyroid hormone levels:

  • Hypothalamus releases Thyrotropin-Releasing Hormone (TRH).
  • TRH stimulates the pituitary to release Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone (TSH).
  • TSH stimulates the thyroid gland to make and release T3 and T4.
  • **Excess Free T3 & T4:** Inhibits the HPT axis, reducing TRH and TSH.
  • **Low T3 & T4:** Increases HPT axis function, boosting TRH and TSH.

This system ensures your thyroid is always producing the right amount of hormones.

Recap: Thyroid Gland Basics

  • Produces T3, T4 (metabolism), Calcitonin (calcium).
  • T3 is more potent than T4.
  • Iodide needed for synthesis.
  • Regulated by HPT axis via negative feedback.

7. Hypothyroidism: The Slowdown!

HypothyroidismA condition caused by decreased thyroid hormone secretion, leading to a slowed metabolic rate. Symptoms include lethargy, weight gain, cold intolerance, and slow pulse. is a condition where your thyroid gland doesn't secrete enough thyroid hormone, causing your body to slow down.

Causes:

  • **Primary (Most Frequent!):** Thyroid gland disorder (e.g., inflammation, radioiodine therapy, antithyroid drug excess, surgical removal).
    • Indicated by: **Decreased T4 and Elevated TSH levels.**
  • **Secondary:** Lack of TSH secretion (pituitary disorder).
  • **Tertiary:** Lack of TRH (hypothalamus disorder).

Myxedema (Severe Hypothyroidism in Adults):

  • Symptoms: Lethargy, apathy, memory impairment, emotional changes, slow speech, deep/coarse voice, edema (eyelids/face), dry skin, cold intolerance, slow pulse, constipation, weight gain, abnormal menses.

Hypothyroidism in Children:

  • **Congenital (Cretinism):** Can cause delayed physical and mental growth.
  • **Juvenile:** Onset before puberty.

Thyroid Drugs (Replacement Therapy):

  • Drugs containing T3 and T4 (alone or in combination) are used to treat hypothyroidism.
  • **Contraindicated in:** Patients with thyrotoxicosis, acute myocardial infarction (AMI), and adrenal insufficiency.
  • **Caution:** Drugs with hepatic enzyme-inducing properties (e.g., carbamazepine, hydantoins, rifabutin) should be used with caution as they speed up thyroid hormone breakdown.
  • Elevated serum calcium could also be related; **exogenous calcitonin** may be prescribed.

Levothyroxine Sodium: The Drug of Choice!

Levothyroxine sodiumA synthetic T4 hormone, it is the drug of choice for replacement therapy in hypothyroidism. It increases metabolic rate and promotes growth. is the most common drug for primary hypothyroidism.

Drug Class Dosage
Thyroid Hormone (Synthetic T4) Primary hypothyroidism: A: PO: Initially 1.6 mcg/kg/d; maint: 50–200 mcg/d; increase in increments of 12.5–25 mcg at 6- to 8-wk intervals.
Watch Out For! (Contraindications & Interactions)
Absolute: Thyrotoxicosis, myocardial infarction.
Caution: Adrenal insufficiency, cardiovascular disease (arrhythmias, hypertension, angina), diabetes mellitus, osteoporosis, hypopituitarism, dysphagia.
Drug:
  • Increased cardiac insufficiency with **sympathomimetics (epinephrine)**.
  • Increases effects of **anticoagulants, TCAs, vasopressors, decongestants, corticosteroids**.
  • Decreases effects of **antidiabetics (oral and insulin), digitalis products, beta blockers**.
  • Decreased absorption with **estrogens, antacids, cholestyramine, colestipol, sucralfate, simethicone**.
  • **Ketamines** can worsen hypertension/tachycardia.
  • **Hepatic inducers (carbamazepine, barbiturates, hydantoins)** increase metabolism, decreasing effect.
Food: Take on an **empty stomach at least 30–60 min before breakfast** and other medications. Do not mix with enteral or soy-based feedings. Certain foods/beverages (soy products, cruciferous vegetables, coffee) inhibit absorption. Herb: Celery seed may reduce thyroid hormones.

  • Liotrix: Mixture of levothyroxine (T4) and liothyronine (T3) in a 4:1 ratio. No significant advantage over levothyroxine alone.

Recap: Hypothyroidism

  • Decreased T3/T4. Primary = thyroid issue (low T4, high TSH).
  • Symptoms: Slowdown (lethargy, weight gain, cold intolerance). Myxedema is severe adult form. Cretinism in kids.
  • Treatment: Thyroid hormones (Levothyroxine is first choice).
  • Levothyroxine: Take on empty stomach. Many drug/food interactions. Side effects often due to incorrect dose (tachycardia, weight loss = too much).
  • Liothyronine (T3) is faster acting, for myxedema.

8. Hyperthyroidism: The Speed Up!

HyperthyroidismA condition caused by an excessive secretion of thyroid hormones (T3 and T4), leading to a sped-up metabolic rate. Symptoms include rapid pulse, weight loss, heat intolerance, and nervousness. is when you have too much circulating T3 and T4, usually from an overactive thyroid. It can range from mild symptoms to a severe thyroid storm (which can be fatal).

Graves Disease (Thyrotoxicosis):

  • Most common type of hyperthyroidism.
  • Symptoms: Rapid pulse (tachycardia), palpitations, excessive sweating (hyperhidrosis), heat intolerance, nervousness, irritability, bulging eyes (exophthalmos), and weight loss.

Treatment Options:

  1. **Surgical removal** of part of the thyroid (subtotal thyroidectomy).
  2. **Radioactive iodine therapy.**
  3. **Antithyroid drugs:** Inhibit thyroid hormone synthesis or release.

Important Note!

Both surgery and radioiodine therapy can lead to **permanent hypothyroidism**, meaning the patient will then need thyroid replacement therapy!

  • **Propranolol** (a beta blocker) can help control cardiac symptoms like palpitations and tachycardia that occur with hyperthyroidism.

Antithyroid Drugs: Slowing it Down!

These drugs reduce excessive thyroid hormone secretion.

Thiourea Derivatives (Thioamides): Drugs of Choice!

  • **How they work:** Interfere with thyroid hormone synthesis. They **do not destroy thyroid tissue**, but block its action.
  • **Examples:** Propylthiouracil (PTU) and Methimazole.
  • **Uses:** Control overactive thyroid (Graves disease, toxic nodular goiter), or before radioiodine treatment/thyroid surgery.
  • **Methimazole:** 10 times more potent than PTU, longer half-life. Preferred due to less severe side effects. Rapidly absorbed. Prolonged use can cause goiter (due to increased TSH trying to stimulate the blocked thyroid), so minimal doses are given.
  • **PTU:** Also inhibits peripheral conversion of T4 to T3.

Strong Iodide Preparations:

  • **Example:** Potassium iodide.
  • **Use:** To suppress thyroid function before subtotal thyroidectomy for Graves disease. Maximum effect in 10-15 days. Dilute and administer after meals, sip through a straw to avoid teeth discoloration.

Drug Interactions with Antithyroid Drugs:

  • **Oral anticoagulants (warfarin):** Increased anticoagulation effect.
  • **Insulin and oral antidiabetics:** Thyroid drugs decrease their effect.
  • **Digoxin and Lithium:** Increase the action of thyroid drugs.
  • **Phenytoin:** Increases serum T3 level.

Recap: Hyperthyroidism

  • Increased T3/T4. Graves disease (thyrotoxicosis) common.
  • Symptoms: Speed-up (tachycardia, weight loss, heat intolerance, exophthalmos).
  • Treatment: Surgery, radioiodine, or antithyroid drugs (thioamides like PTU, Methimazole).
  • Antithyroid drugs: Inhibit synthesis, don't destroy. Methimazole preferred.
  • Potassium iodide for pre-surgery suppression.
  • Many drug interactions (anticoagulants, antidiabetics).

9. Clinical Judgment: Nursing Process - Thyroid Hormones: Replacement and Antithyroid Drugs

Let's apply our nursing judgment to thyroid hormones! Our core concept is: Hormonal Regulation.

Recognize Cues [Assessment]: What do you notice?

  • Baseline Vital Signs & Weight: Obtain for future comparisons. Report abnormal results.
  • Serum T3, T4, TSH: Report any abnormal levels.
  • Drug & Herbal History: Crucial for potential interactions.
  • Signs & Symptoms of Thyroid Crisis (Storm): Look for tachycardia, cardiac dysrhythmias, fever, heart failure, flushed skin, apathy, confusion, behavioral changes, hypotension, and vascular collapse. (Can result from thyroidectomy, abrupt antithyroid withdrawal, excess thyroid hormone ingestion, or not giving antithyroid med before surgery).

Analyze Cues & Prioritize Hypothesis [Patient Problems]: What's the biggest issue?

  • Altered functional ability related to imbalanced thyroid hormone.
  • Need for health teaching related to complex drug regimen.
  • General decreased tissue perfusion related to imbalanced thyroid hormone.

Generate Solutions [Planning]: What's the goal?

  • The patient will report an improved activity level within 1 to 4 weeks of thyroid treatments.
  • The patient will report decreased signs and symptoms of hypothyroidism (within 2 to 4 weeks with prescribed thyroid drug replacement) and not experience side effects.
  • The patient will report decreased signs and symptoms of hyperthyroidism (in 1 to 3 weeks with prescribed antithyroid drug).

Take Action [Nursing Interventions]: What do YOU do?

  • Record Vital Signs: In hypothyroidism, temperature, HR, BP usually decrease. In hyperthyroidism, tachycardia and palpitations occur.
  • Monitor Weight: Weight gain in hypothyroidism is common.
  • Obtain Labs: Pregnancy test, CBC, CMP, LFT, ECG.
  • Report TSH, T3, T4: Periodically report these levels to the provider.

Patient Teaching: Empowering Your Patient!

General Advice:

  • Consistent Dosing: Encourage patients to take the drug at the same time each day in relation to meals. **Levothyroxine is taken 30–60 minutes before any food or medications.**
  • OTC Warnings: Teach patients to check warnings on OTC drug labels. Advise avoiding OTC drugs that caution against use by persons with heart or thyroid disease.
  • MedicAlert: Suggest carrying a MedicAlert ID for their condition and drug.
  • Food Interactions: Instruct that certain foods can interfere with absorption of thyroid hormones (e.g., soy products, cruciferous vegetables, iodized salt, shellfish, coffee).
  • Drug Adherence: Instruct patients to take drugs as prescribed; **abrupt changes may lead to increased thyroid dysfunction (e.g., thyroid crisis if stopping antithyroids, or hypothyroid relapse if stopping replacement).**
  • Family Guidance: Support family members in understanding the disease and drug therapy, providing extra time and written plans for non-English speakers.

Side Effects:

  • Report Hyperthyroidism Symptoms: Direct patients to report symptoms of hyperthyroidism (tachycardia, chest pain, palpitations, excess sweating) caused by drug accumulation or overdosing (for hypothyroidism treatment).
  • Pulse Monitoring: Demonstrate how to take their pulse. Instruct patients to monitor pulse rate and report increases or marked decreases.
  • Antithyroid Side Effects: Teach patients side effects like skin rash, hives, nausea, alopecia, hair pigment loss, petechiae or ecchymoses, and weakness.
  • Agranulocytosis Alert: Advise patients to contact their healthcare provider if **sore throat and fever** occur while taking antithyroid drugs. This could signal agranulocytosis (loss of white blood cells). CBC should be monitored.

Evaluate Outcomes [Evaluation]: Did it work?

  • Evaluate both effectiveness of the treatment and drug compliance.
  • Continue monitoring for side effects from drug accumulation or overdosing.
  • Evaluate the patient’s knowledge of the medication regimen.

Recap: NP - Thyroid Hormones

10. Parathyroid Glands: Calcium Regulators!

The tiny parathyroid glandsFour small glands located behind the thyroid gland that secrete parathyroid hormone (PTH) to regulate calcium and phosphate levels in the blood. are usually four small glands located behind your thyroid gland. They secrete **parathyroid hormone (PTH)**, also called **parathormone**, which plays a crucial role in regulating serum calcium levels:

  • Enhances calcium release from bones.
  • Enhances calcium reabsorption in renal tubules (kidneys).
  • Enhances calcium absorption in intestines by increasing active vitamin D production.

PTH vs. Calcitonin: The Calcium Tug-of-War!

  • A **decrease in serum calcium** (negative feedback) stimulates PTH release.
  • **Calcitonin** (from the thyroid gland) does the opposite: it **decreases serum calcium** by promoting bone osteoclast activity (breaking down bone to release calcium) and calcium excretion by kidneys/intestines.

PTH Deficiency (Hypoparathyroidism):

  • Causes: Damage to parathyroid glands (common), low serum magnesium (hypomagnesemia), vitamin D deficiency, renal impairment, diuretic therapy.
  • Results in: **Hypocalcemia** (low serum calcium).
  • Treatment: PTH analogues (e.g., to replace the missing hormone).

PTH Excess (Hyperparathyroidism):

  • Causes: Malignancies of parathyroid glands, ectopic PTH secretion (e.g., from lung cancer), hyperthyroidism, prolonged immobility (calcium lost from bone).
  • Treatment: Surgical removal (parathyroidectomy) is common for primary hyperparathyroidism. Also treated with calcitonin-salmon, calcimimetics, and bisphosphonates.

Recap: Parathyroid Glands

  • Secretes PTH, which increases serum calcium.
  • Opposite of calcitonin.
  • Hypoparathyroidism = hypocalcemia (treat with PTH analogues).
  • Hyperparathyroidism = hypercalcemia (treat with surgery, calcitonin-salmon, calcimimetics, bisphosphonates).

12. Hyperparathyroidism & Hypercalcemia: Too Much Calcium!

HyperparathyroidismA condition caused by excessive parathyroid hormone (PTH) secretion, leading to hypercalcemia (high blood calcium). results in **hypercalcemia** (high serum calcium). This can lead to bone loss, kidney stones, and other issues.

Common Causes:

  • Malignancies of the parathyroid glands.
  • Ectopic PTH hormone secretion (e.g., from lung cancer).
  • Hyperthyroidism.
  • Prolonged immobility (calcium lost from bone into blood).

Treatment Options:

  • **Partial or full parathyroidectomy:** Most common treatment for primary hyperparathyroidism.
  • **Calcitonin-salmon:** Decreases serum calcium by binding to osteoclast receptors (cells that break down bone). Prevents bone loss, increases bone density, alleviates pain. **Contraindicated in fish allergies.** Side effects: allergic reactions, GI symptoms, headache, hypocalcemia (can be severe, leading to tetany and seizures).
  • **Calcimimetics (e.g., Cinacalcet):** Mimic calcium in circulation, increasing the sensitivity of calcium-sensing receptors on parathyroid cells. This **reduces PTH secretion**, decreasing serum calcium and slowing bone disease. Used for hyperparathyroidism due to chronic renal disease and parathyroid cancer. **Contraindicated in hypocalcemia.**
  • **Bisphosphonates (e.g., alendronate):** Block osteoclast activity, inhibiting bone mineralization/resorption. Helps with osteoporosis caused by hyperparathyroidism. **Requires normal calcium before treatment.** Oral bisphosphonates are contraindicated if unable to sit/stand upright for 30 min after administration or with esophageal strictures. Side effects: GI upset, abdominal/musculoskeletal pain, hypotension, fever.

Calcium imbalances are discussed further in Chapter 12. Individual bisphosphonates are detailed in Chapter 52.

Recap: Hyperparathyroidism

  • Excess PTH, leading to hypercalcemia.
  • Causes: Gland tumors, cancers, immobility.
  • Treatment: Surgery, Calcitonin-salmon (fish allergy!), Calcimimetics (Cinacalcet), Bisphosphonates.
  • Monitor calcium levels closely.

14. Adrenal Glands: Your Stress & Salt Regulators!

Your paired adrenal glandsEndocrine glands located on top of each kidney, consisting of an outer cortex (producing steroid hormones) and an inner medulla (producing epinephrine and norepinephrine). sit on top of your kidneys. They have two main parts:

  • **Adrenal Medulla:** Secretes epinephrine and norepinephrine (catecholamines, involved in 'fight or flight').
  • **Adrenal Cortex:** Produces two main types of steroid hormones: glucocorticoids (cortisol) and mineralocorticoids (aldosterone), plus some androgens and estrogens.

Regulation (HPA Axis):

  • Steroids from the adrenal cortex are regulated by the hypothalamus-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis via negative feedback.
  • **Low Steroids (Hypocortisolism):** Increases CRF (hypothalamus) and ACTH (pituitary), stimulating adrenal glands to release more steroids.
  • **High Steroids (Hypercortisolism):** Inhibits the HPA axis, resulting in fewer steroids released.

Adrenal Imbalances:

  • **Adrenal Hyposecretion (Adrenal Insufficiency / Addison Disease):** A decrease in steroid secretion.
  • **Adrenal Hypersecretion (Cushing Syndrome):** An increase in steroid secretion.

Impact of Steroids!

Hypocortisolism can be very serious, even fatal, due to steroid influences on electrolytes, carbohydrate, protein, and fat metabolism.

Patient Safety! Do NOT Confuse...

  • Calcitonin with calcitriol or calcium
  • Levothyroxine with liothyronine
  • Somatropin with somatostatin or sandostatin

These sound similar but are very different drugs!

Recap: Adrenal Glands

  • Located on kidneys, outer cortex (steroids), inner medulla (epinephrine/norepinephrine).
  • Cortex produces glucocorticoids (cortisol) & mineralocorticoids (aldosterone).
  • Regulated by HPA axis (negative feedback).
  • Hyposecretion = Addison Disease. Hypersecretion = Cushing Syndrome.

15. Glucocorticoids: The Stress & Anti-Inflammation Hormones!

GlucocorticoidsA type of corticosteroid hormone produced by the adrenal cortex (e.g., cortisol) or made synthetically (e.g., prednisone). They have powerful anti-inflammatory, immunosuppressive, and metabolic effects. are the most potent natural cortisol produced by your body. They're heavily influenced by ACTH and affect various bodily functions, including inflammation, metabolism, growth, and blood cell activities.

Indications for Glucocorticoid Therapy:

  • Trauma, surgery, inflammation, emotional upsets, anxiety.
  • Inflammatory conditions: Autoimmune disorders (MS, rheumatoid arthritis, myasthenia gravis), ulcerative colitis, glomerulonephritis, shock, ocular/vascular inflammation, polyarteritis nodosa, hepatitis.
  • Allergic conditions: Asthma, drug reactions, contact dermatitis, anaphylaxis.
  • Debilitating conditions from malignancies.
  • Preventing organ rejection in transplant recipients.

Administration:

  • Many are synthetic (often called cortisone drugs).
  • Routes: Oral, parenteral (IM or IV), topical (creams, ointments), aerosol (inhaler).
  • IM route (seldom used) should be deep into the muscle; **subcutaneous administration is not recommended.**

Common Examples:

  • **Dexamethasone:** Potent, used for severe inflammatory responses (head trauma, allergic reactions).
  • **Prednisone:** Inexpensive, frequently prescribed.

16. Mineralocorticoids: Your Salt & Water Balancers!

MineralocorticoidsA type of corticosteroid hormone (e.g., aldosterone) produced by the adrenal cortex that primarily regulates sodium and potassium balance in the body, influencing fluid volume and blood pressure. primarily promote **sodium retention** and **potassium and hydrogen excretion** in the renal tubules (kidneys).

Aldosterone: The Primary Mineralocorticoid

  • Controlled by the Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS).
  • Maintains fluid balance by promoting sodium reabsorption (sodium attracts water, leading to water retention).
  • If hypovolemia (low circulating fluid) occurs, more aldosterone is secreted to restore fluid balance.
  • With sodium reabsorption, potassium is lost, which can lead to **hypokalemia** (low potassium).

Addison Disease Alert!

A severe decrease in mineralocorticoids can lead to **hypotension and vascular collapse**, as seen in Addison disease (adrenal hyposecretion).

Fludrocortisone: The Common Mineralocorticoid Drug

  • An oral mineralocorticoid, often given with a glucocorticoid.
  • Mimics aldosterone: facilitates sodium reabsorption, promotes hydrogen ion and potassium excretion.
  • Higher doses can inhibit endogenous hormone secretions from adrenal cortex and pituitary, causing a negative nitrogen balance; a **high-protein diet** may be indicated.
  • **Monitor serum potassium level** closely due to potassium excretion.
  • Adverse effects: Fluid imbalance, fluid overload, and hypertension (often signs of overdose). If these occur, **discontinue fludrocortisone and resume at lower doses.**
  • Hypokalemia may cause metabolic alkalosis, leading to GI symptoms (nausea, vomiting), orthostatic hypotension, cardiac rhythm changes, weakness, anorexia, and myalgia.

Recap: Mineralocorticoids

  • Promote sodium retention, potassium/hydrogen excretion.
  • Aldosterone: Primary mineralocorticoid, maintains fluid balance.
  • Fludrocortisone: Common drug, mimics aldosterone.
  • Watch for hypokalemia (potassium loss).
  • Overdose signs: Fluid overload, hypertension (reduce dose!).
  • Deficiency can lead to hypotension/vascular collapse (Addison Disease).

17. Clinical Judgment: Nursing Process - Adrenal Hormones: Corticosteroids

Let's apply our nursing judgment to adrenal hormones (corticosteroids)! Our core concept is: Hormonal Regulation.

Recognize Cues [Assessment]: What do you notice?

  • Baseline Vital Signs: Note for future comparisons.
  • Lab Results: Assess especially **serum electrolytes and blood glucose**. (Potassium typically decreases, glucose increases with prolonged corticosteroid use).
  • Weight & Urine Output: Obtain for future comparisons. Corticosteroids can cause fluid retention and weight gain.
  • Medical & Herbal History: Assess for glaucoma, cataracts, peptic ulcer, psychiatric problems, or diabetes mellitus, as glucocorticoids can worsen these.

Analyze Cues & Prioritize Hypothesis [Patient Problems]: What's the biggest issue?

  • Disrupted fluid and electrolyte balance related to treatment regimen.
  • Risk for decreased immunity.
  • Risk for decreased self-concept.
  • Risk for infection.

Generate Solutions [Planning]: What's the goal?

  • The patient will report decreased signs and symptoms of inflammation.
  • The patient will report minimal side effects from glucocorticoid therapy.
  • The patient will not develop new or worsening infection.
  • The patient will have increased knowledge of the medication regimen.
  • The patient will maintain fluid and electrolyte balance.

Take Action [Nursing Interventions]: What do YOU do?

  • Determine Vital Signs: Corticosteroids can increase blood pressure and sodium/water retention.
  • Administer Corticosteroids: Only as ordered. Routes: oral, IM (not in deltoid), IV, aerosol, topical (apply thin layers). Report rashes, infection, purpura.
  • Record Weight: Report a weight gain of 5 lb in 2 days (indicates water retention from hyperaldosteronism).
  • Monitor Laboratory Values: Especially serum electrolytes and blood glucose. Potassium may decrease (<3.5 mEq/L); sodium and glucose may increase.
  • Watch for Hypokalemia: Signs: nausea, vomiting, muscular weakness, abdominal distension, paralytic ileus, irregular heart rate.
  • Assess for Side Effects: Especially with therapy >10 days or high doses. **Do NOT abruptly stop cortisone preparations – adrenal crisis can result!** Taper the dose.
  • Monitor Older Adults: Watch for increased osteoporosis (corticosteroids promote calcium loss).

Patient Teaching: Empowering Your Patient!

General Advice:

  • Adherence & Tapering: Advise patients to take drugs as prescribed, and **caution them not to abruptly stop drugs.** When discontinued, the dose is tapered over 1 to 2 weeks.
  • Pregnancy: Do not take cortisone preparations (oral or topical) during pregnancy unless necessary and prescribed by provider, as drugs may be harmful to the fetus.
  • Herbal Interactions: Inform that certain herbal laxatives and diuretics may interact and increase severity of hypokalemia.
  • Infection Prevention: Teach patients to avoid large crowds and persons with respiratory infections, as corticosteroids suppress the immune system.
  • Inform Other Providers: Teach patients to inform other healthcare providers of all drugs taken.
  • Medical Alert ID: Encourage carrying a medical alert identification card, tag, or bracelet.
  • Administration: Counsel patients to take cortisone preparations **at mealtime or with food** to prevent gastric irritation.
  • Potassium-Rich Foods: Advise eating foods rich in potassium (fresh/dried fruits, vegetables, meats, nuts) as some corticosteroids promote potassium loss.
  • Family Education: Explain to the family that patient symptoms (e.g., mood changes) are due to the adrenal problem, not lack of interest. Explain that symptoms may progress if therapy is not followed.

Side Effects:

  • Report Cushing Syndrome Signs: Teach patients to report signs/symptoms of Cushing syndrome: moon face, puffy eyelids, feet edema, increased bruising, dizziness, bleeding, and menstrual irregularity.

Evaluate Outcomes [Evaluation]: Did it work?

  • Evaluate effectiveness of corticosteroid therapy. (If clinical manifestations haven't improved, a drug change may be needed.)
  • Continue monitoring for side effects, especially with high doses.
  • Evaluate the patient’s knowledge of the therapy.

Recap: NP - Adrenal Hormones (Corticosteroids)

  • **Assess:** VS, electrolytes/glucose, weight/I&O, medical/herbal history.
  • **Problems:** Fluid/electrolyte imbalance, infection risk, self-concept.
  • **Goals:** Decreased inflammation, minimal side effects, no new infection, patient knowledge.
  • **Intervene:** Monitor VS, administer correctly (IM deep, no subcut), record weight (5lb/2day gain = fluid retention), monitor labs (K+ decrease, Na/glucose increase), watch for hypokalemia, **taper off!**, monitor older adults for osteoporosis.

  • Serum potassium 4.1 mEq/L
  • Serum calcium 9.8 mg/dL
  • Serum albumin 3.8 g/dL
  • Serum total protein 6.9 g/dL
  • Thyroid-stimulating hormone 10 mIU/L

Choose the most likely options for the information missing from the statements below by selecting from the list of options provided.

The nurse suspects ____1____, and when reviewing T3 and T4 levels, the nurse would expect the levels to be ____2____. The nurse reports the assessment findings to the patient’s medical provider, anticipating an order of ____3____.

Option 2: Decreased, Increased, Normal

Rationale: Why these answers are correct!

The patient is exhibiting clinical manifestations of **primary hypothyroidism**, indicated by a TSH level of 10 mIU/L, which is elevated (normal TSH levels 0.5 to 5 mIU/L). TSH levels would be increased if there is not enough circulating thyroid hormone (T3 and T4). With primary hypothyroidism, total T3 and T4 levels would be **decreased**. The patient would be prescribed **levothyroxine** to replace the deficiency.

Secondary hypoadrenalism is a deficiency of the adrenal glands due to other causes, such as hypopituitarism. The adrenal glands do not produce their hormones because the pituitary gland does not release adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH). Hypoadrenalism can result in decreased serum sodium and increased serum potassium; the serum sodium and potassium levels are normal. Primary hypoparathyroidism is a deficiency of the parathyroid gland, which regulates serum calcium. The serum calcium levels were normal. Hyperpituitarism could cause an elevated TSH level; but its other hormones, such as ACTH, would cause an increased activity to its respective endocrine gland, such as the adrenals. However, serum electrolytes (sodium and potassium) are normal. If the adrenal glands were hyperactive, serum sodium would be elevated, and serum potassium would most likely be decreased. Fatigue decreases metabolism, which can cause weight gain. Secondary hyperadrenalism can result in increased serum sodium and decreased serum potassium; these were normal. Fatigue could be due to anemia; however, the patient’s hemoglobin and hematocrit were normal.
