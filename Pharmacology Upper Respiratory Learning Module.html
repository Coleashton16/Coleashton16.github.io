1. Introduction: Your Respiratory Superhighway!

Your respiratory tract is like a two-lane highway for air!

  • The upper respiratory tractConsists of the nares (nostrils), nasal cavity, pharynx (throat), and larynx (voice box). It's the entry point and filter for air. includes your nose, nasal cavity, pharynx, and larynx.
  • The lower respiratory tractConsists of the trachea (windpipe), bronchi, bronchioles, alveoli, and alveolar-capillary membrane. This is where the magic of gas exchange happens! includes your trachea, bronchi, bronchioles, and those tiny air sacs, the alveoli.

Visualize It!

FIG. 38.1: Basic structures of the respiratory tract. (Imagine a diagram here with the upper and lower tract highlighted!)

Don't mix these up! They're distinct terms:

  • Ventilation: This is simply the **movement of air** in and out of your lungs. Like air flowing through a pipe.
  • Respiration: This is the actual **gas exchange**! It has three phases:
    1. Ventilation: (Yes, the term appears twice!) Oxygen passes through airways.
    2. Perfusion: Blood flow at the tiny air sac (alveolar-capillary) level. It needs to match ventilation for good gas exchange.
    3. Diffusion: Oxygen moving from high concentration (in alveoli) to low (into blood), and CO2 moving from high concentration (in blood) to low (into alveoli) to be exhaled.

Focus Prompt!

Things like **mucosal edema (swelling), secretions, and bronchospasm** can increase resistance to airflow and decrease how well gases exchange!

Recap: Respiratory Basics

  • Respiratory tract: Upper (nose, throat) & Lower (windpipe, lungs).
  • Ventilation = air movement. Respiration = gas exchange (ventilation, perfusion, diffusion).
  • Swelling/mucus/spasm block air flow and gas exchange.

2. Common Cold, Acute Rhinitis, and Allergic Rhinitis: What's Bugging You?

Upper respiratory infections (URIs)Infections affecting the nose, throat, and larynx, including the common cold, acute rhinitis, sinusitis, and pharyngitis. are super common! The average adult gets 2-4 colds a year, and kids get even more (4-12!). While not life-threatening, they're annoying and costly.

The Common Cold:

  • Cause: Primarily the rhinovirus.
  • Affects: Mainly the nose and throat (nasopharyngeal tract).
  • Symptoms: Runny nose (rhinorrhea), stuffy nose (nasal congestion), cough, and increased snot.
  • Contagion: Most contagious **1-4 days before symptoms** and during the **first 3 days of the cold.**
  • Transmission: More often from touching contaminated surfaces and then your nose/mouth than from sneezes!

Acute Rhinitis:

  • What it is: Acute inflammation of the nose's mucous membranes.
  • Usually comes with: The common cold.

Allergic Rhinitis (Hay Fever):

  • What it is: Inflammation of the nose's mucous membranes caused by pollen or allergens like animal dander.
  • Not the same as: The common cold!

Did you know?

If a bacterial infection tags along with a cold, your snot might turn thick, yellow, or yellow-green due to white blood cells fighting the infection. That's when antibiotics might come in handy!

Drugs to Manage Cold Symptoms: Your Go-To Categories!

  • Antihistamines (H1 blockers)
  • Decongestants (sympathomimetic amines)
  • Antitussives (cough suppressants)
  • Expectorants (mucus loosers)

Recap: Cold & Rhinitis

  • Common cold = rhinovirus, affects nose/throat.
  • Acute rhinitis = nose inflammation, usually with cold.
  • Allergic rhinitis (hay fever) = from allergens (pollen, dander).
  • Drugs: Antihistamines, decongestants, antitussives, expectorants.

3. Antihistamines: Blocking the Sneeze & Itch!

AntihistaminesAlso known as H1 blockers or H1 antagonists, these drugs compete with histamine at receptor sites to prevent allergic reactions and reduce symptoms like sneezing, runny nose, and itching. are like bouncers at a club, preventing histamine from getting to its receptor sites and causing trouble! There are two types of histamine receptors:

  • H1 receptor: When stimulated, it causes **constriction of smooth muscles**, including those in your nasal cavity. This leads to sneezing, runny nose, and itching.
  • H2 receptor: When stimulated, it increases stomach acid (related to ulcers). Don't mix these up!

Antihistamines work by blocking the H1 receptor, which **decreases nasopharyngeal secretions** and helps with the itch and tickle that cause sneezing. They're great for seasonal allergies but **NOT for emergencies like anaphylaxis** (they're not potent enough for that).

First-Generation Antihistamines: The Sleepy Ones!

  • Characteristics: These often cause **drowsiness, dry mouth, and other anticholinergic symptoms** (like blurred vision or urinary retention).
  • Common in: Many OTC cold remedies.
  • Caution: Don't drive or operate heavy machinery if you're taking these!
  • Benefit: Their anticholinergic properties (drying effect) are actually helpful for runny noses and decreasing nasal itching/tickling.

Second-Generation Antihistamines: The Non-Drowsy Crew!

  • Characteristics: Often called "nonsedating" because they cause **little to no drowsiness** and fewer anticholinergic symptoms.
  • Examples: Cetirizine, fexofenadine, azelastine, desloratadine, and loratadine.
  • Administration: Many are administered by nasal spray.
  • Caution: Even though they're less sedating, it's generally advised to avoid mixing them with large amounts of alcohol or other CNS depressants.

5. Nasal and Systemic Decongestants: Unstuff Your Nose!

Nasal congestion happens when nasal blood vessels swell up due to infection, inflammation, or allergies, leading to fluid leaking into tissues and a stuffy nose.

Nasal decongestants (sympathomimetic amines)Drugs that stimulate alpha-adrenergic receptors in nasal capillaries, causing vasoconstriction, shrinking mucous membranes, and reducing fluid secretion to relieve nasal congestion. are like tiny traffic cops for your nose blood vessels! They stimulate alpha-adrenergic receptors, causing those capillaries in your nose to constrict (vasoconstriction). This shrinks the mucous membranes and reduces snot (runny nose).

Ways to Take Them:

  • Nasal spray or drops (topical)
  • Tablet, capsule, or liquid (systemic)

Topical (Nasal Spray/Drops) Dangers:

Focus Prompt!

Frequent use (even as little as 3 days!) of nasal sprays/drops can lead to **tolerance and rebound nasal congestion!** This means instead of constricting, your nasal membranes rebound and dilate, making your stuffy nose even worse. Emphasize limiting their use!

Side Effects Reminder:

Topical decongestants have low side effects, but some can cause nervousness or restlessness. These usually get better as your body adjusts.

Systemic Decongestants (Oral):

  • Examples: Tetrahydrozoline, phenylephrine, oxymetazoline, and pseudoephedrine.
  • Primary Use: Allergic rhinitis (hay fever) and acute coryza (profuse runny nose).
  • Advantage: Relieve congestion for longer periods than nasal sprays.
  • Caution: **Phenylpropanolamine** was removed from OTCs due to stroke, hypertension, and cardiac risks.
  • Pseudoephedrine: Often combined with antihistamines, analgesics, or antitussives. Its sales are strictly controlled due to its use in illicit drug manufacturing.

General Decongestant Warnings:

  • As with any alpha-adrenergic drug, they can **increase blood pressure and blood glucose levels!**
  • Contraindicated or used with extreme caution in patients with: Hypertension, cardiac disease, hyperthyroidism, and diabetes mellitus.

Drug Interactions with Decongestants:

  • Pseudoephedrine: May decrease the effect of beta blockers.
  • MAOIs: Taking decongestants with MAOIs can increase the risk of hypertension or cardiac dysrhythmias.
  • Caffeine: Large amounts of caffeine (coffee, tea) can increase restlessness and palpitations.

Recap: Decongestants

  • Stimulate alpha-adrenergic receptors, causing vasoconstriction (shrinking nasal membranes).
  • **Topical:** Faster, fewer systemic side effects, but **risk of rebound congestion if overused (>3 days).**
  • **Systemic:** Longer relief.
  • **Warnings:** Can increase BP and blood glucose. Contraindicated/caution in hypertension, heart disease, hyperthyroidism, diabetes. Many drug interactions.

6. Clinical Judgment: Nursing Process - Antihistamine: Diphenhydramine

Let's use our nursing judgment with **Diphenhydramine**! Remember our core concept: **Gas Exchange** (getting enough oxygen in and carbon dioxide out).

Recognize Cues [Assessment]: What do you notice?

  • Vital Signs: Determine baseline vital signs.
  • Drug History: Obtain a full drug history to spot potential drug-drug interactions.
  • Urinary Function: Assess for signs of urinary issues (retention, dysuria, altered frequency) – anticholinergic effects can worsen these.
  • CBC: Note complete blood count (CBC) during drug therapy.
  • Cardiac & Respiratory Status: Assess both.
  • Environmental Exposures: Get a history of exposures (drugs, recent foods, stress).

Analyze Cues & Prioritize Hypothesis [Patient Problems]: What's the biggest issue?

  • Airway obstruction (from congestion/secretions).
  • Decreased gas exchange (less O2 in, less CO2 out).
  • Hypoxemia (low oxygen in blood).
  • Discomfort (from symptoms).

Generate Solutions [Planning]: What's the goal?

  • Patient will have decreased nasal congestion, mucosal secretions, and cough.
  • Patient will sleep 6 to 8 hours per night (relevant if using for insomnia/sedation).

Take Action [Nursing Interventions]: What do YOU do?

  • Oral Admin: Give oral Diphenhydramine **with food** to decrease stomach upset.
  • IM Admin: Administer IM injections in a large muscle. **Avoid subcutaneous injection.**

Patient Teaching: Empowering Your Patient!

General Advice:

  • Driving/Dangerous Activities: Warn patients to **avoid driving a motor vehicle** and performing other dangerous activities if drowsiness occurs, or until they're stable on the drug.
  • CNS Depressants: Advise patients to **avoid alcohol and other central nervous system (CNS) depressants** (opioids, hypnotics, barbiturates).
  • Compliance & Reporting: Encourage patients to take drugs as prescribed. Tell them to **notify a healthcare provider if confusion or hypotension occurs.**
  • Motion Sickness Prophylaxis: If taking for motion sickness, teach them to take the drug **at least 30 minutes before** the event, and also before meals and at bedtime during the event.
  • Breastfeeding: Inform breastfeeding mothers that small amounts of the drug pass into breast milk, and children are more susceptible to side effects (excitement, irritability). **Breastfeeding is not recommended** while using these drugs.

Side Effects:

  • Children: Advise family members/parents that children are more sensitive; **nightmares, nervousness, and irritability** are more likely.
  • Older Adults: Inform older adults that they are more sensitive and more likely to experience **confusion, difficult/painful urination, dizziness, drowsiness, feeling faint, and dryness of mouth/nose/throat.**
  • Dry Mouth Relief: Suggest using sugarless candy or gum, ice chips, or a saliva substitute for temporary relief of dry mouth.

Evaluate Outcomes [Evaluation]: Did it work?

  • Evaluate effectiveness of the drug in relieving allergic symptoms or as a sleep aid.
  • Confirm patient is free from excessive drowsiness or adverse effects.

Recap: NP - Diphenhydramine

  • **Assess:** VS, drug history, urinary function, CBC, cardiac/resp. status.
  • **Problems:** Airway obstruction, decreased gas exchange, discomfort.
  • **Goals:** Decreased congestion/cough, improved sleep.
  • **Intervene:** Give oral with food, IM in large muscle.
  • **Teach:** Avoid driving/CNS depressants, report confusion/hypotension, take for motion sickness 30 min before, breastfeeding not recommended. Watch for side effects in children/older adults. Manage dry mouth.
  • **Evaluate:** Symptom relief, adequate sleep, no significant side effects.

7. Intranasal Glucocorticoids: Anti-Inflammatory Nasal Sprays!

Intranasal glucocorticoids (steroids)Nasal sprays containing corticosteroids that reduce inflammation in the nasal passages, effectively treating symptoms of allergic rhinitis like rhinorrhea, sneezing, and congestion. are super effective for allergic rhinitis because they have a powerful **anti-inflammatory action**. They decrease symptoms like runny nose (rhinorrhea), sneezing, and congestion.

Examples of Intranasal Steroids:

  • Beclomethasone
  • Budesonide
  • Flunisolide
  • Fluticasone
  • Mometasone furoate
  • Triamcinolone

Team Up!

These can be used alone or combined with an H1 antihistamine for extra power!

Important Usage Tips:

  • Direct the spray **away from the nasal septum** (the wall between your nostrils) and sniff gently.
  • With continuous use, **dryness of the nasal mucosa/lining may occur.**
  • Common side effects include **headache, nasal irritation, pharyngitis, fatigue, insomnia, and candidiasis** (yeast infection in the nose/throat).
  • They undergo rapid deactivation after absorption, meaning most of the effect is localized to the nose.
  • Most allergic rhinitis is seasonal, so these drugs are usually for **short-term use**, unless your doctor says otherwise.

Recap: Intranasal Glucocorticoids

  • Anti-inflammatory action, great for allergic rhinitis symptoms (runny nose, sneezing, congestion).
  • Examples: Beclomethasone, Fluticasone.
  • Spray away from septum, sniff gently.
  • Side effects: Dry nasal mucosa, irritation, headache, candidiasis.
  • Usually for short-term/seasonal use.

8. Antitussives: Quieting That Annoying Cough!

AntitussivesMedications that suppress the cough reflex by acting on the cough control center in the medulla of the brain. Used for nonproductive, irritating coughs. are drugs that work on the **cough control center in your brain (medulla)** to suppress your cough reflex. Coughing is normally a good thing (it clears your airways!), but if it's nonproductive (not bringing anything up) and irritating, an antitussive can help. Hard candy can also help with a tickly throat!

The Three Types:

  1. Nonopioid (e.g., Dextromethorphan)
  2. Opioid (e.g., Codeine)
  3. Combination preparations (often with other cold meds)

11. Sinusitis: When Your Sinuses Get Mad!

SinusitisInflammation of the mucous membranes lining one or more of the sinuses (maxillary, frontal, ethmoid, or sphenoid). Can cause facial pain, pressure, and congestion. is an inflammation of the mucous membranes in one or more of your sinuses (like maxillary, frontal, ethmoid, or sphenoid). Think of it as a stuffy, painful head!

What Helps?

  • A **systemic or nasal decongestant** can help reduce swelling and open things up.
  • **Acetaminophen (Tylenol), fluids, and rest** are also helpful for symptom relief.
  • If it's acute or severe, an **antibiotic** might be prescribed if a bacterial infection is suspected.

Recap: Sinusitis

  • Inflammation of sinus membranes.
  • Treatment: Decongestants, acetaminophen, fluids, rest.
  • Antibiotics if bacterial.

12. Acute Pharyngitis: The Dreaded Sore Throat!

Acute pharyngitisInflammation of the throat (pharynx), commonly known as a "sore throat." It can be caused by viruses, bacteria (like strep), or other irritants. is simply the medical term for **inflammation of the throat, or a "sore throat."** It can strike alone or tag along with a common cold or sinusitis.

Causes:

  • **Virus** (most common)
  • **Beta-hemolytic streptococci** ("strep throat")
  • Other bacteria

Symptoms:

  • Elevated temperature (fever)
  • Cough

Diagnosis & Treatment:

  • Always get a **throat culture** to rule out "strep throat."
  • If the culture is positive for **beta-hemolytic streptococci**, a **10-day course of antibiotics** is usually prescribed.
  • For all types of sore throats, **saline gargles, lozenges, and increased fluid intake** are usually helpful.
  • Acetaminophen can help lower fever.
  • **Important:** Antibiotics **do NOT work for viral pharyngitis!**

Recap: Acute Pharyngitis

  • Inflammation of the throat (sore throat).
  • Caused by viruses or bacteria (like strep).
  • Throat culture for strep.
  • Bacterial = 10-day antibiotics.
  • General care: Saline gargles, lozenges, fluids, acetaminophen.
  • Antibiotics **don't work for viral**.

13. Clinical Judgment: Nursing Process - Nasal Decongestant: Oxymetazoline

Let's use our nursing judgment with **Oxymetazoline** (a nasal decongestant)! Our core concept is still: **Gas Exchange**.

Recognize Cues [Assessment]: What do you notice?

  • History of Hypertension: Crucial to determine if the patient has a history of high blood pressure, especially with decongestants.
  • Baseline Vital Signs: Note these for comparison. An elevated temperature (99°F-101°F / 37.2°C-38.3°C) might mean a viral infection.
  • Drug History: Get a full drug history to identify potential drug-drug interactions (e.g., Dextromethorphan with MAOIs, narcotics, etc., can increase toxicity).
  • Cardiac & Respiratory Status: Assess both.

Analyze Cues & Prioritize Hypothesis [Patient Problems]: What's the biggest issue?

  • Airway obstruction (due to nasal congestion).
  • Decreased gas exchange.
  • Hypoxemia.
  • Discomfort.
  • Fatigue.

Generate Solutions [Planning]: What's the goal?

  • Patient’s cough will be eliminated or diminished.
  • Patient will be free from a secondary bacterial infection.
  • Patient will experience reduced nasal congestion.

Take Action [Nursing Interventions]: What do YOU do?

  • Monitor Vital Signs: Blood pressure can go up with decongestants; dysrhythmias can also occur. Watch closely!
  • Observe Bronchial Secretions: Yellow or green mucus suggests a bacterial infection, which might need antibiotics.
  • Codeine Warning: If codeine is part of the treatment, warn patients that it can lead to tolerance and physical dependence.

Patient Teaching: Empowering Your Patient!

General Advice:

  • Dextromethorphan + MAOIs: Tell patients that hypotension (low BP) and hyperpyrexia (very high fever) can occur if dextromethorphan is taken with MAOIs.
  • Nasal Spray Use: Teach patients proper technique for nasal sprays and "puff" or squeeze products.
  • 🚫 Rebound Congestion: **CAUTION PATIENTS NOT TO USE NASAL SPRAYS FOR MORE THAN 3 DAYS** to prevent rebound nasal congestion (where the stuffiness actually gets worse!).
  • OTC Labels: Advise patients to always **read labels on over-the-counter (OTC) drugs** and to check with a healthcare provider before taking any cold remedies. This is super important if they're taking other drugs or have health issues like hypertension or hyperthyroidism. Also, remind them that acetaminophen might be hidden in many products, risking an overdose.
  • Antibiotics: Inform patients that **antibiotics are NOT helpful for common cold viruses**. They're only for secondary bacterial infections.
  • Older Adults/Chronic Conditions: Encourage older patients or those with heart disease, asthma, emphysema, diabetes, or hypertension to **contact their healthcare provider** before choosing any cold/flu drugs, including OTCs.
  • Drowsiness: Tell patients **not to drive** during initial use of a cold remedy containing an antihistamine due to drowsiness.
  • Fluid Intake: Encourage patients to **maintain adequate fluid intake** (at least 8 glasses/day). Fluids help thin bronchial secretions for easier coughing.
  • Bedtime Dosing: Teach patients **not to take a cold remedy before or at bedtime** if it contains a decongestant, as it can cause insomnia.
  • Rest: Encourage adequate rest.
  • Infection Control: Inform patients that cold and flu viruses spread easily by hand-to-hand contact or touching contaminated surfaces. Emphasize hand washing!
  • Environmental Irritants: Advise patients to avoid pollutants, fumes, smoking, and dust to reduce irritating coughs.
  • Cough Technique: Teach patients to perform **three effective coughs before bedtime** to promote uninterrupted sleep.
  • Child Safety: Direct patients and parents to **store drugs out of reach of children** and request childproof caps.
  • When to Call Provider: Advise patients to **contact a healthcare provider if cough persists for more than 1 week** or is accompanied by chest pain, fever, or headache.

Self-Administration:

  • Teach patients to self-administer medications like nose drops and inhalants correctly.
  • Encourage effective coughing: take deep breaths before coughing, and be in an upright position.

Evaluate Outcomes [Evaluation]: Did it work?

  • Evaluate effectiveness of drug therapy.
  • Determine that the patient is free from nonproductive cough, has adequate fluid intake and rest, and is afebrile (without fever).
  • Confirm decreased nasal congestion.

Recap: NP - Oxymetazoline (Nasal Decongestant)

  • **Assess:** History (hypertension!), VS, drug interactions, cardiac/resp. status.
  • **Problems:** Airway obstruction, decreased gas exchange, discomfort.
  • **Goals:** Reduced cough/congestion, no secondary infection.
  • **Intervene:** Monitor VS (BP, dysrhythmias), check mucus color (bacterial?), warn about codeine dependence.
  • **Teach:** DON'T overuse nasal sprays (>3 days rebound!), read OTC labels, no antibiotics for viral, adequate fluids/rest, avoid bedtime decongestants, hand hygiene, avoid irritants, effective coughs, child safety, when to call provider.
  • **Evaluate:** Symptom relief, adequate hydration/rest, no fever/secondary infection.

14. NCLEX Next Gen! Critical Thinking Case Study

Cognitive Skill: Prioritize hypothesis.

A 35-year-old patient has allergic rhinitis. Her prescriptions include loratadine 5 mg/day and fluticasone, two nasal inhalations per day. Previously, she had taken OTC drugs and asked if she should continue to take the OTC drug with her prescriptions. She has never used a nasal inhaler before.

1. What additional information is needed from the patient concerning her health problem?

2. What is your response to the patient concerning the use of OTC drugs with her prescription drugs?

3. How would you instruct the patient to use a nasal inhaler? Explain your answer.

4. What are the similarities and differences between loratadine and diphenhydramine? Could one of these antihistamines be more effective than the other? Explain your answer.

5. What could you suggest to decrease allergens such as dust mites in the home?

Rationale: Why these answers are correct!

  1. **Additional Information Needed:** The nurse should ask about the specific OTC drugs she was taking (names, dosages, frequency), as well as any underlying health conditions (e.g., hypertension, cardiac disease, diabetes, hyperthyroidism) that might interact with decongestants or other ingredients in OTC cold remedies. Also, inquire about her specific allergic triggers, severity of symptoms, and how long she has had allergic rhinitis.
  2. **Response to OTC Use:** The nurse should advise the patient **NOT to continue taking OTC drugs without first checking with her healthcare provider or pharmacist.** Many OTC cold remedies contain decongestants and/or antihistamines that could interact with her prescribed loratadine and fluticasone. Combining them could lead to excessive sedation, increased anticholinergic side effects (if a first-gen antihistamine), or cardiovascular issues (if a decongestant is present). It's crucial to avoid potential overdoses or adverse drug interactions.
  3. **Nasal Inhaler Instruction:**
    • **Preparation:** Instruct her to gently blow her nose first to clear nasal passages.
    • **Shake:** Shake the inhaler well before each use.
    • **Positioning:** Tilt her head slightly forward.
    • **Aim:** Place the tip of the nozzle into one nostril, aiming the spray **away from the nasal septum** (the center divider) towards the outer wall of the nostril.
    • **Spray & Sniff:** As she sprays, she should gently sniff inward.
    • **Repeat:** Repeat for the other nostril if prescribed.
    • **Avoid Blowing:** Advise her not to blow her nose immediately after spraying.
    • **Cleaning:** Explain how to clean the nozzle regularly according to the manufacturer's instructions.
    • **Consistency:** Emphasize that fluticasone is a preventative medication and needs to be used consistently every day for full effectiveness, not just when symptoms are severe.
  4. **Loratadine vs. Diphenhydramine:**
    • **Similarities:** Both are antihistamines (H1 blockers) used to treat allergic symptoms like rhinitis, sneezing, and itching. They both block histamine's effects at the H1 receptor.
    • **Differences:** **Loratadine is a second-generation antihistamine**, meaning it is generally **non-sedating** and has fewer anticholinergic side effects (like dry mouth, blurred vision, urinary retention) because it does not cross the blood-brain barrier as readily. **Diphenhydramine is a first-generation antihistamine**, which is known for causing **significant drowsiness** and having more pronounced anticholinergic effects.
    • **Effectiveness:** Loratadine might be considered "more effective" in terms of patient compliance and quality of life due to its non-sedating profile, making it suitable for daytime use without impairing daily activities. Diphenhydramine can be very effective for acute symptoms, especially when sedation is desired (e.g., for nighttime relief of itching/congestion). Neither is inherently "more effective" at blocking histamine, but their side effect profiles make them more or less appropriate for different patient needs and daily routines. Given her current prescription, her doctor likely chose loratadine for its non-drowsy properties.
  5. **Decreasing Allergens:**
    • Use allergen-proof covers for mattresses and pillows.
    • Wash bedding weekly in hot water (at least 130°F / 54°C).
    • Vacuum regularly with a HEPA filter vacuum cleaner.
    • Remove carpets if possible, especially in the bedroom.
    • Keep humidity levels low (below 50%) using a dehumidifier.
    • Avoid upholstered furniture or replace it with leather/vinyl.
    • Clean regularly to remove dust, mold, and pet dander.
    • Consider air purifiers with HEPA filters.
    • Keep windows closed during high pollen seasons.
    • Bathe pets regularly if pet dander is a trigger.

Reference: Pharmacology, McCuistion, et al. Pharmacology, Chapter 38 (relevant sections on Antihistamines, Intranasal Glucocorticoids, and patient teaching for cold remedies).

Recap: Case Study Takeaways

  • Always assess for other OTC meds and underlying health conditions.
  • Advise against combining OTCs with prescriptions without provider approval.
  • Proper nasal inhaler technique (aim away from septum, sniff gently, consistent use).
  • Loratadine is non-drowsy (2nd gen), Diphenhydramine is drowsy (1st gen). Choose based on patient needs.
  • Dust mite control: covers, hot washes, vacuuming, humidity control.
