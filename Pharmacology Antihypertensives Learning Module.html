1. Hypertension: The Silent Killer

HypertensionPersistently elevated blood pressure (BP) which is the most common condition leading to myocardial infarction (MI), stroke, renal failure, and death. is the most common condition leading to serious cardiovascular events like MI, stroke, renal failure, and death.

Essential Hypertension:

  • Affects 95% of persons with high blood pressure.
  • Exact cause is unknown.
  • Contributing factors: hyperlipidemia, African American race, diabetes, aging, stress, excessive alcohol, smoking, obesity (contributes to 30% of cases), family history.

Secondary Hypertension:

  • Accounts for 5% of hypertension cases.
  • Related to identifiable causes like renal and endocrine disorders.

Guidelines for Hypertension (JNC 8, 2017):

BP data should be based on an average of more than two readings on more than two occasions.

  • Normal BP: Less than 120/80 mm Hg
  • Elevated BP: Systolic BP (SBP) 120-129 AND Diastolic BP (DBP) less than 80 mm Hg.
  • Stage 1 Hypertension: 130/80 to 139/89 mm Hg.
  • Stage 2 Hypertension: 140/90 mm Hg or greater.

SBP is considered more important than DBP as a cardiovascular disease (CVD) risk factor.

Only approximately 48% of hypertensive individuals have their condition under control.

Recap: Hypertension Basics

  • Leading cause of MI, stroke, renal failure, death.
  • Essential HTN (95%): Unknown cause, linked to lifestyle/genetics.
  • Secondary HTN (5%): Due to other medical conditions.
  • BP Guidelines: Normal (<120/80), Elevated (120-129/<80), Stage 1 (130-139/80-89), Stage 2 (≥140/90).
  • Less than half of hypertensives have controlled BP.

2. Selected Regulators of Blood Pressure: The Body's Control Systems

Your body has intricate systems to regulate and maintain blood pressure (BP).

Kidneys: Fluid and Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS)

  • Fluid Volume: Kidneys control sodium and water elimination and retention, directly affecting cardiac output and systemic arterial BP.
  • RAAS: When BP decreases, renin is released from renal cells.
    • Renin stimulates Angiotensin II production (a potent vasoconstrictor).
    • Angiotensin II causes Aldosterone release (promotes sodium and water retention).
    • Result: Increased fluid volume, elevated BP.
FIGURE 42.1 The Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS) (Not displayed here).

Neural and Hormonal Regulators:

  • Baroreceptors: Located in the aorta and carotid sinus, they sense changes in BP.
  • Vasomotor Center: In the medulla, controls vasoconstriction/vasodilation.
  • Catecholamines (Norepinephrine, Epinephrine): Released by sympathetic nerves and adrenal medulla, increase BP via vasoconstriction.
  • Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH): From posterior pituitary, stimulates kidneys to conserve water during fluid deficit, increasing BP. Inhibited during fluid overload.

Recap: BP Regulators

  • Kidneys: Regulate fluid volume, manage RAAS (Renin -> Angiotensin II -> Aldosterone -> Na+/H2O retention -> increased BP).
  • Baroreceptors/Vasomotor Center: Neural control.
  • Catecholamines: Increase BP via vasoconstriction.
  • ADH: Conserves water, raises BP during fluid deficit.

3. Physiologic Risk Factors & Hypertension

Beyond genetics and race, certain lifestyle and physiological factors significantly contribute to hypertension:

  • Diet: High intake of saturated fat and simple carbohydrates can increase BP.
  • Alcohol: Increases renin secretions, leading to more angiotensin II and higher BP.
  • Obesity: Affects sympathetic and cardiovascular systems by increasing cardiac output, stroke volume, and left ventricular filling. Two-thirds of hypertensive persons are obese.

Even mild to moderate interventions can help:

  • Weight loss: Can significantly decrease hypertension.
  • Sodium restriction: Can lower BP.

Recap: Risk Factors

  • Diet: High saturated fat/simple carbs.
  • Alcohol: Increases renin.
  • Obesity: Increases cardiac workload.
  • Weight loss & sodium restriction can help.

4. Cultural Responses to Antihypertensive Agents

It's crucial for nurses to understand that response to antihypertensive drugs can vary significantly across different cultural and racial groups.

  • African Americans:
    • More likely to develop hypertension at an earlier age than Caucasians.
    • Have a higher mortality rate from hypertension than the Caucasian population.
    • Less effective response to beta-adrenergic blockers (beta blockers) and ACE inhibitors *unless* combined with a diuretic.
    • Often have low-renin hypertension, which explains their poorer response to beta blockers and ACE inhibitors.
    • Respond well to alpha1 blockers, calcium channel blockers (CCBs), and diuretics as initial monotherapy.
  • Caucasian Patients:
    • Usually have high-renin hypertension.
    • Generally respond well to all antihypertensive agents.
  • Asian Americans:
    • Approximately twice as sensitive as Caucasians to beta blockers and other antihypertensives.
    • Often require a reduction in antihypertensive dosing.
  • Native Americans:
    • Have a reduced or lower response to beta blockers compared with Caucasians.

Ongoing monitoring of BP and drug dosing is essential for patients in all cultural groups to ensure optimal and safe treatment.

Recap: Cultural Responses

  • African Americans: Higher prevalence/mortality. Less effective response to beta blockers/ACEIs (unless with diuretic); respond well to alpha1 blockers, CCBs, diuretics. Often low-renin.
  • Caucasians: Usually high-renin, respond well to all.
  • Asian Americans: Twice as sensitive to beta blockers/other antihypertensives; often need lower doses.
  • Native Americans: Reduced response to beta blockers.
  • Individualized monitoring is key for all groups.

5. Hypertension in Older Adults

Hypertension is highly prevalent in older adults:

  • Approximately 70 million American adults have hypertension, representing 46% of the adult population.
  • Of individuals over 60 years of age, 65% (or two in three people) have developed hypertension.
  • Only about 48% of hypertensive individuals have their condition under control.
  • Males aged 18-49 typically have a higher prevalence of hypertension than females in that age range.

Antihypertensive therapy can significantly reduce cardiovascular disorders: 34% decrease in stroke and 19% decrease in coronary heart disease.

Orthostatic Hypotension: A Key Concern in Older Adults

One troublesome side effect of antihypertensive agents in older adults, especially frail or institutionalized persons, is orthostatic (postural) hypotension. This is a sudden drop in BP upon standing, causing dizziness due to blood pooling in the lower extremities.

In older adults, the sympathetic nervous system may not respond as quickly to correct orthostatic hypotension, particularly when potentiated by antihypertensive drugs. The drug dose may need to be decreased or another drug used.

Lifestyle modifications for older adults with hypertension:

  • Restrict dietary sodium to 2400 mg daily.
  • Avoid tobacco.
  • Modify diet (e.g., DASH diet).
  • Engage in exercise.
  • Decrease stress.

Recap: HTN in Older Adults

  • Very common: 65% of those >60 have HTN (2 in 3).
  • Therapy reduces stroke (34%) and CHD (19%).
  • Key concern: Orthostatic hypotension (due to slower sympathetic response).
  • Lifestyle modifications are vital: low sodium, no tobacco, diet, exercise, stress reduction.

6. Nonpharmacologic Control of Hypertension: First Steps

Significant reductions in blood pressure can often be achieved through nonpharmacologic methods. These methods should be the first line of approach, especially for mild hypertension.

However, if systolic BP is greater than 140 mm Hg, antihypertensive drugs are generally ordered in combination with these nonpharmacologic strategies.

Key Nonpharmacologic Strategies:

  • Stress-reduction techniques.
  • Regular exercise.
  • Salt restriction.
  • Decreased alcohol ingestion.
  • Smoking cessation.

Recap: Nonpharmacologic HTN Control

  • First approach for mild HTN.
  • Always combine with drugs if SBP > 140 mm Hg.
  • Strategies: Stress reduction, exercise, salt restriction, less alcohol, no smoking.

7. Pharmacologic Control of Hypertension: The Drug Arsenal

Pharmacologic control of hypertension involves an individualized approach by healthcare providers, emphasizing the lowest effective doses and often using combination therapy to minimize adverse effects.

Antihypertensive Drug Categories:

Used singly or in combination, these drugs fall into seven main categories:

  1. Diuretics
  2. Sympatholytics (Sympathetic Depressants)
  3. Direct-Acting Arteriolar Vasodilators
  4. Angiotensin-Converting Enzyme (ACE) Inhibitors
  5. Angiotensin II–Receptor Blockers (ARBs)
  6. Direct Renin Inhibitors
  7. Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs)

Recap: Pharmacologic HTN Control

  • Individualized approach, low effective doses.
  • Often combination therapy.
  • 7 main categories: Diuretics, Sympatholytics, Direct Vasodilators, ACEIs, ARBs, Direct Renin Inhibitors, CCBs.

8. Diuretics: First-Line for Mild Hypertension

Diuretics are effective as first-line drugs for treating mild hypertension because they promote sodium depletion, which decreases extracellular fluid volume (ECFV).

  • Hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ): Most frequently prescribed diuretic for mild hypertension. Can be used alone or with other antihypertensive drugs.
  • Many antihypertensive drugs can cause fluid retention, so diuretics are often co-administered.

Thiazide diuretics are NOT recommended for patients with renal insufficiency (creatinine clearance <30 mL/min). In such cases, loop (high-ceiling) diuretics like furosemide are preferred as they do not depress renal blood flow.

Loop diuretics are generally not used if hypertension is due to RAAS involvement, as they can immediately elevate serum renin levels.

Combinations of potassium-wasting (e.g., thiazides) and potassium-sparing diuretics can minimize potassium loss while enhancing the diuretic effect.

ACE inhibitors, when combined with a thiazide diuretic, help minimize serum potassium loss because ACE inhibitors tend to increase serum potassium levels.

Combinations of Thiazides with Other Antihypertensive Drugs:

  • Thiazide With Potassium-Sparing Diuretics: Hydrochlorothiazide with spironolactone, amiloride, or triamterene.
  • Thiazide With Beta Blockers: Hydrochlorothiazide with bisoprolol fumarate, metoprolol, or bendroflumethiazide with nadolol. (e.g., Tenoretic = Chlorthalidone with atenolol)
  • Thiazide With Angiotensin-Converting Enzyme Inhibitors: Hydrochlorothiazide with benazepril, captopril, enalapril maleate, fosinopril, lisinopril, moexipril, or quinapril.
  • Thiazide With Angiotensin II Antagonists (ARBs): Hydrochlorothiazide with candesartan, eprosartan, irbesartan, losartan, olmesartan, telmisartan, or valsartan.
  • Thiazide With Centrally Acting Alpha2 Agonist: Chlorthalidone with clonidine, or Hydrochlorothiazide with methyldopa.

Recap: Diuretics for HTN

  • First-line for mild HTN (e.g., HCTZ).
  • Promote Na+ depletion, decrease ECFV.
  • Thiazides: Not for renal insufficiency.
  • Loop diuretics: Preferred for renal insufficiency, but not RAAS HTN.
  • Often combined with other antihypertensives to reduce fluid retention and balance K+ levels.

9. Sympatholytics (Sympathetic Depressants): Quieting the System

Sympatholytics reduce sympathetic nervous system activity, leading to lower blood pressure. There are five groups:

  1. Beta-Adrenergic Blockers
  2. Centrally Acting Alpha2 Agonists
  3. Alpha-Adrenergic Blockers
  4. Adrenergic Neuron Blockers (Peripherally Acting)
  5. Alpha1- and Beta1-Adrenergic Blockers

Beta-Adrenergic Blockers (Beta Blockers)

Beta blockers reduce cardiac output by diminishing the sympathetic nervous system response, leading to decreased heart rate, contractility, and renin release. This lowers BP.

African American hypertensive patients typically do NOT respond well to beta blockers alone. Combination with diuretics is often needed for effective BP control in this population.

Types of Beta Blockers:

  • Nonselective Beta Blockers (e.g., Propranolol, Carvedilol): Inhibit beta1 (heart) and beta2 (bronchial) receptors. Slows HR, decreases BP, but can cause bronchoconstriction. Should not be given to patients with COPD.
  • Cardioselective Beta Blockers (e.g., Acebutolol, Atenolol, Metoprolol): Primarily act on beta1 receptors, decreasing HR and BP with less risk of bronchoconstriction. Preferred.

Critical Warning!

Beta blockers should NOT be abruptly discontinued! Tapering the dose over 1-2 weeks is crucial to prevent rebound hypertension, angina, dysrhythmias, and MI.

Centrally Acting Alpha2 Agonists

These drugs (e.g., Clonidine, Methyldopa) decrease sympathetic activity from the brainstem to peripheral vessels. They reduce peripheral vascular resistance and cause vasodilation by:

  • Stimulating alpha2 receptors.
  • Decreasing cardiac output.
  • Decreasing serum epinephrine, norepinephrine, and renin release.

Beta blockers are NOT given with centrally acting sympatholytics due to increased bradycardia and rebound hypertension upon discontinuation.

Clonidine is available in a transdermal patch (7-day duration). Methyldopa is often used for chronic or pregnancy-induced hypertension, but crosses the placental barrier.

  • Drowsiness, dry mouth, dizziness, slow heart rate (bradycardia).
  • Can cause sodium and water retention (peripheral edema); often given with diuretics.
  • Methyldopa: Should not be used in impaired liver function; monitor liver enzymes.

Rebound Hypertension Risk!

This group of drugs must NOT be abruptly discontinued! A severe rebound hypertensive crisis (restlessness, tachycardia, tremors, headache, increased BP) can result. Guanfacine has a lower risk of this rebound effect.

Herbal Interactions with Antihypertensives!

  • Ma-huang (Ephedra): Decreases/counteracts antihypertensive effects; increases HTN with beta blockers.
  • Black cohosh, Goldenseal, Coleus: May increase hypotensive effect.
  • Hawthorn: May increase beta blocker and ACEI effects.
  • Licorice, Korean ginseng, Milk thistle: Decrease antihypertensive effects.
  • Cayenne: May cause cough with ACEIs.
  • Chinese skullcap: May increase losartan levels.
  • St. John’s wort: Decreases CCB effects (nifedipine, verapamil).

Recap: Sympatholytics

  • Reduce sympathetic activity.
  • Beta Blockers: Reduce HR, contractility, renin release. Less effective alone in African Americans. DON'T STOP ABRUPTLY (rebound HTN!). Cardioselective preferred.
  • Centrally Acting Alpha2 Agonists (Clonidine, Methyldopa): Decrease sympathetic outflow, cardiac output. Cause drowsiness, dry mouth, fluid retention. DON'T STOP ABRUPTLY (rebound HTN!).
  • Many herbal interactions.

10. Clinical Judgment: Nursing Process - Antihypertensives: Beta Blockers

Our core concept here is: Perfusion.

Recognize Cues [Assessment]: What do you notice?

  • Medication and Herbal History: Identify all current drugs and supplements. Report potential drug-drug or drug-herbal interactions.
  • Vital Signs: Obtain baseline BP and pulse. Report abnormal findings and compare with baseline (especially bradycardia).
  • Laboratory Values: Periodically check renal and liver function (BUN, serum creatinine, AST, LDH).

Analyze Cues and Prioritize Hypothesis [Patient Problems]: What's the biggest issue?

  • Hypertension
  • Fatigue
  • Bradycardia
  • Risk for rebound hypertension
  • Sexual dysfunction

Generate Solutions [Planning]: What's the goal?

  • The patient’s BP will be 120/80 or below.
  • The patient will take medication as prescribed.

Take Action [Nursing Interventions]: What do YOU do?

  • Monitor Vital Signs: Especially BP and pulse.
  • Monitor Laboratory Results: Especially BUN, serum creatinine, AST, and LDH.

Patient Teaching: Empowering Your Patient!

General:

  • Compliance: Encourage strict adherence to the drug regimen. Advise that abrupt discontinuation may cause rebound hypertension.
  • Herbals: Inform patients that herbs can interfere with beta blockers.
  • OTC Drugs: Advise avoiding OTC drugs (cold, cough, allergy meds) without consulting provider, as many carry warnings for hypertensive patients.
  • Safety ID: Suggest wearing a MedicAlert bracelet or carrying a card.
  • Trauma Situations: Teach patients to inform healthcare providers about beta blocker use in trauma, as these drugs block compensatory effects to shock (Glucagon may be needed to reverse effects).

Self-Administration:

  • Teach patients or family how to take radial pulse and BP, and to report abnormal findings.

Side Effects:

  • Orthostatic Hypotension: Advise that dizziness may occur; warn to rise slowly from lying/sitting to standing.
  • Report Symptoms: Advise reporting dizziness, slow pulse rate, BP changes, heart palpitations, confusion, or GI upset.
  • Diabetes Mellitus: Alert patients with diabetes to possible hypoglycemic symptoms (beta blockers can mask these).
  • Sexual Dysfunction: Inform male patients that impotence may occur (report to provider).

Diet:

  • Nonpharmacologic Methods: Teach nonpharmacologic methods to decrease BP (low-salt diet, relaxation, exercise, smoking cessation, decreased alcohol).
  • Constipation: Advise reporting constipation. Suggest high-fiber foods, stool softener, increased water (unless contraindicated in HF).

Evaluate Outcomes [Evaluation]: Did it work?

  • Evaluate effectiveness (decreased BP, absence of side effects).
  • Determine patient adherence to the drug regimen.

  • Problems: HTN, fatigue, bradycardia, rebound HTN risk, sexual dysfunction.

  • Intervene: Monitor VS/labs.

11. Alpha-Adrenergic Blockers: Vasodilation & Lipid Benefits

Alpha-adrenergic blockers (alpha blockers) block alpha-adrenergic receptors, leading to vasodilation and decreased BP. They help maintain renal blood flow.

Key Benefits:

  • Useful for hypertension in patients with lipid abnormalities: Decrease VLDL and LDL, increase HDL levels.
  • Safe for patients with diabetes as they do not affect glucose metabolism.
  • Do not affect respiratory function.

Nonselective alpha-adrenergic blockers (e.g., phenoxybenzamine, phentolamine) should NOT be given to patients with coronary artery disease due to stimulating effects and increased myocardial oxygen demand.

Selective Alpha1-Adrenergic Blockers:

  • Prazosin, Terazosin, Doxazosin.
  • Used to reduce BP and can treat benign prostatic hypertrophy (BPH).
  • Prazosin is common. Terazosin and doxazosin have longer half-lives (once daily at bedtime).

These drugs, like centrally acting alpha2 agonists, can cause sodium and water retention leading to edema. Diuretics are frequently given concomitantly.

Adrenergic Neuron Blockers (Peripherally Acting Sympatholytics)

These potent drugs (e.g., Reserpine) block norepinephrine release from sympathetic nerve endings, decreasing both cardiac output and peripheral vascular resistance, thus lowering BP. They are considered the last choices for chronic hypertension due to significant side effects.

  • Common side effect: Orthostatic hypotension (rise slowly!).
  • Reserpine can cause pseudoparkinsonism, nightmares, and suicidal ideation.
  • Can cause sodium and water retention; may be taken with a diuretic.

Alpha1- and Beta1-Adrenergic Blockers

This group (e.g., Labetalol) blocks both alpha1 and beta1 receptors.

  • Alpha1 blockade: Causes vasodilation, decreasing peripheral resistance and BP. (Stronger effect on alpha than beta).
  • Beta1 blockade: Decreases heart rate and AV contractility.
  • Large doses can block beta2 receptors, increasing airway resistance; use with caution in severe asthma.
  • Orthostatic hypotension, dizziness, paresthesia.
  • Nasal congestion, nausea, fatigue.
  • Large doses of labetalol may cause AV heart block.

Recap: Alpha Blockers & Other Sympatholytics

  • Alpha Blockers: Vasodilate, decrease BP. Good for lipid abnormalities (decrease VLDL/LDL, increase HDL). Don't affect glucose/respiratory function. Watch for first-dose orthostatic hypotension.
  • Adrenergic Neuron Blockers (Reserpine): Block NE release. Last resort for HTN. Severe orthostatic hypotension, pseudoparkinsonism, suicidal ideation.
  • Alpha/Beta Blockers (Labetalol): Block both alpha1 (vasodilation) and beta1 (decreased HR/contractility). Caution in severe asthma.
  • All Sympatholytics have risk for orthostatic hypotension and may cause fluid retention.

  • Salt Intake: Encourage decreased salt intake, unless otherwise indicated.

  • Evaluate drug effectiveness (controlled BP, absent side effects).
  • Evaluate patient adherence to medication schedule.
  • Evaluate patient knowledge of medication.

13. Direct-Acting Arteriolar Vasodilators: Opening the Vessels

Direct-Acting Arteriolar VasodilatorsPotent antihypertensive drugs that relax the smooth muscles of the blood vessels, primarily arteries, causing vasodilation. Used for moderate to severe hypertension and hypertensive emergencies. are potent antihypertensive drugs that relax the smooth muscles of blood vessels, mainly arteries, causing vasodilation.

Key Effects:

  • Increase blood flow to the brain and kidneys.
  • Decrease BP.
  • Can cause sodium and water retention (peripheral edema); often given with diuretics.
  • Cause less orthostatic hypotension compared to other classes due to minimal dilation of arterioles.

Examples: Hydralazine and Minoxidil (for moderate to severe hypertension). Nitroprusside (for acute hypertensive emergencies).

Reflex tachycardia and renin release can occur due to vasodilation and decreased BP. Beta blockers are often prescribed concurrently to decrease heart rate and counteract reflex tachycardia.

  • Hydralazine: Reflex tachycardia, palpitations, edema, nasal congestion, headache, dizziness, lupus-like symptoms, neurologic symptoms (tingling, numbness).
  • Minoxidil: Similar side effects, plus excess hair growth, can precipitate an anginal attack.
  • Nitroprusside: Reflex tachycardia, palpitations, restlessness, agitation, nausea, confusion.

Recap: Direct Vasodilators

  • Relax arterial smooth muscle, cause vasodilation.
  • Increase blood flow to brain/kidneys, decrease BP.
  • Cause fluid retention (give with diuretics).
  • Less orthostatic hypotension.
  • Cause reflex tachycardia (give with beta blockers).
  • Examples: Hydralazine, Minoxidil, Nitroprusside (emergency).

14. Angiotensin-Converting Enzyme (ACE) Inhibitors: Blocking the RAAS

ACE InhibitorsDrugs that inhibit the angiotensin-converting enzyme, which prevents the formation of angiotensin II (a potent vasoconstrictor) and blocks the release of aldosterone. This leads to vasodilation and decreased fluid retention. inhibit ACE, preventing the formation of angiotensin II (a potent vasoconstrictor) and blocking aldosterone release (which normally promotes sodium retention and potassium excretion). This leads to:

  • Sodium excretion with water.
  • Potassium retention.
  • Little change in cardiac output or heart rate.
  • Lowered peripheral resistance.

ACE inhibitors are used primarily for hypertension and heart failure. They are first-line antihypertensive therapy, though JNC 8 recommends thiazide diuretics first.

Examples: Benazepril, Captopril, Enalapril, Lisinopril, Ramipril, etc.

Contraindications!

  • Pregnancy: Do NOT give during pregnancy (can harm fetus by reducing placental blood flow).
  • Potassium-Sparing Diuretics/Salt Substitutes: Do NOT take with these (risk of severe hyperkalemia).

African Americans and older adults may not respond as well to ACE inhibitors alone; often require a diuretic for effective BP reduction.

  • Primary side effect: A constant, irritated, nonproductive cough (may resolve upon discontinuation; ARB may be substituted).
  • Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, headache, dizziness, fatigue, insomnia, tachycardia.
  • Hyperkalemia (serum potassium excess).
  • Major adverse effect: First-dose hypotension (more common in patients taking diuretics).
  • Angioedema (swelling of face, tongue, lips, larynx, extremities): Rare but serious hypersensitivity reaction, higher incidence in African Americans. Requires immediate discontinuation and sometimes epinephrine.

Recap: ACE Inhibitors

  • Block Angiotensin II formation & Aldosterone release.
  • Result: Na+/H2O excretion, K+ retention, vasodilation, decreased peripheral resistance.
  • Uses: HTN, HF.
  • Side effects: Persistent cough, hyperkalemia, first-dose hypotension, angioedema (rare, serious, higher in African Americans).
  • Contraindicated in pregnancy, and with K+-sparing diuretics/K+ salt substitutes.
  • Less effective alone in African Americans/older adults (combine with diuretic).

15. Clinical Judgment: Nursing Process - Antihypertensives: ACE Inhibitors

Our core concept here is: Perfusion.

Recognize Cues [Assessment]: What do you notice?

  • Drug and Herbal History: Obtain a complete history. Report potential interactions.
  • Baseline Vital Signs: Obtain for future comparisons.
  • Laboratory Values: Check serum protein, albumin, BUN, creatinine, potassium, and WBC count for baseline.

Analyze Cues and Prioritize Hypothesis [Patient Problems]: What's the biggest issue?

  • Hypertension
  • Anxiety
  • Fatigue
  • Risk for hyperkalemia
  • Risk for first-dose hypotension
  • Risk for angioedema

Generate Outcomes [Planning]: What's the goal?

  • Patient’s blood pressure (BP) will decrease to 120/80 or below.
  • Patient will rise to an upright position slowly to avoid orthostatic hypotension.

Take Action [Nursing Interventions]: What do YOU do?

  • Monitor BP: Report a sudden drop in BP.
  • Monitor Laboratory Tests: Renal function (BUN, creatinine, protein), blood glucose levels. Caution: Watch for hypoglycemic reactions in diabetic patients (ACEIs can potentiate insulin).
  • Bleeding Signs: Report any bruising, petechiae, or bleeding (may indicate severe adverse reaction like thrombocytopenia, especially with captopril).

Patient Teaching: Empowering Your Patient!

General:

  • Discontinuation: Warn patients NOT to abruptly discontinue (rebound hypertension possible).
  • OTC Drugs: Inform patients not to take OTC drugs (e.g., cold medications) without consulting a provider.
  • Salt Substitutes: Advise patients NOT to use salt substitutes containing potassium (risk of hyperkalemia).
  • Pregnancy: Warn pregnant patients or those contemplating pregnancy NOT to take ACE inhibitors or ARBs (harm to fetus).
  • Orthostatic Hypotension: Teach patients to rise slowly.

Self-Administration:

  • Teach patients/family how to take and record BP. Establish a chart and report changes.

Side Effects:

  • Dizziness/Lightheadedness: Explain it may occur during the first week of therapy; notify provider if it persists.
  • Monitor for: Angioedema (swelling of face/lips/tongue), cough, dysgeusia (taste disturbance), weakness, hyperkalemia, orthostatic hypotension, renal impairment.
  • Bleeding: Advise patients to report any occurrence of bleeding.

Diet:

  • Captopril Timing: Teach to take captopril 20 minutes to 1 hour before a meal (food decreases absorption).
  • Taste Disturbance: Warn that taste of food may be diminished during first month of therapy.
  • High Potassium Foods: Advise patients to avoid foods high in potassium due to hyperkalemia risk.

Evaluate Outcomes [Evaluation]: Did it work?

  • Evaluate effectiveness (absence of severe side effects, BP returns to desired range).
  • Evaluate patient’s renal function.

Recap: NP - ACE Inhibitors

  • Assess: Drug/herb history, baseline VS, labs (protein, albumin, BUN, creatinine, K+, WBC).
  • Problems: HTN, anxiety, fatigue, hyperkalemia risk, hypotension risk, angioedema risk.
  • Goals: BP ≤120/80, slow position changes.
  • Intervene: Monitor BP, renal labs, bleeding signs.
  • Teach: No abrupt stop, avoid OTCs/K+ salt substitutes. Pregnancy warning. Rise slowly. Self-monitor BP. Report dizziness/angioedema/cough/bleeding. Captopril on empty stomach. Taste changes. Avoid high K+ foods.
  • Evaluate: BP control, side effect absence, renal function.

16. Angiotensin II–Receptor Blockers (ARBs): Alternative to ACEIs

Angiotensin II–Receptor Blockers (ARBs)Drugs that block angiotensin II from binding to angiotensin I (AT1) receptors in tissues, causing vasodilation and decreased peripheral resistance. Used for hypertension and heart failure, particularly in patients who cannot tolerate ACE inhibitors. are similar to ACE inhibitors in that they prevent aldosterone release and act on the RAAS. However, ARBs directly block angiotensin II from binding to angiotensin I (AT1) receptors in many tissues.

Key Differences from ACEIs:

  • Cause vasodilation and decrease peripheral resistance.
  • DO NOT cause the constant, irritated cough often seen with ACE inhibitors.

Examples: Losartan, Valsartan, Irbesartan, Candesartan, Olmesartan, etc.

Like ACE inhibitors, ARBs should NOT be taken during pregnancy. They are also less effective for treating hypertension in African American patients unless combined with a diuretic.

ARBs may be used as a first-line treatment for hypertension. They can be taken with or without food and are suitable for patients with mild hepatic insufficiency.

17. Direct Renin Inhibitors: A New Approach

The first FDA-approved direct renin inhibitor for treating hypertension is Aliskiren. It works by directly binding with renin, which leads to a reduction of angiotensin I, angiotensin II, and aldosterone levels.

  • Effective for mild and moderate hypertension.
  • Can be used alone or with other antihypertensive agents.
  • Has an additive effect when combined with a thiazide diuretic or an ARB.

When used as monotherapy, Aliskiren has not proven to be as effective in reducing BP in the African American population.

  • Dizziness, hypotension, hyperkalemia.
  • Peripheral edema, diarrhea, hyperuricemia, gout, pharyngitis, cough.

Recap: Direct Renin Inhibitors

  • Aliskiren: Directly binds renin, reduces Angiotensin I/II/Aldosterone.
  • Uses: Mild/moderate HTN (alone or combo).
  • Less effective alone in African Americans.
  • Side effects: Dizziness, hypotension, hyperkalemia.

18. Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs): Inhibiting Calcium Influx

Slow calcium channels are found in the myocardium and vascular smooth muscle (VSM) cells. Free calcium increases muscle contractility, peripheral resistance, and BP.

Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs)Also called calcium antagonists. These drugs block calcium channels in vascular smooth muscle, promoting vasodilation and decreasing myocardial contractility. Used for hypertension, angina, and dysrhythmias. block the calcium channels in VSM, promoting vasodilation.

Key Effects:

  • Promote vasodilation (especially in coronary and cerebral arteries and peripheral vessels).
  • Decrease myocardial contractility.
  • Lower peripheral resistance and BP.

CCBs are highly protein bound but often have short half-lives (slow-release preparations decrease administration frequency).

Groups of CCBs:

  • Phenylalkylamines (e.g., Verapamil): Act on arterioles and heart. Used for chronic HTN, angina, cardiac dysrhythmias.
  • Benzothiazepines (e.g., Diltiazem): Act on arterioles and heart. Used for HTN, angina, cardiac dysrhythmias.
  • Dihydropyridines (e.g., Amlodipine, Nifedipine): Largest group, primarily for hypertension.

Nifedipine Warning!

Immediate-release Nifedipine (10- and 20-mg capsules) is associated with increased incidence of profound hypotension, MI, and death, especially in older adults. ONLY extended-release preparations are recommended for chronic hypertension. Immediate-release Nifedipine is typically reserved for acute BP rises in the hospital setting, as needed.

CCBs lower BP better in African Americans than do drugs in other categories.

CCBs can cause reflex tachycardia, especially nifedipine. Beta blockers are generally not prescribed with CCBs because both decrease myocardial contractility, leading to excessive bradycardia or AV block.

Rationale for Critical Thinking Case Study

Question 1: Why was hydrochlorothiazide prescribed for this patient? Explain the effects of hydrochlorothiazide on blood pressure.

Hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ) is a thiazide diuretic, which is often a first-line drug for treating mild to moderate hypertension. It was likely prescribed for this patient to help lower her elevated blood pressure (176/94) and manage her heart failure by reducing fluid volume, which would also help with any associated edema. HCTZ works by acting on the renal distal tubules, promoting the excretion of sodium, chloride, and water. This leads to a decrease in vascular fluid volume and a concomitant decrease in cardiac output and blood pressure. It also acts on arterioles, causing vasodilation, which directly lowers blood pressure.

Question 2: Abnormal electrolytes and other laboratory test results may occur when taking hydrochlorothiazide. Would the following serum electrolyte and laboratory values be expected to increase or decrease?
  • a. Sodium: **Decrease** (Thiazides promote sodium excretion.)
  • b. Potassium: **Decrease** (Thiazides are potassium-wasting diuretics.)
  • c. Calcium: **Increase** (Thiazides promote calcium reabsorption.)
  • d. Magnesium: **Decrease** (Thiazides cause magnesium loss.)
  • e. Glucose: **Increase** (Thiazides can cause hyperglycemia.)
  • f. Uric acid: **Increase** (Thiazides can block uric acid excretion, leading to hyperuricemia.)
Question 3: Why should this patient’s blood glucose level be monitored while she is taking hydrochlorothiazide?

The patient has diabetes. Hydrochlorothiazide (a thiazide diuretic) can affect glucose tolerance and may cause hyperglycemia (elevated blood glucose levels). This is especially true in patients who are already diabetic or prediabetic. Therefore, regular monitoring of her blood glucose is essential to ensure her diabetes remains controlled and to adjust her antidiabetic medication if necessary.

Question 4: What response does a health care provider need to keep in mind when prescribing antihypertensive agents (specifically for African American patients)? Explain your answer.

When prescribing antihypertensive agents for African American patients, a healthcare provider needs to be aware that certain drug classes, specifically beta-adrenergic blockers (beta blockers) and Angiotensin-Converting Enzyme (ACE) inhibitors, are generally less effective for hypertension control in this population when used as monotherapy. African American patients are more susceptible to "low-renin hypertension," which means these drugs, which primarily work by impacting the renin-angiotensin system, may not be as efficacious. Instead, African American patients tend to respond well to alpha1 blockers, calcium channel blockers (CCBs), and diuretics as initial monotherapy or in combination with other agents.

Question 5: Atenolol is what type of antihypertensive? Would atenolol be effective in lowering this patient’s blood pressure if given as the only antihypertensive drug? Explain your answer.

Atenolol is a **cardioselective beta1 blocker**. It works by blocking beta1-adrenergic receptors, primarily in the heart, leading to a decrease in heart rate, myocardial contractility, and blood pressure. However, given that this patient is African American, atenolol would likely **NOT be effective in lowering her blood pressure if given as the only antihypertensive drug.** As discussed in Cultural Responses, beta blockers are generally less effective as monotherapy for African American patients due to their predisposition to low-renin hypertension. Atenolol would typically need to be combined with a diuretic for optimal BP control in this patient.

Question 6: How effective is the combination of hydrochlorothiazide and atenolol for controlling this patient’s blood pressure? Explain your answer.

The combination of hydrochlorothiazide (a thiazide diuretic) and atenolol (a beta blocker) is generally **more effective** for controlling blood pressure in an African American patient than atenolol alone. Hydrochlorothiazide, as a diuretic, is a first-line agent and is effective in African American patients. When combined with a beta blocker like atenolol, the diuretic helps to reduce fluid volume and addresses the responsiveness issues of beta blockers in low-renin hypertension common in this population. This combination can lead to a more significant reduction in blood pressure than either drug used as monotherapy in this specific patient.

Question 7: When using a combination drug therapy to correct hypertension, would the dosage for each drug be the same? Explain your answer.

No, when using combination drug therapy, the dosage for each drug would **not necessarily be the same**. Dosages are typically individualized based on the patient's specific needs, response to the medications, presence of comorbidities (like heart failure and diabetes in this case), and potential side effects. The goal is to use the lowest effective dose of each component to achieve target blood pressure while minimizing adverse effects. Often, combination products come with fixed low doses of each component, but individual titration of each drug may be done before or during the use of a fixed-dose combination.

Question 8: When abruptly discontinuing beta blockers for hypertension without the patient taking another antihypertensive, what might occur? Explain how adverse effects can be avoided.

When beta blockers are abruptly discontinued for hypertension, especially without another antihypertensive in place, the patient can experience a **rebound hypertensive crisis**. This is due to the sudden removal of beta blockade, leading to an exaggerated sympathetic nervous system response. Symptoms can include severe hypertension, tachycardia, angina, dysrhythmias, and even myocardial infarction. These adverse effects can be avoided by **tapering the beta blocker dose down gradually** over a specified period (e.g., 1 to 2 weeks), allowing the body's cardiovascular system to adjust. If the beta blocker must be stopped immediately, another appropriate antihypertensive drug should be prescribed concurrently to mitigate the rebound effect.

Question 9: Why were this patient’s hydrochlorothiazide and atenolol discontinued? Explain your answer.

The patient's hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ) and atenolol were likely discontinued for several reasons based on her new presentation:

  • Elevated Blood Glucose (229): HCTZ (a thiazide diuretic) is known to cause hyperglycemia, which would worsen her pre-existing diabetes. Atenolol (a beta blocker) can also mask symptoms of hypoglycemia and may worsen glucose control in some diabetic patients.
  • Elevated Cholesterol and LDL: Thiazide diuretics (HCTZ) can cause hyperlipidemia (elevated cholesterol, LDL, and triglycerides). Beta blockers can also have varying effects on lipids, sometimes increasing triglycerides and decreasing HDL.
  • Serum Potassium 3.2 mEq/L (Hypokalemia): HCTZ is a potassium-wasting diuretic, and her low potassium level (normal is 3.5-5.0 mEq/L) indicates that it was contributing to this electrolyte imbalance. This is particularly concerning as hypokalemia can increase the risk of digoxin toxicity (she is on digoxin).
  • Inadequate BP Control: Despite being on these drugs, her BP was still significantly elevated at 176/94, indicating that the current regimen was not sufficiently controlling her hypertension, especially given the racial considerations for beta blockers.

The combination of these side effects and inadequate BP control necessitates a change in therapy.

Question 10: What type of antihypertensive is prazosin? Explain the physiologic action of prazosin for lowering the blood pressure.

Prazosin is a **selective alpha1-adrenergic blocker (alpha blocker)**. Its physiologic action involves blocking the alpha1-adrenergic receptors located on the smooth muscle of peripheral blood vessels (both arterioles and venules). This blockade prevents the vasoconstrictive effects of norepinephrine, leading to relaxation and dilation of these blood vessels. The resulting vasodilation decreases peripheral vascular resistance, which directly lowers blood pressure. Prazosin causes only a slight increase in heart rate, making it a good option for BP control.

Question 11: Does prazosin have an effect on the blood glucose level? What effect could prazosin have on this patient’s abnormal lipid levels? Explain your answer.
  • Blood Glucose: Prazosin, as an alpha-adrenergic blocker, generally **does not affect glucose metabolism**, making it a safer option for diabetic patients compared to thiazides or some beta blockers that can worsen hyperglycemia or mask hypoglycemia.
  • Lipid Levels: Prazosin can have a **beneficial effect on abnormal lipid levels**. Alpha blockers are known to decrease very-low-density lipoprotein (VLDL) and low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol, which are "bad" cholesterols contributing to fatty plaque buildup (atherosclerosis). They also tend to increase high-density lipoprotein (HDL) levels, which is "good" cholesterol. This would be advantageous for a patient with elevated cholesterol and LDL, like this one.
Question 12: Why was hydrochlorothiazide again added to the drug regimen?

Hydrochlorothiazide was likely added back to the drug regimen because the patient developed **edema** in her ankles. While prazosin is effective for hypertension and beneficial for lipids and glucose, it can cause sodium and water retention, leading to peripheral edema. Adding a diuretic like HCTZ helps to counteract this fluid retention and reduce the edema. Given her history of heart failure, managing fluid overload is crucial, and HCTZ would directly address the edema by promoting fluid excretion.

Question 13: Is the daily prazosin dose within the safe therapeutic prescribed range for this patient? Explain your answer. (You may refer to Prototype Drug Chart: Prazosin Hydrochloride.)

The patient is prescribed prazosin 10 mg three times daily (tid), which totals 30 mg/day. According to the Prototype Drug Chart: Prazosin Hydrochloride:

  • Adult (A) maintenance dose: 6–15 mg/d; max: 20 mg/d in divided doses.
  • Older A: PO: Initially 1 mg qd/bid; max: 20 mg/d.

Since the patient is 72 years old, she falls into the "Older Adult" category where the maximum recommended dose is 20 mg/d. Her prescribed dose of 30 mg/day is **above the maximum recommended daily dose for an older adult**. While individual patient needs can vary, this dose is typically outside the safe therapeutic prescribed range for an older adult and increases the risk of side effects like orthostatic hypotension.

Question 14: List the groups of antihypertensive drugs that can cause sodium and water retention.

The groups of antihypertensive drugs that can cause sodium and water retention (leading to peripheral edema) include:

  • **Centrally Acting Alpha2 Agonists** (e.g., clonidine, methyldopa)
  • **Alpha-Adrenergic Blockers** (e.g., prazosin, terazosin, doxazosin)
  • **Adrenergic Neuron Blockers (Peripherally Acting Sympatholytics)** (e.g., reserpine)
  • **Direct-Acting Arteriolar Vasodilators** (e.g., hydralazine, minoxidil)
  • **Calcium Channel Blockers** (e.g., amlodipine, nifedipine – particularly dihydropyridines)

Recap: Case Study Takeaways

  • HCTZ (thiazide diuretic) is common for HTN/HF, but causes K+ wasting, hyperglycemia, hyperlipidemia, and hyperuricemia.
  • Atenolol (beta blocker) is less effective alone in African Americans due to low-renin HTN; combination with diuretics improves efficacy.
  • Beta blockers should be tapered slowly upon discontinuation to avoid rebound hypertension.
  • Prazosin (alpha blocker) is good for HTN with lipid abnormalities, doesn't affect glucose, and can help with BPH.
  • Alpha blockers and direct vasodilators can cause fluid retention, often requiring co-administration with diuretics.
  • Always verify drug dosages against recommended guidelines, especially for older adults, to ensure safety.
